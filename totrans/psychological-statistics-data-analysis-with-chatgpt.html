Chapter 2
Initial Data Analysis (IDA)

Initial Data Analysis (IDA) is a crucial preparatory step conducted before performing statistical analysis.

It enhances the fundamental understanding of the collected dataset and clarifies the information and assumptions necessary for progressing with the analysis.
 

The key tasks in IDA include:

●      Understanding the Data: Identifying the types, structure, range, distribution, and missing values in the dataset.

●      Assessing Data Quality: Detecting issues such as missing values, outliers, and input errors.

●      Verifying Assumptions: Checking whether the data meets assumptions such as normality and independence.

●      Initial Visualization: Exploring trends and patterns through visual representations (e.g., histograms, scatter plots).

●      Computing Summary Statistics: Calculating basic statistical measures such as mean, median, and standard deviation.

●      Planning Data Preprocessing: Preparing the dataset for subsequent analysis by removing unnecessary data, transforming variables, and adjusting scales.

●      Adjusting Analysis Plans: Selecting appropriate analytical methods based on data characteristics and refining the plan accordingly.

Relationship Between Initial Data Analysis (IDA) and Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA)

While IDA is an essential step in verifying and improving data quality (e.g., identifying missing values and outliers, confirming data types, and computing descriptive statistics), Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA) serves a different purpose.
 

EDA focuses on understanding data structures, discovering patterns and relationships, and generating hypotheses.
 

In recent years, the importance of EDA has grown in psychological research. During hypothesis generation and the development of new theories, EDA is particularly useful for uncovering unknown patterns.

For example, in modern psychological studies utilizing big data and multivariate analysis, EDA serves as a powerful tool for gaining new insights.
 

IDA prepares the data for analysis, ensuring that it is in an analyzable state, whereas EDA helps develop a deeper understanding of the data to formulate analytical strategies.
 

Thus, IDA is regarded as the "preparation phase," while EDA represents the "exploration phase," with both processes complementing each other.

Chapter 3
Frequency Analysis

Frequency analysis techniques are used to understand the distribution and patterns of categorical data. The following methods are commonly employed:
 

  1. Chi-Square Test

●      Purpose: Tests the independence between two or more categorical variables.

●      Example: Examining the relationship between age group and effectiveness ratings.

●      Feature: Evaluates effect size using Phi coefficient or Cramér’s V.

  1. Fisher’s Exact Test

●      Purpose: Suitable for 2×2 contingency tables with small frequencies.

●      Example: Analyzing gender preference in binary choice data.

●      Feature: Computes exact probability values for small sample sizes.

  1. Residual Analysis

●      Purpose: Identifies which cells show significant deviations between expected and observed frequencies after a Chi-square test.

●      Example: Detailed analysis of differences in effectiveness ratings across age groups.

  1. McNemar’s Test

●      Purpose: Assesses significance in 2×2 contingency tables with paired data.

●      Example: Comparing choices made by the same participants at two different time points.

●      Feature: Designed for paired datasets.

  1. G-Test (Likelihood Ratio Test)

●      Purpose: An alternative to the Chi-square test, using likelihood ratios.

●      Example: Testing categorical variable fitness.

●      Feature: More flexible than Chi-square analysis.

  1. Proportion Test

●      Purpose: Examines whether the difference in proportions between two groups is statistically significant.

●      Example: Comparing purchasing tendencies between male and female consumers.

●      Feature: Specialized for proportion data analysis.

  1. Exact Multinomial Test

●      Purpose: Used when dealing with small sample sizes across multiple categories.

●      Example: Testing whether a die rolls each number with equal probability.

●      Feature: Provides precise probability calculations for multi-category data.

When analyzing associations among multiple categories, the Chi-square test is a fundamental approach.

If expected frequencies are too small, Fisher’s Exact Test or the Exact Multinomial Test should be used.

Selecting an appropriate analysis method depends on data size and contingency table dimensions.

Correlation Analysis

Correlation analysis is a statistical method used to assess the relationship and strength of association between two or more variables.

Depending on the type of data, different correlation coefficients are used:

  1. Pearson's Product-Moment Correlation Coefficient

●      Measures the linear relationship between continuous variables.

●      Ranges from -1 (perfect negative correlation) to 1 (perfect positive correlation), with 0 indicating no correlation.

●      Example: The correlation between height and weight.

  1. Spearman’s Rank Correlation Coefficient

●      Measures monotonic relationships, suitable for ranked data.

●      Does not assume a normal distribution and is robust against outliers.

●      Example: The correlation between exam rankings and athletic performance rankings.

  1. Kendall’s Rank Correlation Coefficient

●      Measures agreement between ranked data.

●      Suitable for small datasets or ranked data.

●      Example: Ranking consistency in sports team evaluations.

  1. Polychoric Correlation Coefficient

●      Measures correlation when one variable is ordinal and the other is continuous.

●      Assumes the ordinal variable reflects an underlying continuous variable.

  1. Tetrachoric Correlation Coefficient

●      Measures correlation when both variables are binary, assuming they originate from an underlying continuous distribution.

●      Example: The correlation between Yes/No responses.

  1. Point-Biserial Correlation Coefficient

●      Used when one variable is binary and the other is continuous.

●      Example: The correlation between gender (male/female) and body weight.

Testing for Differences in Means

Testing for differences in means is a statistical method used to determine whether there is a significant difference between the mean values of two or more groups.
 

This method is categorized into two major types:

  1. Parametric Tests:

●      t-test: Used for comparing two independent groups (independent t-test) or for comparing two conditions within the same participants (paired t-test).

●      Analysis of Variance (ANOVA): Used for comparing the means of three or more groups.

-       Examples: One-way ANOVA, Two-way ANOVA
 

  1. Non-Parametric Tests:

●      Mann-Whitney U test and Wilcoxon signed-rank test: Used when normality assumptions are violated.

●      Kruskal-Wallis test: Used for comparing three or more groups when the normality assumption does not hold.

Procedure
 

  1. Calculation of Basic Statistics: Compute the mean, standard deviation, and 95% confidence interval for the population mean.

  2. Normality Test: Check whether the data follows a normal distribution (e.g., Shapiro-Wilk test).

  3. Homogeneity of Variance Test: Verify whether the variance between groups is equal (e.g., Levene’s test).

  4. Selection of an Appropriate Test: Based on the results of normality and homogeneity tests, choose either a parametric or non-parametric test.

  5. Effect Size Calculation: If a statistically significant difference is found, assess the magnitude of the effect (e.g., Cohen’s d, η²).

  6. Multiple Comparisons (if necessary): If a significant difference is found in a test involving three or more groups, conduct post-hoc comparisons.

Analysis of Variance

Analysis of Variance (ANOVA) is a statistical method used to evaluate whether there are statistically significant differences in means among multiple groups.

It is employed to examine the effect of factors (independent variables) on a dependent variable and to investigate interactions between multiple factors.

ANOVA determines whether observed differences are due to chance by comparing "between-group variance" and "within-group variance."

Types of ANOVA

  1. One-Way ANOVA

●      Examines the effect of a single independent variable (factor) on a dependent variable.

●      Example: Comparing test scores among three classes using different textbooks.

  1. Two-Way ANOVA

●      Analyzes the effects of two independent variables on a dependent variable and their interaction.

●      Example: Examining the effects of gender (male/female) and age group (young/middle-aged/elderly) on physical performance.

  1. Factorial ANOVA (Multifactor ANOVA)

●      An extension of Two-Way ANOVA that examines the effects of more than two independent variables.

●      Example: Investigating the effects of occupation (student/employee/freelancer), gender (male/female), and age group (young/middle-aged/elderly) on stress levels.

  1. Repeated Measures ANOVA

●      Analyzes differences when the same subjects experience multiple conditions.

●      Example: Measuring blood pressure changes in the same individuals taking different medications.

  1. Mixed ANOVA

●      A design that includes both between-subject and within-subject factors.

●      Example: Evaluating the effects of different learning methods over time across groups.

  1. Analysis of Covariance (ANCOVA)

●      A variation of ANOVA that includes covariates (continuous control variables) to adjust for extraneous influences.

●      Example: Comparing the effectiveness of different teaching methods while controlling for students' prior test scores.

  1. Multivariate Analysis of Variance (MANOVA)

●      An extension of ANOVA that analyzes multiple dependent variables simultaneously.

●      Example: Examining the impact of different learning strategies on both math and English test scores.

When significant differences are found through ANOVA, multiple comparisons (e.g., Tukey's HSD test) are commonly conducted to identify specific group differences.

In recent years, it has become desirable to calculate effect sizes (e.g., η², partial η²) based on ANOVA results to assess the magnitude of an independent variable's effect on the dependent variable.
