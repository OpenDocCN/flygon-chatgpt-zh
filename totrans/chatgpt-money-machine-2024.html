What Is AI?

Forget about HAL singing scary songs or robots planning the end of the world. The actual revolution brought about by AI is considerably more exciting—and significantly less dangerous. Imagine your smartphone suddenly evolving into a highly intelligent companion, picking up on your habits, predicting your needs, and even making lighthearted comments as you walk. That is AI.

Allow me to explain it in simpler terms before you imagine intelligent machines assuming control (don’t worry, we’ll address it later). At its core, AI is all about programming computers to think and act like humans. Being able to learn, adapt, and even make decisions similar to a human, rather than just linearly processing data (like a computer) or mechanically (like a washing machine).

AI is essentially the study and creation of computer systems with the ability to mimic human intellect. From learning and problem-solving to comprehending natural language and identifying patterns, these tasks cover it all. Essentially, the goal of AI is to push computing to its traditional limits by building machines that can perform cognitive processes.

Alan Turing and John McCarthy, two trailblazers in the field of AI, established themselves in the middle of the 20th century (Pigro, 2021). Initially, AI was all about rule-based systems and symbolic reasoning. But, with the introduction of deep learning and machine learning (ML), the subject has undergone tremendous change, particularly in the last several years. Unlike traditional computers, which need to be explicitly programmed, AI systems learn and change based on data. This lets them do highly complex tasks swiftly.

Think about how much easier it would be if your toaster could detect when bread is becoming too brown, or if your car could adapt to your driving style and handle rush hour with ease. AI offers that kind of magic. It wasn’t invented overnight, though. What would later be known as AI began in the 1950s when a group of bright minds got together to discuss how to program computers to “think” (Pigro, 2021). It was like training a kid to tie its shoes—quite sluggish at the start, but AI has acquired some very remarkable feats over the years. It is now capable of real-time language translation, pinpoint medical diagnosis, and even defeating international chess champions.

Types of AI

AI has been introduced to the world in mainly three different forms. Each one has its own set of capabilities and limitations. Let’s discuss them and see where each one shines and works around us. The three types are narrow, general, and super AI.

Narrow AI

Narrow AI, often called weak AI, is a specialized AI that can only do one or two things well and lacks the general intelligence present in humans. Think of it as an expert who works hard at becoming very good at what it does. Siri, Alexa, and other virtual personal assistants, as well as the recommendation algorithms used by video-sharing websites like YouTube and Netflix, are all examples of narrow AI.

Example: Virtual Personal Assistants

●  Siri: Personal digital assistant developed by Apple that can be asked questions, send messages, and set reminders with voice commands.

●  Alexa: A virtual assistant developed by Amazon that can control smart home gadgets, give you the weather, and play music when you want it to.

Gaining knowledge of narrow AI is important because it is the basis for many everyday uses. Within this area, you’ll find specialized systems for image recognition, language translation, and chatbots.

General AI

One branch of AI, general AI (also called strong AI), seeks to imitate human intelligence in its broadest sense. General AI, in contrast to narrow AI, can comprehend, learn, and apply information across various disciplines; it is not task-specific. Mastering general AI is no easy feat; it requires teaching computers to understand the world in the same way that we humans do.

Example: Human-Like Robots

Sophia, a Hanson Robotics creation, is a humanoid robot with the ability to mimic human speech, facial expressions, and body language.

Recognizing general AI is critical because it stands for the idea of computers possessing a human-like, all-encompassing knowledge of the world. Even if we haven’t seen general AI in action just yet, talking about it raises concerns regarding its possible social effects and ethical ramifications.

Super AI

Superintelligent AI, often called super AI, is a quantum leap beyond anything that humans are currently capable of creating. In the long run, we want computers to be smarter and more efficient than humans in every aspect of thinking and solving problems, not just the ones where people really shine (narrow AI). Super AI is currently in its infancy, and there are many valid ethical questions about how it should be developed.

Example: Speculative and Theoretical

There may not be any super AIs in the actual world just yet, but you’ll see them in science fiction films like Ex Machina and Her.

Due to the potential societal effects and the necessity to give serious consideration to ethical, safety, and governance systems, it is critical to comprehend super AI. It is important to discuss acceptable ways to develop and use AI as it continues to advance. This is particularly the case for those considering solutions driven by AI, such as chatbots. To ensure that AI systems adhere to human goals and ideals, it is helpful to categorize them as either narrow, general, or super AI. This will guide designers and engineers toward more practical and ethical considerations.

Differentiating the Types

Those interested in chatbots and other AI-driven solutions would do well to familiarize themselves with the several forms of AI. The classification of AI into narrow, general, and super AI helps to define the present and future state of the technology. Businesses and developers exploring AI-driven apps, such as chatbots, would do well to familiarize themselves with these kinds to guarantee proper technology selection and deployment.

Chatbots built for certain roles, such as customer support or data retrieval, are well-suited to narrow AI due to their specialized skill, which is perfect for focused jobs. Theoretically, general AI is a source of motivation for developing chatbots with the ability to learn and adapt to new situations, allowing them to provide a more natural user experience. To foresee future developments and possible ethical concerns, it is essential to acknowledge the ambitious goals of super AI.

For chatbots to work at their best and make users happy, it’s important to match the type of AI with its intended usage. With this detailed knowledge, programmers can fully utilize AI’s capabilities, making chatbots that can adapt to new technology while still satisfying users’ needs.

Understanding ML and NLP

Learning the ins and outs of ML and NLP could feel like negotiating a technical maze. Rest assured, I will explain these ideas in a way that is more appropriate for a casual conversation than a formal data science conference.

Demystifying ML

Let’s start with ML. What it boils down to is the science and art of computer learning and decision-making without the need for direct programming. You’re not giving your computer a precise script, but rather facts, and then you’re letting it work things out on its own.

Example: Spam Filter

You might want to think about the spam filter on your email. The filter learns what you consider spam based on your behavior, rather than being taught specifically what is spam. As it learns more and more, it becomes better at preventing spam from reaching your inbox.

Unraveling NLP

Now let’s talk about NLP. It’s what makes computers able to understand, analyze, and make up a language that sounds natural. Imagine it like instructing your computer to understand and use English fluently.

Example: Virtual Assistants

NLP is on display whenever you have a conversation with an AI like Alexa, Tim, or Siri. With the help of these virtual assistants, you may have natural-sounding conversations with them. They can comprehend what you say or write, understand the context, and answer accordingly. It’s about understanding the finer points of language, not merely finding keywords.

IBM Watson for Oncology uses ML to determine the best course of treatment for each individual patient by analyzing their medical history, results from clinical trials, and other literature. This is a perfect illustration of how ML may improve healthcare decision-making.

When it comes to spotting instances of fraud in the banking industry, ML algorithms are like superheroes. They scan all of your financial transactions for unusual trends and flag them before you even realize there’s a problem.

Example: Credit Card Fraud Detection

Banks use ML models that can swiftly identify unusual spending patterns after learning your spending habits. In many cases, the system may raise an alarm to avoid fraudulent transactions if your card is suddenly used for a purchase at an unexpected place or for an unusual amount.

Online Shopping: Tailored Suggestions

How is it that your go-to online retailer always seems to have the perfect item in stock for you? That’s ML in action in the ecommerce realm, providing you with tailored suggestions according to your past actions.

Example: Amazon Recommendations

Your search history, Amazon purchases, and the purchases of other customers with similar tastes are all inputted into the recommendation engine. For a more tailored buying experience, it proposes items you might like.

Temperature and Max Tokens

An ingenious method to circumvent prompt engineering is to fiddle with the temperature and maximum tokens. Changing the “max tokens” option lets you limit the longest answer, and the “temperature” parameter controls how random the output is. By fine-tuning these parameters, you can get the right amount of unpredictability and reaction time.

You can change the temperature measure to control how creative your language model is. Imagine a creativity dial. If the temperature is higher, say 0.8, the model is more likely to try out different word choices and sentence patterns. Example

In your API call, specify the temperature parameter like this (Python example):

response = openai.Completion.create(

engine=“text-davinci-003”,

prompt=“Translate the following English sentence to French: ‘The quick brown fox jumps over the lazy dog.’”,

temperature=0.8,

max_tokens=100

)

Adjust the temperature value to find the sweet spot that aligns with your desired level of creativity.

Configuring Max Tokens

Use the max_tokens parameter to cap the length of the text that is created. It limits the output to a certain number of words or lines. If you set it to a certain number, like changing it to 50 in the above example, the program will always give you a short answer within that range.

Example

In your API call, add the max_tokens parameter:

response = openai.Completion.create(

engine=“text-davinci-003”,

prompt=“Translate the following English sentence to French: ‘The quick brown fox jumps over the lazy dog.’”,

temperature=0.8,

max_tokens=50

)

The max_tokens value lets you change how long or short the generated text should be. Remember that you need to try different things until you find the right mix for your needs. You can change these numbers as much as you want until you get the desired results!
