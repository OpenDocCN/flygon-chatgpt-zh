1. ChatGPT – Your New AI Assistant

 

1.1 Overview

 

Welcome to the future! Dive into the world of ChatGPT, the latest innovation capturing global attention! ChatGPT is not just any AI assistant; it is founded on the groundbreaking technology of Generative AI. This technology harnesses unstructured data (such as texts, images, and sounds) to discern intricate patterns, which it stores as statistical values. When prompted by a user via a simple interface (Figure 1.1), Generative AI leverages these patterns to generate new content, either text, sound, 2D images, or 3D objects. It is a harmonious fusion of art and science, underpinned by advanced algorithms.

Figure 1.1 ChatGPT user interface

ChatGPT stands out as a unique application within the Generative AI domain. It is specifically designed for generating and processing text and is primed for conversational interaction. Such tools are broadly termed Large Language Models, or LLMs for short, and they evolve at an astonishing rate.

This book primarily delves into ChatGPT LLM and the DALL-E image generator, both luminaries in the world of Generative AI. ChatGPT was unveiled by OpenAI in November 2022. Made freely available to the public, its reception was overwhelmingly positive, amassing one hundred million users in merely two months.

What makes Generative AI so transformative? It is the first technology that allows us to delegate tasks requiring human-like intelligence. This ushers in an era where machines not only execute predefined actions but also conceive original ideas, making informed decisions and sparking innovations.

1.2 Architecture

 

To learn human talk patterns, ChatGPT reads a lot of text from books, websites, chats, encyclopedias, and other text resources. It keeps these patterns in a computer brain called a neural network. It is like a sponge soaking up tons of text. This learning stage helps it learn the words, the rules of grammar, and some facts about the world. As it learns how words and sentences are structured, it is able to predict how the sentence will continue. Then it goes through more training where it sees lots of questions and answers back-and-forth. This helps ChatGPT to improve its responses to questions (guessing word by word) in a way that "feels" more human. This part of the training is like having a teacher who gives detailed feedback.

How is this done?

ChatGPT is built on a blueprint called the Transformer Architecture1 (Figure 1.2). This architecture has two main components: an Encoder and a Decoder. The Encoder delves into the text input, analyzing and understanding the meaning of the words. A built-in mechanism, called "Attention"2 helps the Encoder focus on the proper parts of the text and empowers the language model to home in on pertinent parts of the input and to discern the relations between words and phrases:

– The domain of discourse and context of the discussion,

– The position of words in the sentence, and

– The relation of every word to the other words in the sentence.

Figure 1.2 Simplified representation of the interaction with a Language Model

Word position in a sentence is important, like the order of ingredients in a recipe. The relationship between words (weak or strong) is also important as it can change the meaning of the sentence. The Decoder has a record of words, grammar patterns, and authorial style acquired during training. It crafts responses based on these insights, mirroring the language patterns evident in your text input.3

1.3 Capabilities

 

ChatGPT's generative potential extends far beyond simple Q&As, making it a valuable tool for a plethora of creative and innovative tasks.

For writers, it serves as an ideation partner, suggesting story arcs, and character developments, and even crafting poetry or prose snippets.

Bloggers and journalists can leverage it for headline generation, lead paragraph optimization, or even for brainstorming article topics.

Teachers can utilize ChatGPT to create tailored lesson plans, exam questions, or inventive educational scenarios to engage students.

Researchers, on the other hand, can employ it to synthesize abstracts or summaries from dense academic texts.

Business professionals can harness its power for brainstorming sessions, generating product names, and taglines, or even formulating business strategies based on provided data.

Designers and artists are not left out either; with a textual prompt, ChatGPT proposes design concepts, art themes, or even music lyrics.

In coding and development, it aids in suggesting code snippets, debugging solutions, or proposing algorithmic structures.

Moreover, its ability to simulate diverse conversational styles makes it an ideal tool for scriptwriters, enabling drafting dialogues for plays, movies, or video games.

In essence, ChatGPT's generative prowess offers unparalleled versatility, transforming how professionals across sectors ideate, create, and innovate.

But, since language models learn from text and not real events, their knowledge is a mix of real and made-up stuff. They do not know the difference between right and wrong. They might sound like they understand, but they do not. They just give back text that fits the one we provide.

1.4 Limitations

 

We, as humans, have invented a machine that mimics the way we talk. This is huge because text is still the primary form in which human experience, knowledge, and feedback are stored. And now, it seems we finally have access to machines with the ability to analyze, interpret, and generate language.

How cool is that? And how cool is to work with such a machine?

Before we go any further, it is time to discuss ChatGPT's temporary limitations.

Like all LLMs, ChatGPT does not truly "understand," or "reason," it does not really "process" information the way we, as humans, do. We train an LLM with vast amounts of textual data, but we give it no mechanism to distinguish between opinions and scientific facts expressed in those texts. Unlike humans, LLMs do not possess a consistent "worldview" or a definite "source of truth."

We can consider ChatGPT a storybook character, a "Keeper of Knowledge," who have read and now remembers tons of texts. It may suggest the books he have read, now summarizing them, answering questions, and acting like the characters from the books. However, he does not have access to those books anymore and most of the time, when discussing a subject, he combines ideas read in different writings, having difficulties remembering exactly which idea is from what book or even if the idea is an opinion or a real fact. So, we need to verify everything he says.

But the "Keeper of Knowledge" is still useful because he can pull out tiny details from a huge pile of information, finding links that even smart humans might miss. The trick of getting those hidden insights that even human experts might miss is to ask proper questions.

This book is about how to ask those questions, and how to talk to AI.

When OpenAI introduced ChatGPT, its impact was profound. The nuanced, human-like interaction resonated with millions, echoing the dream of interactions like those with Commander Data from "Star Trek: The Next Generation" (Figure 1.3). The fluency of such LLMs might lead one to anticipate capabilities beyond mere language comprehension and generation. We might yearn for genuine reasoning, dynamic learning, and external resource access. However, linguistic prowess does not guarantee accuracy.

Technologies that mimic Commander Data would possess:

– A comprehensive world model, equipped with learning dynamics,

– Capability for intelligent processes, inclusive of causal rules and algorithms, and

– Real-time access to diverse real-world data.

Subsequent mathematical operations produce contextually relevant outputs. LLMs nowadays, including ChatGPT, remain tools rather than sentient entities like Commander Data (Figure 1.4).

All right then. Now we know that ChatGPT, the "Keeper of Knowledge," is a machine, not a human.

What is so appealing in ChatGPT?

Its prowess in illuminating connections across vast data sources is unique. The tool blends human-generated content seamlessly, revealing unexpected parallels between diverse human works. Such AI tools exemplify original social collaboration. And this is the real performance.

2.2 The Art of Prompting

 

With ChatGPT, what you ask for is what you get. The better the prompt the better it responds. The art of prompt crafting is essential.

Of course, there is no perfect prompt because an LLM will always use statistics to learn the patterns and probability to select the words when it answers. (You can test this by entering the same prompt several times. Each time, you will get a slightly different worded response.) It means that the art of prompting is about how to craft the prompts that maximize our chances of getting what we want. The practice of designing better prompts to instruct the model to perform a task is referred to as prompt design. The good news is we can achieve a lot with simple prompts.

Let's take a simple example where we ask ChatGPT to summarize a text.

There are many use cases with simple prompts. Here are a few keywords.

SUMMARIZE

 

To generate a concise and condensed overview of the main points or key elements of a larger piece of text.

REWRITE

 

To rephrase or reword content while retaining its core meaning; to improve clarity, style, or adapt it for a different audience.

Example: Transforming a complex scientific article into a simpler text, without technical jargon, for general audience.

GENERATE

 

To craft content, brainstorming, and/or idea generation.

Example: You are writing a project draft and need to be creative with the usability of the results. You can use ChatGPT as a brainstorming tool to generate ideas.

SEARCH

 

To seek factual answers and clarifications.

Example: You are researching for a school project on climate change and its effects on marine life. You input "impact of climate change on marine life" into ChatGPT and will receive a range of opinions and facts about the topic, things it learned during training.

If ChatGPT is connected to the internet, it presents an updated range of articles, research papers, and studies about this topic.

EXTRACT

 

To retrieve specific information or data from a text.

Example: Pulling out city names from a report.

CLASSIFY

 

To categorize or sort data or information into distinct groups based on given criteria.

Example: Sorting emails into folders like "Work," "Personal," or "Promotions" based on their content.

CLUSTER

 

To group related items together based on shared characteristics or patterns.

Example: You upload a group of customer reviews and ask ChatGPT to cluster reviews that share common sentiments and discuss similar aspects.

CONVERSATION

 

To mimic natural, contextually relevant communication as if it were between two humans.

Example: Chatting with a virtual assistant that responds in a conversational manner, understanding context and providing meaningful answers. Another example is to use ChatGPT as a tutor.

TRANSLATE

 

To convert text from one language to another while preserving its meaning and context.

Example: Using a language translation tool to convert a paragraph from English to Spanish.

SENTIMENT ANALYSIS

 

To determine the emotional tone or sentiment expressed in a text by using natural language processing and computational techniques.

Example: Analyzing social media posts to understand whether they convey positive, negative, or neutral feelings about a product.

2.3 General Tips for Crafting Prompts

 

When engaging with ChatGPT, the way you formulate your prompts greatly influences the accuracy, relevance, and quality of the responses. Here are some essential guidelines:

– Be clear about what you want

Clarity is key to ensuring you receive accurate and on-point answers. Use direct language and avoid ambiguity. Compare the output of the following prompts.

Give me a summary of [Title of the book].

Could you summarize the key themes and character development in [Title of the book]?

– Set up the stage

Context is essential for meaningful conversations. Offer background information to help AI understand the context of your query. For instance:

What are the benefits of exercise?

In the context of overall well-being, could you elaborate on the recommended physical and mental exercises for a 70-year-old person?

– Refine your prompt through iterations.

Experiment with new wording, context, and style to see how AI's responses evolve. Learn from observed patterns to further fine-tune your approach. Do not be afraid to ask multi-step questions. Ask a question and get a response. Based on that response, ask another question. Do it as many times as necessary and you will get wonderful results.

2.4 Preserving Your Conversations

 

ChatGPT is a powerful tool that generates a wealth of content. To make the most of your interactions and keep track of your conversations, the platform provides a convenient feature. After each conversation, ChatGPT saves the entire conversation thread in the History tab. The History tab serves as a repository of your interactions, providing a record of your discussions with AI.

This allows you to revisit and continue conversations at any time. To access them, navigate to the History tab where you will find a list of your conversations in reverse chronological order. Click on any conversation to re-enter the discussion (or delete it) or start a new one whenever you like.

If you wish to use or share a conversation you have had, you can employ the traditional approach of copying the conversation and pasting it into another platform, such as a document, email, or note-taking application.

For more structured and permanent record-keeping, ChatGPT offers the option to download your conversations. This is particularly useful if you want to keep an offline copy of your interactions. To do this, follow these steps:

– Open sidebar History (click on the icon on the left upper corner);

– At the bottom of the History tab, click in the Select Settings/Data control;

– Click Export and follow the instructions.

CONTEXT Component

Just as in having a friendly chat with a colleague, before seeking advice or posing a question it is essential to set the stage and provide context, to establish the conversation's foundation.

By using the CONTEXT instructions, we enable AI to:

– Understand our overall goal

– Decode jargon, abbreviations, or acronyms

– Generate output relevant to us

– Ensure outputs are sensitive to regional and cultural differences.

AI ROLE Component

The next most important thing when we talk to AI is to assign AI a certain role. This empowers it to take on distinct responsibilities, such as teacher, language instructor, or designer.

– which words it should consider for the next part of the text it generates (top_p)

3.3 Context Memory

 

If you have a fairly long conversation with a person, it might happen that the conversation goes off the track. The person you talk to might remember only the last few dozen interactions and completely forget the initial topic.

This also happens if you have a long conversation to ChatGPT.

This limitation is a common aspect of all language models, being known as "Context Memory" or "Context Window" (Figure 3.3).

It happens because a technical limitation allows AI to recall only a limited amount of information. If this limitation is expressed in words, it is called "Word Limit."

A Word Limit of 6,000 indicates that the model considers the past 6,000 words of the conversation when generating a response.

Figure 3.3 Context Window

Another way to express this limitation is to use "tokens," in this case, the limitation being known as "Context Window." Since a "token" describes not only a specific word but also some other contextual elements that the models need to know about that word when processing it, it may have various lengths. So, if we want to convert the technical specification "Context Window" (expressed in tokens) into "Word Limit" (expressed in words), we need to use an average value, each token corresponding to roughly 0.75 words.

Let's try a simple example.

[Word Limit] is expressed in "words"

[Context Window] is expressed in "tokens"

1 token ~ 0.75 words (Average token length)

Given a Context Window of 1,000 tokens, what is the Word Limit?

Word Limit = Context Window / Average Token Length

Word Limit = 1,000 tokens / 0.75 words per token

Word Limit ≈ 1,333 words

The Context Window limitation has implications for the way we use the model. One way to maintain context is to control the output and specify the length of the response. We want to stop AI from being very talkative when we need brief answers. Another way is to control the input and, for example, if we intend to ask AI to summarize a lengthy text, to divide it into manageable chunks.

3.5 Fact-checking AI

 

The most important good practice habit is to check AI output, assessing the accuracy and reliability of the information provided by the model. Here are some practical steps you can employ for cognitive verification:

– Start with general information: Begin by asking the AI model for general information on a topic. This can give you an initial overview and help you gauge the accuracy of its responses in the domain.

– Look for consistency: If the information provided by the AI model sticks with what you already know or have learned from other sources, it adds to the credibility of the response.

– Check dates and context: If the AI model provides information that is time-sensitive, check that it is up-to-date. Additionally, take into consideration the context the information is being presented; sometimes, context matters as much as the information itself.

– Cross-reference multiple sources: After receiving a response from ChatGPT, cross-reference the information with multiple reliable sources. like academic papers, books, or experts in the field. If multiple sources confirm the information, it is more likely to be accurate.

– Question unusual claims: If the AI model provides complex or unusual claims or if they contradict widely accepted knowledge, it is wise to be skeptical.

– Use critical thinking: Apply your own critical thinking skills to assess the plausibility and logic of the information provided. If something seems too good to be true or does not stick with your understanding, delve deeper to verify.

– Evaluate source reliability: AI-generated information might lack proper citation or come from less reliable sources. Be cautious when information is not attributed to well-known sources.

– Verify technical details: If you are dealing with technical details or complex concepts, consult experts in the field to validate the information. They can provide insights that might not be readily apparent to non-experts.

– Seek clarification: If you are not sure about the accuracy of a response, you can ask the AI's model follow-up questions, requesting more detailed explanations or additional context.

Following these steps, you can leverage ChatGPT's generative capabilities while maintaining a high level of accuracy and reliability in the generated information.

4. Prompting Techniques

 

You can ask AI almost anything. One thing makes the difference. What and how you ask is what and how you get. In the early days of computer programming, the expression describing the relation between the quality of the program and what you get was "garbage in, garbage out." The meaning is that the computers' output is limited to their input. Nowadays, it is valid for AI prompting.

Ranking prompting techniques by importance and usability can be subjective and context-dependent. Different approaches may prove more useful depending on the complexity of the task and the desired outcome1. Typically experiencing, users find out which techniques work best for their specific needs.

Below are a few fundamental techniques to improve our interaction with AI. The effectiveness of each of them can vary greatly depending on each situation. The most basic and widely used ones—listed below—are not discussed in detail:

– Natural Language Prompting, simply asking the model a question as you would to a human,

– Iterative Prompting, back-and-forth interactions for incremental clarification,

– Corrective Prompting, providing the model with feedback and asking it to correct itself,

– Analogical Prompting, asking AI to think by analogy. It can be helpful for creative problem-solving and tasks requiring knowledge transfer.

As AI technology evolves, new prompting techniques may emerge, or the existing ones may gain or lose relevance.

4.1 One-Shot Prompting

 

Zero-shot and Few-shot prompts describe the interactions in which we provide the model with no example (Zero-shot) or a few examples (Few-shot) to guide the model on the new task.

One-shot prompting means crafting one concise prompt with clear instructions, context, and specific details. The model generates a response based solely on this one prompt.

The One-shot prompting goal is to craft concise, effective prompts to maximize the information extracted from a single interaction.

One-shot prompting is useful when you have a specific goal in mind, without any need for extended conversations. We have already used it so many times in this book. The results are as good as the prompt, so focus on crafting good prompts.

 

Benefits:

– Obtaining desired information or responses in a single interaction.

– Saving time (One-shot prompting streamlines the interaction).

– Allowing ChatGPT to draw connections across large bodies of text.

By refining prompt composition based on empirical findings, you can enhance the quality and relevance of the model's responses. It is a dynamic strategy that involves a deep understanding of both the language model's capabilities and the art of prompt construction.

 

Challenges:

– Crafting a comprehensive prompt within the constraints of the prompt length can be challenging, especially for complex tasks.

– Balancing the amount of context provided in the prompt while keeping it concise requires practice.

 

Use Cases:

– Quick Information Retrieval: When you need a specific piece of information or a direct answer, One-shot prompting can provide immediate results.

– Simple Queries: When you have straightforward queries or instructions, One-shot prompting eliminates the need for long conversations.

– Specific Tasks: Tasks with clear parameters and well-defined goals can often be solved effectively using One-shot prompting.

– Task Summaries: When you want the model to summarize a topic, a well-crafted one-shot prompt can yield concise and relevant summaries.

 

Empirical Findings

– The wording of instructions significantly influences the quality of the model's response. Choose words that precisely convey your intent, avoiding ambiguity.

– The order of the instructions plays a pivotal role. Experiment with different instruction sequences to understand how they affect the model's interpretation and response.

– Models are sensitive to the context, but overloading them with context may lead to confusion.

– Demonstrations can be helpful, but models sometimes misinterpret demonstrations.

– Reuse the same context for multiple prompts and then average the results. The method can mitigate the effects of context variation on the model's responses.

4.2 Chain-of-Thought Prompting (CoT)

 

Chain-of-thought prompting is an advanced technique for complex problems requiring logical reasoning or step-by-step explanations. The goal is to promote more transparency in AI's thought process.

This technique involves creating a continuous and evolving conversation by chaining a sequence of prompts and responses. Each subsequent prompt is informed by the model's previous response, leading to a coherent and contextually rich conversation.

CoT is a powerful technique that leverages the model's previous responses to create dynamic and evolving conversations. This technique enhances the naturalness and richness of interactions with language models.

The process begins with an initial prompt that sets the context or topic for the conversation. This prompt can be a question, instruction, or statement that initiates the interaction.

The language model generates a reply based on the initial prompt. This reply becomes the foundation for the following prompt in the sequence. The subsequent prompts reference the model response, and the conversation builds upon earlier interaction, ensuring that responses align with the established context.

As the CoT continues, the interaction evolves naturally. The model's replies are shaped by its previous outputs, leading to a dynamic and engaging conversation that feels more human-like.

 

Benefits:

– The conversations flow naturally, mirroring human interactions.

– The technique allows for the gradual development of complex topics, enabling the model to provide nuanced insights.

– CoT is efficient for exploring multifaceted topics or brainstorming ideas.

– Since each prompt references the previous response, there is a reduction in repetitive statements.

 

Challenges:

– While the technique enhances context, the model may occasionally misinterpret the evolving context.

– Over multiple interactions, the length of responses can accumulate and may require limiting the model's verbosity.

– The model might get stuck in a loop, repeatedly referring to the same context.

 

Use Cases:

– To explore a topic from various angles, generating diverse insights.

– (E.g., for writers) to collaboratively brainstorm and develop plotlines, characters, and story arcs.

– To brainstorm solutions to complex problems, iteratively refining ideas.

– To seek creative ideas and innovative suggestions by building on previous model responses.

4.3 Tree-of-Thought Prompting (ToT)

 

The Tree-of-thought prompting is similar to CoT but with a focus on branching paths. Just as a tree branches out in various directions, this approach enables you to guide the conversation down different paths, each stemming from the initial prompt. It involves posing follow-up questions or prompts based on the model's responses, creating a dynamic and multifaceted exchange.

To visualize the technique, imagine a prompt about hobbies. It leads to different branches of the conversation. Each one delves deeper into one of the mentioned hobbies.

ToT is useful especially when discussing complex subjects with multiple possible outcomes or in a decisional process. ToT creates multi-layered conversations that branch into various paths within a single interaction with ChatGPT.

The ToT technique requires good skills in maintaining the coherence and context of the conversation.

 

Benefits:

– ToT allows for in-depth exploration of a topic. Branching into different facets, you can extract insights, ideas, and perspectives.

– This technique leads to more diversity as the model delves into various facets of the topic.

– The branching nature of the conversation keeps the interaction engaging and adaptable, catering to evolving thoughts and questions.

– Through follow-up prompts, you can guide the model to learn progressively about a specific domain or subject matter.

 

Challenges:

– As the conversation branches out, ensuring coherence and relevance across different threads may become increasingly difficult, potentially resulting in fragmented responses.

– Striking a balance between exploring multiple threads and maintaining a coherent flow of conversation can be challenging.

– Models may struggle to seamlessly transition between different branches of thought if the context is not maintained clear.

 

Use Cases:

– Ideal to conduct in-depth interviews with a model, allowing you to follow up various aspects of a person's life, career, or experiences.

– (For brainstorming ideas) to explore different angles and variations using branching prompts to stimulate creativity.

– To dissect complex problems, breaking them into smaller components, analyzing each aspect separately.

4.4 Continuity Prompting

 

Continuity prompting is a vital technique for rich conversations over several sessions as the underlying architecture processes each new session independently without memory of previous sessions. Continuity prompting involves crafting prompts that reference former sessions in conversations. When re-starting the conversation, input the prompt to refresh ChatGPT's memory.

To ensure continuity, users would summarize or reference important details from earlier interactions at the beginning of a new session. The reference can include:

– Summarizing the previous conversation's outcomes

– Listing relevant facts, decisions, or action items

– Highlighting any important unsolved questions or tasks.

By incorporating these elements into the initial prompts of a new session, users effectively "remind" AI the previous context, helping maintain a coherent and relevant dialogue flow. As language models become more sophisticated, they may develop longer-term memory capabilities, which can make continuity prompting less relevant.

 

Benefits:

– Contextual continuity prevents disjointed and fragmented interactions.

– Continuity prompts encourage the model to provide deeper insights and more thoughtful responses.

– The model remembers user preferences, choices, or details from previous prompts.

 

Challenges:

– There is a limit to how much information models can remember. (Remember Context Window)

– When the conversation includes multiple threads, the continuity prompt can be challenging.

– Balancing the inclusion of context with natural language flow can be tricky, as overly repetitive references can disrupt the conversational rhythm.

 

Use Cases:

– Tasks related to learning skills or subjects; maintaining contextual continuity allows building upon prior lessons and discussions, enhancing the learning experience.

– Interactive stories; when creating interactive stories or narratives, continuity ensures that characters and events remain consistent throughout the conversation.

– Multiple-step tasks; maintaining context helps the personal assistants to provide relevant and accurate information.

4.5 Chain-of-Density Prompting (CoD)

 

Chain-of-density prompting is a nuanced prompting technique designed to refine information processed by AI, producing increasingly dense and concise summaries. It is not widely recognized in standard the AI's prompting methodologies, but stands out for its unique approach to enhance content richness without expanding length.

In CoD, AI progressively incorporates more complex and substantial information – 'densifying' the content – while adhering to a strict word limit. This method involves starting with a basic summary and, through iterative prompting, adding significant entities from the source material. The challenge is to act without increasing the overall summary length, compressing more information.

The technique is particularly effective for generating summaries where each iteration demands greater detail and specificity, encouraging AI to prioritize information efficiently.

 

Benefits:

– CoD fosters the ability to communicate more information in fewer words, maintaining brevity and relevance.

– It ensures essential entities and concepts, enhancing the summary.

– Refining summaries iteratively, AI's outputs become more precise.

 

Challenges:

– Balancing the introduction of new entities while maintaining summary length requires skillful prompt crafting.

– There is a potential risk of oversimplifying complex information to meet the word count constraint.

– Ensuring that the summary remains coherent and contextually accurate while adding density can be challenging.

 

Use Cases:

– To assist students and researchers in creating summaries of complex academic texts.

– To produce high-density informative content without verbosity.

– (In data-heavy fields) to summarize reports by focusing on the most significant data points.

 

Example Framework:

Initial Prompt:

TASK:

Write a summary of the following text, not exceeding 100 words. "[TEXT]"

CoD Process:

Step 1:

Learn what constitutes a 'New Entity' within the provided TEXT.

Step 2:

Identify one 'New Entity' absent from the existing summary.

Step 3:

Compose an improved summary incorporating all entities from the previous version plus the 'New Entity.'

Cycle:

Repeat the cycle ten times to enhance entity density.

Guidelines:

To maintain the word limit, utilize contractions, eliminate superfluous language, and continuously refine the summary for flow and clarity.

4.6 Skeleton-of-Thought Prompting (SoT)

 

The Skeleton-of-thought approach structures prompts to outline the 'bones' or essential elements of reasoning before fleshing out the details. It is like building the frame of a house before you add the walls and roof. This technique allows you to construct a clear and logical framework for the conversation, setting a foundation for detailed exploration.

Imagine a prompt asking for a plan to start a small business. SoT would lay out the foundational steps before delving into the intricacies of each step.

SoT is particularly beneficial when addressing multifaceted issues or concepts that require a solid foundational understanding before moving into complex territory. It helps in creating a structured and comprehensive discourse with ChatGPT.

The SoT technique is valuable for its organizational benefits and clarity for complex conversations.

 

Benefits:

– To establish a clear base to build upon, ensuring that complex information is well-organized.

– To promote clarity and a better understanding of the conversation's direction.

– To foster a logical flow of information.

– After establishing the skeleton, the discussion can go into detail in a focused manner, directly addressing each point.

 

 

Challenges:

– Starting with an overly simplified outline, it can omit crucial nuances for understanding complex issues.

– The structured nature of SoT may limit the fluidity of conversation, making it less dynamic.

– Ensuring that the expansion of details remains tied to the original outline can be more difficult as the conversation progresses.

 

Use Cases:

– To teach concepts in a structured manner, laying out the basics before moving into complexities.

– To devise strategies, providing a clear plan with established preliminary steps.

– (For writers and content creators) to outline articles or stories before fleshing them out.

– (In research) to outline a study's methodology before detailing the specific procedures and tests.

The technique proves to be effective in scenarios requiring structured output from AI.

TASK GUIDELINES: (Skeleton)

5.4 Temporary Limitations

 

The magic of ChatGPT is great, but it has its limits. Every user should know these limits before diving in. Being aware helps avoiding unexpected surprises. But be aware that the short list of limits addressed below does not cover it all. ChatGPT can mislead with its fancy talk, so you must check its answers carefully. This could be harder than looking up stuff on your own. ChatGPT is a tool, not a replacement for critical thinking.

As with any tool, over-relying on ChatGPT can be a pitfall. It offers rapid, extensive insights, but it is vital to cross-reference and verify crucial data. Diversifying information sources, collaborating with experts, and periodically grounding AI interactions with manual checks can strike a balance between efficiency and accuracy.

 

1. Hallucinations

ChatGPT speaks well, but that does not mean it is always right. It is so good with words it makes things sound right even when they are not. The explanation is related to the design of ChatGPT and the training data. By design, ChatGPT is built to predict the next word based on the previous words. Its only job is to autocomplete, but it can keep autocompleting very coherently to make long texts. The quality and accuracy of the ChatGPT autocompletion job reflects the quality and accuracy of the data it was trained on. And this does not always lead to factual accuracy as textual data is not always a source of truth. During training, ChatGPT uses more textual data from the internet than science books. And there is a lot of wrong info out there, about 30% being false.

Now, even with this limit, ChatGPT often gets the answers right, which is cool! This is likely because of the good info on places like Wikipedia. But, as a rule of thumb, it is crucial to thoroughly fact-check the content. Make a point to check dates, locations, numbers, and any claim that seems very specific.

 

2. Arithmetic and Mathematics

When you ask ChatGPT math stuff, sometimes it messes up. It can guess simple answers right if it has seen them before. It is getting better at math, but it is not quite there yet. This is because ChatGPT does not have a source of truth; it relies on recognizing patterns in the language of math problems it has seen before.

 

3. Citations

ChatGPT struggles with citations. It guesses a lot. It knows how a citation looks and can make up fancy-sounding titles, but most of them do not really exist. The model does not have access to external sources in real-time (unless you use a browsing plugin) so it generates what it "thinks" would be a correct citation based on patterns it learned during training.

 

4. Consistency

ChatGPT can get an answer right but messes up the explanation. This inconsistency occurs as the model might pick up on the correct answer from patterns in the training data, but fails to understand the underlying logic or facts, leading to incorrect explanations.

Current Events: ChatGPT's info stops in January 2022. If you ask about recent stuff, it makes up answers. ChatGPT does not have real-time access to the internet, so it is like asking someone who has been out of the loop for a while.

AI's journey is long but filled with promise. Today's issues could pave the way for tomorrow's wonders.

6.1 What is DALL-E?

 

DALL-E is a 12-billion parameter version of GPT-3 trained using a dataset of text–image pairs.1

GPT-3 transformer architecture is excellent at handling sequences of data.

While learning, images are converted into a sequence of discrete tokens in a process that is analogous to tokenizing text and DALL-E is trained on pairs of images and corresponding text captions. The model learns to associate the text with the visual concepts in the images by processing the sequences of tokens from both modalities.

DALL-E has a diverse set of capabilities, including creating anthropomorphized versions of animals and objects, combining unrelated concepts in plausible ways, rendering text, and applying transformations to existing images.

It receives both the text and the image as a single stream of data containing up to 1,280 tokens, being trained to use maximum likelihood to generate all the tokens, one after another.

What can DALL-E do?

– Transforms natural language descriptions into distinctive visuals by blending various concepts, attributes, and styles.

– Generates original images (up to a resolution of 1024x1024 pixels).

Once you generate the images, you can download and modify them using any preferred editing tool.

DALL-E's latest edition (the 3rd) brings new features:

– Generates legible text within images.

– Depicts historical personalities and well-known celebrities.

– Incorporates filters for ethical public usage.

– "Out-painting" function extends images beyond their original frame for fresh compositions.

– "In-painting" function allows for selective replacements within images.

– Adjusts real photos based on captions, adding or removing elements such as objects, shadows, reflections, and textures.

– Produces high-resolution images.

To save resources, DALL-E generates variations to your prompt (most of the time expanding it), rendering 4 images. You can choose whatever image you like and ask DALL-E, in the next prompt, to modify it to your needs.

If your prompt is 76-word long, DALL-E truncates it.

 

DALL-E generated images and copyright

Firstly, any artwork generated by DALL-E is not solely yours. What it means is that you can use the AI-created images, but they are not exclusive; others might use them as well. Secondly, modifying copyrighted material with DALL-E can lead to copyright infringement, even if the result looks different.

Our recommendation is to use DALL-E responsibly and to honor intellectual property boundaries.

7. Troubleshooting common issues

 

Despite their vast capabilities, LLMs may occasionally hesitate and ask for clarifications. This section delves into those nuances, highlighting common mistakes and providing solutions.

7.1 Understanding Misinterpretations

 

Misinterpretations, common with LLMs like ChatGPT, often arise from the nuanced nature of human language. Our everyday communication depends on context, cultural references, and implicit assumptions. When we prompt using idiomatic or region-specific expressions, we should not always expect ChatGPT to understand them.

To mitigate these challenges, we must craft clear and direct queries. Although ChatGPT is trained to manage complex language patterns, we can achieve better outcomes by simplifying their requests. Removing extraneous details and focusing on the core query can yield more precise results.

Moreover, understanding that ChatGPT bases its responses on patterns in its training data helps set realistic expectations. When a question leans into areas less represented in its training or is structured unconventionally, ChatGPT has a lower chance of interpreting it correctly.

Regularly revisiting and tweaking our approach to questioning can be effective. Analyzing the model's responses, learning from previous interactions, and adjusting the phrasing or structure of queries can progressively enhance the quality of the conversation. Ultimately, grasping the art of effective communication with ChatGPT is a continuous journey that involves learning, adaptation, and a keen sense of observation.

7.2 Handling Biases

 

Artificial Intelligence, such as ChatGPT, is molded by its training data. A significant proportion of it comes from the internet and carries embedded biases, whether overt or nuanced. Consequently, ChatGPT might unintentionally mirror certain societal, cultural, or specific biases present in its foundational material, leading to imbalanced or contextually inappropriate outputs.1

Central to addressing biases is understanding cultural sensitivity. Every culture embodies unique norms, values, and beliefs. It is pivotal for users to recognize that AI responses might not always resonate with or appreciate these cultural nuances, potentially leading to generalizations or misunderstandings.

Awareness is the cornerstone of combating biases. Users should critically assess AI responses, comprehending that no model is inherently objective and biases are unintended artifacts of extensive training.

To actively mitigate biases:

– Explicit Contextualization: When discussing specific cultural contexts, provide clear and comprehensive details to guide AI.

– Diversify Queries: Pose questions from varied perspectives or rephrase them to assess AI's consistency and impartiality.

– Cross-Reference: Validate AI responses against other reliable sources or platforms to ensure a more rounded and unbiased understanding.

In summary, while ChatGPT heralds a new era of information access, users must judiciously interact, scrutinizing and ensuring that acquired insights are unbiased and culturally attuned.

7.3 Handling Ambiguous Responses

 

Ambiguity in responses is not uncommon when interacting with ChatGPT or, for that matter, any sophisticated AI language model. Given the vastness of its training data and the complexity of human language, ChatGPT can sometimes provide answers that are more generalized than desired or are open to multiple interpretations.

When faced with ambiguous responses, it is essential to adopt a proactive stance. Here are some strategies to manage them:

– Rephrase the Question: Sometimes, a simple rewording of your query leads to a clear response.

– Ask for Specifics: If a response seems too broad, prompt AI to provide more detailed information or examples. For example, if you ask about the benefits of a particular diet and receive a generalized answer, you might follow up with "Please list specific health benefits."

– Seek Clarity: If a response is confusing or open to multiple interpretations, ask AI to clarify its answer. A simple "Explain that in simpler terms" might be effective.

– Sequential Questioning: Breaking down your main question into a series of smaller, related questions can guide AI to provide more specific answers for each aspect of your query.

– Feedback Loop: If a response is ambiguous, providing feedback about what you found unclear guides AI toward a more precise answer on a subsequent attempt.

Recognizing that AI's output might serve as a conversation starter rather than a definitive conclusion might reshape the user's expectations and enhance their experience. With patience and a strategic approach, users can navigate ambiguities and extract the specific information they seek from ChatGPT.

7.4 Control Verbosity and Redundancy

 

While ChatGPT aims to deliver in-depth and holistic answers, sometimes it falls into verbosity or redundancy. Replies, although rich in content, could be overwhelming or repetitive for users seeking succinct information.

To effectively manage such situations:

– Set Clear Expectations: Begin by explicitly stating your preference for a concise answer. Phrases like "in a nutshell" or "in brief" can guide the model toward a focused response.

– Refine the Question: Simplifying or narrowing down your question can reduce the chances of receiving a lengthy or redundant answer. For instance, instead of asking "What is the history of the internet?," you might ask, "What are the key milestones in the internet's development?"

– Ask for Summaries: When provided with a too long response, request summary.

– Limit Word Count: Explicitly set a word limit, like "Please explain [this] in 50 words or less."

– Feedback: If ChatGPT provides a redundant answer, let the model know. Feedback helps in refining subsequent interactions.

7.5 Inappropriate Content

 

Interacting with artificial intelligence platforms like ChatGPT introduces the potential for unintended outputs, some of which might be inappropriate or harmful. This challenge arises because ChatGPT's knowledge represents diverse datasets from the internet, which, while encompassing a broad spectrum of information, often include biased, controversial, or sensitive material.

OpenAI is aware of this challenge and has taken measures to address it. Firstly, OpenAI employs rigorous fine-tuning processes, where the model is trained on narrower, more specific datasets after its broad initial training. Feedback loops involving human reviewers also play an important role, as they evaluate model outputs against a wide range of inputs and provide corrections.

OpenAI also invests in research and engineering to reduce subtle and glaring biases in ChatGPT's responses. They actively seek feedback from the user community and have iterated on their models based on this invaluable input. The objective is to refine continuously the model to ensure that it respects user values while minimizing the chances of generating potential harmful content.

7.6 Limitations in Depth and Breadth

 

ChatGPT possesses an impressive span of knowledge, but like any tool it has its limitations. There can be instances where its insights on certain subjects might not be as exhaustive or specialized as expected. OpenAI constantly updates the model, incorporating diverse data and feedback to improve depth and accuracy.

Nevertheless, users should approach niche or specialized topics with an understanding that ChatGPT might not replace committed experts in those fields. It is essential to view it as an informative gateway rather than an ultimate authority and to consider complementing its insights with information from specialized sources when needed.

7.7 The Importance of User Feedback

 

OpenAI is soliciting user feedback (after each interaction with ChatGPT) to understand users' experiences, challenges, and expectations. User feedback provides insights into how ChatGPT performs in real-world scenarios.

Consider each piece of feedback, you give as your opportunity to refine ChatGPT, making it more attuned to user's needs and minimizing potential pitfalls.

Basic Terminology

Getting started with a revolutionary technology like Generative AI (GenAI) can be overwhelming. A key element is to establish a common terminology. It is essential to allow a more accurate, effective, and knowledgeable use of this modern technology. To my knowledge, as of now, September 2023, there are no universal definitions of Artificial Intelligence and Machine Learning.

ARTIFICIAL INTELLIGENCE (AI): Field of study that focuses on creating computer systems capable of performing tasks that would require human intelligence. These tasks include problem-solving, learning, and decision-making.

ARTIFICIAL NEURAL NETWORK: Computing system inspired by the human brain. It is a web of nodes, or "neurons," connected by lines, called "synapses." Data flows through these connections. Each node processes the data, like a mini-decision maker. Layer by layer, they work together to solve a problem or make a prediction. Simple tasks need just a few layers. Complex ones, many layers. It represents the backbone of modern AI.

ATTENTION: Tool in computer algorithms that helps focus on important parts of data. In the context of AI and language models like ChatGPT, it helps the system pay attention to keywords or phrases to make better sense of long sentences.

MULTI-HEAD ATTENTION: Advanced form of attention that uses multiple "focus points" to understand different parts of data at the same time. It is like reading a sentence and understanding the meaning, tone, and context all at once.

CLASSIFICATION: Task in machine learning where the goal is to sort data into specific categories. Picture sorting fruits into baskets labeled 'apples' or 'bananas' based on their color. That is a classification. You are putting things into categories. For example, it can help decide if a medical test shows a dangerous tumor or a harmless one. (Image adapted from Oracle.) LLMs are also able to execute classification tasks like classifying the reviews (positive or negative) of a product using customer reviews.

CLUSTERING: Task that groups things based on their similarities without you labelling them. Think of it as a toy organization. You group your cars, dolls, and action figures into separate piles based on what they look like. (Image adapted from Oracle.) LLMs are also able to execute clustering tasks. E.g., clustering customer feedback based on their preferences by grouping feedback based on similar features or issues.

CONTEXT WINDOW: Range of words or data points that a machine learning model, like ChatGPT, looks at to understand what is currently being discussed. It is like the model's short-term memory, helping it to make sense of the words or phrases it is currently focused on.

CORPUS OF DATA: Collection of information that a computer uses to learn or make decisions. This can include text, images, sounds, or numbers. Think of it as the "study material" for a machine learning model.

CUTOFF DATE: The last day the training data was updated. After that, we cannot access new information. ChatGPT cutoff date, as of September 2023, is January 2022. To access more recent information, you can use plugins that add browsing capabilities or access to private resources.

DATA MINING: The act of digging through a lot of data to find useful information or patterns. It helps make sense of messy or random info, like lots of text or pictures.

DEEP LEARNING (DL): Type of machine learning, based on neural networks, that is good at finding subtle patterns in lots of data. It uses layers of calculations to get better and more detailed insights. Imagine a smart box that can tell if a picture is a dog or a cat. The box has multiple layers that look for different traits like fur or ears to figure out the animal. It is not perfect at first but gets better with more data.

DECODER: Transformer component that takes a sequence of input representations and produces a sequence of output tokens. (see TRANSFORMER ARCHITECTURE)

DIFFUSION MODELS: Generative AI models that create images in a process similar to the one of a sculptor molding clay. It starts with a raw, undefined canvas and gradually shapes it, refining every requested detail until the desired output is attained.

The training process starts with a dataset of clean, structured images. New images are created by gradually "adding noise" over a series of steps until the data is completely random noise. The model learns how to reverse the diffusion process by learning the parameters that define the denoising process at each step.

 

The training dataset is labeled. The words "dog, Cocker Spaniel, sitting" act like a "cue" telling the model what it is expected to generate.

Image generation tools that utilize diffusion models can generate images that are variations on the themes found in their training datasets, which can be astonishingly realistic or artistically stylized.

EMBEDDING: Feature for turning words or symbols into a set of numbers, known as vectors, that a computer can understand. These number sets help the machine get the meaning, context, or importance of each word.

ENCODER: Transformer component that takes a sequence of input tokens and produces a sequence of hidden representations. (see TRANSFORMER ARCHITECTURE)

EXPLAINABLE AI (XAI): Techniques that attempt to make AI's inference process more transparent, interpretable, and understandable by humans.

FINE-TUNING: An add-on that comes after the model is built. Fine-tuning is about specialization, and refining the skills of a language model. It is about taking a general-purpose Language Model (a FOUNDATION MODEL) and making it an expert in something specific. The goal is to teach the model new tricks or to improve its skills in certain fields. You make the "adjustment" by training the Foundation Model on a specific dataset.

FOUNDATION MODEL: Type of language machine trained on a huge amount of data, like text and code. It serves as a starting point for creating more specialized AI tools.

GENERATIVE AI: Type of AI that creates new content, such as text, images, or sounds. While the term "generate" is used, it is worth noting that AI is not inventing from scratch but rearranging existing data it has learned from.

HALLUCINATION: When a generative AI, like a language model, produces something that is not based on given facts or data. One mistake the machine can make.

INFERENCE: In AI's world, this means making educated guesses based on existing data. While it is called "reasoning," it is more about finding patterns in the data and making predictions, rather than true logical thought.

"KNOWLEDGE" TRANSFER (LEARNING TRANSFER): Pre-trained language model has already learned a lot from tons of data. When you fine-tune it, you are letting the new model inherit that broad knowledge, and then specialize in a certain field. This way, the fine-tuned model gets a head start in its own training.

LANGUAGE: Set of symbols or sounds used for communication and, to some extent, thinking. People use it to share ideas, emotions, and information. It can be spoken, written, or signed and varies in complexity.

LANGUAGE MODEL (LM): Type of AI trained to mimic human-like text or speech. It learns from a vast "corpus" of textual data and uses those patterns to generate language that sounds like it comes from a person.

 

LANGUAGE MODEL PARAMETERS: The numbers a language model learns while training. These numbers, like weights and biases, guide how the model turns what it is given into an answer or text. For example, 187 billion parameters were used to train ChatGPT.

LANGUAGE MODEL PRE-TRAINED: Language model that has already been trained on a huge amount of data before you even use it. This means it can start generating text or answering questions without needing extra training first.

 

LANGUAGE MODEL, FINE-TUNED: Pre-trained language model that has been further trained on specific data. This makes it better at handling tasks or questions in a certain field.

 

LAYER: The building blocks in a language model's architecture. Think of these as steps in a language model's brain. Each layer learns a bit about the data, makes some changes, and passes it on. They represent how the model learns what it knows.

 

MACHINE LEARNING (ML): The science of how to make computers learn on their own. Instead of being told what to do (i.e., programmed what to do), they use statistics to find patterns in data like numbers, words, or pictures.

MESSAGE: Texts exchanged between AI and user. Conversations can be as short as one message or many back-and-forth turns.

NATURAL LANGUAGE PROCESSING (NLP): Part of AI that helps computers get our language. It builds tools so computers can read, understand, and even write like us. It handles stuff like translating languages and answering questions.

PLUGIN: Software tools used to extend or add new capabilities to a system, that otherwise would not be able to do. In the ChatGPT case, plugins are pieces of software that allow ChatGPT to access third-party resources or functionalities. Only install plugins that come from trusted sources that respect your privacy and do not re-use your data.

 

PROMPT: Question or statement that you give to an AI model to get a response. OpenAI documentation highlights the importance of providing context and preferences to tailor AI's responses to your needs. The additional information could be structured using the Prompt Components Framework.

 

PROMPT DESIGN (see PROMPT ENGINEERING)

PROMPT ENGINEERING: Often referred to as PROMPT DESIGN, represents the art of asking AI (LLMs like ChatGPT, Bard, and more) the right way. You study how to frame your questions to get the best, most accurate answers from the model. It is all about finding the sweet spot through trial and error.

REGRESSION: Special task in machine learning where the computer tries to predict a specific number. Imagine guessing someone's age by their height. You plot age against height on a graph and use this to predict a new person's age. So, regression helps you make number-based guesses.

 

REINFORCEMENT LEARNING FROM HUMAN FEEDBACK (RLHF): Feature to make AI smarter using human input. You give feedback, and the model learns from it.

SELF-ATTENTION: Feature that lets a model like ChatGPT pay close attention to different parts of what it is reading, even if those parts are far apart.

SUPERVISED LEARNING (SL): Type of ML in which a computer, given problems and the correct answers (labeled data), learns over time to solve similar problems on its own. Think of it as a tutor-student setup. The computer gets a bunch of questions and their answers. It studies them so it can answer similar questions on its own later.

UNSUPERVISED LEARNING (USL): Type of machine learning that does not require labeled data. Picture a detective piecing together a puzzle without a guide. The computer scours through data, finding patterns all on its own.

TEXT NORMALIZATION: The process of converting text to a standard format, such as lowercasing all text, removing special characters, and converting numbers to their written form.

TOKEN: The smallest semantic unit that makes up the input and output sequences processed by a language model. Tokens typically represent individual words or sub-words, although they can also include characters or other linguistic units. Each token is assigned a unique identifier (usually integers).

TOKENIZATION: The process of breaking down text into individual units, such as words or phrases. This is an important step in preparing text data for NLP tasks.

TRANSFORMER ARCHITECTURE: Blueprint for building advanced AI tools like ChatGPT. It consists of two main parts: an encoder that understands the input and a decoder that creates the output. Many transformers are pre-trained, meaning they have already learned from a large set of data before they are fine-tuned for specific tasks.

PRE-TRAINED NEURAL NETWORK: Neural network that has already been trained on a large dataset to recognize general patterns in data. It forms the basis of specific AI tools like ChatGPT and can be fine-tuned for specialized tasks.

TRANSFORMER LANGUAGE MODEL: Language model that is built using the transformer architecture. The transformer enables the Language Model to capture contextual information effectively.

 

TRANSFORMER (GENERATIVE PRE-TRAINED TRANSFORMER, GPT): Special type of transformer. Unlike standard transformers with encoders and decoders, GPT uses just the decoder. It is trained on a heap of text, the goal being to excel at understanding and to be a pro at crafting human-like text.

TUNING (FINE-TUNING): The process of further training a pre-trained language model, like ChatGPT, on a smaller dataset to adapt it to a specific task or domain (e.g., health care). The model already knows a lot from its general training. But fine-tuning makes it an expert in, for example, healthcare or cooking recipes. It is about mastering the details.

TRAINING: The process of teaching a Language Model, like ChatGPT, to perform a specific task by exposing it to large amounts of data. It is like teaching a young mind. You feed a language model heap of data, and the model picks up patterns and grasps the rhythm of words.

VECTOR: The numerical representation of a word in the context of the phrase. Basically, it is a list of numbers representing different aspects of the word. Let's make an analogy between a word and a person. Think of a vector like a digital ID for a word. Imagine each word as a person, and the vector as their profile—height, age, and hobbies, all converted into numbers. This profile helps the computer understand the word's role in a sentence. So, in a world of numbers, a vector is a word's identity card.
