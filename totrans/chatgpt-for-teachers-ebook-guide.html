Sending Effective Prompts

Earlier we mentioned around sending effective prompts. We mentioned that important elements were to be clear and specific in the request, set some context, provide structure and to iterate when the output wasn’t quite what you needed.

Here are some more things to consider:

Breaking down queries

When prompting ChatGPT it is important to not overload the questions in one prompt, this is a conversation. You can start the prompt with a clear question, some context and desired output structure. Then develop that with further prompts after the initial prompt is received.

For example, you want to ask about UK history, instead of just sending summarise World War 1 you might want to ask first about the lead up to WW1, then ask about what happened during WW1 and finally ask about the conclusion.

Adding roles

Another effective method of prompting is to add a role to the prompt.

i.e. you can say “act as a teacher”, “act as a social media expert”, “act as a scientist”, “act as a storyteller”.

This will help the context for ChatGPT to know how to respond.

Explaining who the output is for

Another key element of the context for ChatGPT is to explain who you want to relay this information to.

For example, “make the explanation suitable for 5-year-olds”, “explain this to a beginner”, “provide the output suitable for a parent”

Tone of voice

ChatGPT can change how it responds in different tones of voice.

For example, you can say, “respond in a formal tone”, “respond in a casual tone”, “respond like Shakespeare”

Giving it some help

You may know exactly how you want it to respond

For example, “provide output as a list”, “provide output with explanations”, “start with x”, “make sure to include x, y, z”

All these help ChatGPT narrow its aim and provide a much more effective output which will reduce the number of iterations, if any, you need to get the output you want.

Enhancing Lesson Planning

How ChatGPT Can Generate Lesson Ideas

Lesson planning is often a time-consuming task for teachers. ChatGPT can act as a creative partner, providing ideas that are both innovative and tailored to specific teaching goals. By simply describing the subject, topic, and learning objectives, teachers can receive structured suggestions in moments.

When interacting with ChatGPT it is important to give content, to trial different ways of asking the same question and to provide feedback. For example, if the response hasn’t given a suitable answer, then you can say “try again” or “try again with more focus on [x]”. The current drawback with ChatGPT (which is improving with each release) is a thing called hallucinations. This is where ChatGPT might make up information or provide answers that just look right but factually aren’t. In this case you always need to fact check the responses you get and tell it when things are not right.

Examples of Simple Lesson Idea Generation

ChatGPT excels at generating quick ideas for lessons across subjects and age groups. Consider the following examples:

Primary Science:
Prompt: "Create a lesson plan for Year 5 students to understand the water cycle."
Output: A structured lesson that includes a hands-on experiment (e.g., creating a mini water cycle in a plastic bag), visual aids to explain evaporation, condensation, and precipitation, and an engaging class discussion about real-world applications.

Secondary English:
Prompt: "Help me create a lesson exploring themes in Shakespeare’s 'Macbeth' for Year 10."
Output: Suggestions for activities like group discussions on ambition and guilt, creative role-playing exercises to explore character motivations, and a worksheet analysing key soliloquies to deepen understanding.

History:
Prompt: "Outline a Year 8 lesson on the causes of World War I. Ensure you cover [x], [x], and [x]."
Output: A dynamic plan including a timeline activity, primary source analysis, and a collaborative class debate on the war's underlying causes.

Maximising ChatGPT’s Potential for Advanced Lesson Planning

Providing detailed prompts is the key to unlocking ChatGPT’s full potential for lesson planning. The more context you include, the better the results. Here’s an example of a more advanced prompt:

Example:
"You are an expert in creating lesson plans. Develop a detailed lesson plan for Year 5 students learning about the water cycle. The objectives for the lesson are:

  • To understand the water cycle’s features and sequence.

  • To identify and explain each stage of the cycle.

  • To recognise the importance of water.

  • To explain the water cycle’s role in sustaining life.

The lesson plan should include:

  • Clear objectives.

  • A compelling introduction.

  • Step-by-step instructions for activities.

  • Practical, hands-on elements.

  • Methods to assess understanding.

  • A conclusion that reinforces learning.

Ensure the plan is engaging, age-appropriate, and aligned with curriculum standards in the UK."

This level of specificity ensures a higher-quality response, often delivering a ready-to-use framework that teachers can customise further. This template can also be easily adapted to different subjects and topics by swapping out the objectives and content focus.

Adapting AI-Created Plans for Your Classroom

While ChatGPT provides a solid foundation, teachers should always adapt its outputs to meet the specific needs of their students. Here are four strategies:

Review for Accuracy: Ensure that the generated content aligns with your subject knowledge and curriculum standards.

Adjust for Differentiation: Change tasks for different ability levels. For instance, simplify language for less advanced students or add challenges for high achievers.

Incorporate Local Context: Make the lesson relatable by adding examples or references relevant to your school’s community.

Blend with Your Style: Add your personal teaching style and preferred methods to enhance engagement.

Example: Adapting a ChatGPT-Suggested Lesson

  • Original suggestion: A group activity to analyse propaganda posters from World War II.

  • Adaptation: Add local historical posters to make the activity more engaging for students.

By personalising content, ChatGPT thus enables teachers to address each learner’s unique needs in a time saving way during preparation.

Ethics, Myths and Challenges

The integration of AI tools like ChatGPT into education brings immense opportunities for innovation, but it also raises critical ethical considerations and challenges that must be addressed. From inclusivity and bias to privacy concerns and the myths surrounding AI, educators must navigate these complexities to use the technology responsibly and effectively.

Myths and Misconceptions About AI in Education

As AI becomes more prominent in classrooms, several myths can create scepticism or confusion. Addressing these misconceptions is essential to building trust and understanding:

  1. "AI will take my job":

AI tools like ChatGPT are designed to assist, not replace, teachers. While they can streamline administrative tasks and inspire lesson ideas, they cannot replicate the human qualities of empathy, cultural understanding, and adaptability that educators bring to their work. AI enhances teaching rather than replacing the critical role of teachers.

  1. "It can answer any question, even controversial ones like ‘Is the government lying?’":

ChatGPT provides responses based on patterns in its training data but does not possess consciousness, opinions, or the ability to verify the accuracy of its outputs. It’s important to remind students and users that AI responses should be critically evaluated and supplemented with trusted sources.

  1. "AI is selling my data":

ChatGPT does not store personal data from interactions, but users must still follow best practices to protect sensitive information. OpenAI adheres to stringent privacy policies, and no data entered into the system is retained for training purposes unless explicitly shared for feedback.

By debunking these myths, educators can foster a balanced perspective and encourage responsible use of AI in education.

Ensuring Inclusivity and Avoiding Bias

AI systems like ChatGPT are only as unbiased as the data they are trained on, meaning that biases can inadvertently appear in outputs. Teachers must take an active role in recognising, mitigating, and teaching about bias to ensure the equitable use of AI.

Addressing Bias in AI Outputs:

  • Recognising Bias: ChatGPT may reflect cultural or gender stereotypes embedded in its training data. For example, when asked for examples of scientists, it may predominantly list men unless prompted otherwise.

  • Promoting Inclusivity: Teachers can use prompts that encourage diversity, such as "Provide examples of influential scientists from various cultural backgrounds."

  • Providing Feedback: OpenAI offers tools for reporting biased or harmful outputs, helping improve the model over time.

Strategies for Educators:

  • Be a Gatekeeper: Review all AI-generated content to ensure it aligns with classroom values and educational goals.

  • Encourage Critical Thinking: Use biased or incomplete outputs as teaching moments to spark discussions about bias and inclusivity.

  • Diversify Sources: Supplement AI-generated content with materials that provide diverse perspectives, ensuring a well-rounded educational experience.

Navigating Privacy and Data Protection Issues

Data privacy is a significant concern when using AI tools in education. ChatGPT processes prompts and generates responses but does not store user data for future interactions. However, teachers should still adhere to best practices to safeguard sensitive information.

Key Privacy Considerations

  1. Avoid Sharing Personal Information: Never input student names, personal details, or confidential data into ChatGPT.

  2. Use Anonymised Data: When generating reports or summaries, provide anonymised or generic information to protect student privacy.

  3. Follow School Policies: Ensure that your use of AI tools aligns with your institution’s data protection and privacy guidelines.

Educating Students on Privacy:

Teachers can also use ChatGPT to teach students about digital safety and privacy. For example:

Balancing Human Expertise with AI Assistance

While AI tools like ChatGPT can enhance teaching, they should never replace the critical role of educators. Teachers bring emotional intelligence, cultural context, and professional judgement that no AI can replicate.

Maintaining the Human Element

  1. Enhancing, Not Replacing: Use ChatGPT to streamline tasks or inspire ideas, but ensure human expertise shapes the final content.

Students Use of AI

As AI tools like ChatGPT become more accessible, students are increasingly likely to experiment with them for academic purposes. From generating essay outlines to solving complex equations, AI offers students powerful capabilities. However, its use must be guided by principles of integrity, responsibility, and critical thinking to ensure it enhances learning rather than undermining it. Teachers must be prepared to recognise AI use, encourage appropriate applications, and explain why reliance on tools like ChatGPT for tasks such as essay writing is not a substitute for authentic learning.

How Students Might Be Using AI

Students are already exploring AI tools in various ways, sometimes positively and sometimes inappropriately. Common applications include:

  1. Homework Help:

    1. What They Do: Students use ChatGPT to explain concepts, summarise textbook material, or solve problems.

    2. Example: "Explain Newton’s laws of motion in simple terms."

  1. Essay Writing and Editing:

    1. What They Do: Some students may use ChatGPT to generate essays, edit their drafts, or check grammar.

    2. Example: "Write a 500-word essay on the causes of World War I."

  1. Problem-Solving:

    1. What They Do: AI tools assist students with calculations, coding, or brainstorming solutions for projects.

    2. Example: "Generate ideas for a science fair project on renewable energy."

  1. Creative Tasks:

    1. What They Do: Students use ChatGPT for storytelling, poetry, or brainstorming creative ideas.

Example: "Help me write a short story about a child who discovers a hidden island."

While many of these uses can enrich learning, students may also misuse AI by passing off generated content as their own or by over-relying on it to bypass critical thinking.

How to Spot AI Use

Teachers can stay vigilant for signs that students may be using AI inappropriately:

  1. Uncharacteristic Work:

    1. Sudden improvements in writing style, vocabulary, or coherence that don’t align with a student’s usual abilities can indicate AI assistance.

  1. Lack of Understanding:

    1. When questioned about their work, students may struggle to explain key ideas, methods, or reasoning behind their answers if they relied heavily on AI.

  1. Generic Responses:

    1. AI-generated content may lack nuance, originality, or specific references to class discussions or assigned materials.

  1. AI Detection Tools:

    1. Many platforms now offer tools to detect AI-generated content. While not foolproof, these can provide teachers with additional evidence when suspecting AI use.

Explaining Why Writing Essays with ChatGPT Isn’t the Answer

It’s essential to address why relying on ChatGPT for essay writing undermines the learning process. Students may be tempted by the convenience of AI, but teachers can explain the value of authentic learning:

  1. Critical Thinking and Originality:

Writing essays develops essential skills like analysing information, forming arguments, and expressing original thoughts. ChatGPT can’t replicate a student’s personal insights or unique perspective.

  1. Understanding the Subject:

Completing essays builds deeper understanding of the material. AI-generated content might cover surface-level facts but can’t replace the learning that happens through researching, planning, and writing.

  1. Academic Integrity:

Passing off AI-generated work as one’s own is a form of plagiarism. Explaining the ethical implications helps students see the importance of honesty in their academic journey.

  1. Preparation for the Future:

The ability to think critically, communicate clearly, and solve problems independently is vital for future success. Over-reliance on AI can hinder the development of these skills.

How to Address This with Students:

  • Have Open Conversations: Discuss with students the purpose of essays and how the writing process contributes to their growth.

  • Explain AI’s Role: Emphasise that AI is a tool for support, not a replacement for their own effort. For example, ChatGPT can help brainstorm ideas or provide feedback but should not complete the assignment.

  • Set Clear Boundaries: Clearly outline acceptable and unacceptable uses of AI for assignments, including consequences for misuse.

Shaping a Balanced Perspective on AI

As AI becomes an integral part of education and daily life, teaching students to use it responsibly is crucial. By understanding how students might use AI, spotting inappropriate reliance on it, and explaining the importance of authentic learning, teachers can foster a balanced and ethical approach. ChatGPT and similar tools should be viewed as collaborators, not substitutes, helping students develop their skills while preparing them for an AI-driven future.

Best Practices for Using ChatGPT

  • Start with Clear Prompts:

    • Vague prompts can lead to irrelevant or confusing outputs. Be specific and detailed in your instructions.

    • Example: Instead of “Tell me about history” try “Summarise the key events leading to World War II in simple terms for Year 9 students.”

  • Iterate and Refine:

    • Don’t hesitate to refine prompts to improve the quality of responses. ChatGPT often delivers better results with more precise inputs.

    • Activity: Encourage students to practise rewriting prompts for clarity and focus.

  • Blend AI with Traditional Methods:

    • Use ChatGPT to complement, not replace, traditional teaching techniques. Combine its outputs with your expertise to create richer learning experiences.

  • Review AI Outputs Carefully:

    • Always review and adapt ChatGPT’s responses to ensure accuracy and alignment with educational goals.

    • Example: Check for factual accuracy in historical timelines or science explanations before presenting them in class.

  • Set Boundaries:

    • Define clear rules for how students can use ChatGPT. Discuss appropriate use cases, such as research assistance, and discourage misuse, like copying assignments.

How to Write Effective Prompts

Writing effective prompts is the key to getting the most out of ChatGPT. Teachers can follow these steps to craft queries that yield high-quality results:

Prompt Writing Tips

  • Be Specific:

    • Include the subject, audience, and purpose. Example: “Create a Year 7 lesson plan on photosynthesis with hands-on activities and assessment questions.”

  • Provide Context:

    • If needed, provide background information to guide the response. Example: “Summarise the causes of the French Revolution, focusing on economic factors.”

  • Ask for Structure:

    • Request specific formats, such as bullet points, outlines, or essays. Example: “List five discussion questions about climate change for a Year 10 geography class.”

  • Use Iterative Feedback:

    • Follow up with clarifying prompts to refine outputs. Example: “Make the explanation simpler for Year 5 students.”

Avoiding Common Pitfalls in AI Adoption

While ChatGPT can be a powerful ally, missteps can lead to less effective or even problematic outcomes. Here are some common pitfalls and how to avoid them:

  • Over-Reliance on AI:

    • Pitfall: Using ChatGPT as a sole source for lesson planning or student support.

    • Solution: Treat ChatGPT as a tool to enhance, not replace, your role as an educator.

  • Misuse by Students:

    • Pitfall: Students copying ChatGPT outputs for assignments without understanding the material.

    • Solution: Teach ethical AI use and emphasise the importance of originality and comprehension.

  • Neglecting Critical Evaluation:

    • Pitfall: Accepting ChatGPT’s responses without reviewing for accuracy.

    • Solution: Verify all AI-generated content and encourage students to cross-check information.

  • Inadequate Training:

    • Pitfall: Teachers feeling overwhelmed or unprepared to use AI tools effectively.

    • Solution: Participate in training sessions and experiment with ChatGPT to build confidence.

The Role of Reflection in Effective AI Use

Reflection is crucial for maximising the benefits of ChatGPT. Regularly assess how it fits into your teaching practice and adapt as needed.

Reflection Questions

  • What Worked Well?

    • Identify successful uses of ChatGPT in lesson planning or student engagement.

  • What Could Be Improved?

    • Note areas where AI outputs didn’t meet expectations and explore how to refine prompts or methods.

  • How Did Students Respond?

    • Gather feedback from students to understand how ChatGPT impacts their learning experience.
