How Artificial Intelligence Works

Artificial Intelligence (AI) can be understood through a simple analogy: imagine AI as a very advanced recipe book for a computer. This recipe book doesn't just contain fixed recipes; it also teaches the computer how to create new recipes based on the ingredients (data) it gets.

  1. Learning from Data (Training): Just like you learn to cook better by trying different recipes, AI learns from data. We give lots of examples – these can be pictures, texts, numbers, etc. For instance, if we want the AI to recognize cats in photos, we show thousands or even millions of cat pictures. Each picture is a 'recipe' that the AI studies.

  2. Making Connections (Algorithms): The AI uses special sets of rules called algorithms to find patterns in the data. Think of it like learning to recognize which ingredients work well together in cooking. For example, in our cat recognition AI, the algorithm learns to identify patterns like the shape of ears, the texture of fur, etc.

  3. Predicting and Deciding (Inference): After learning from a lot of data, the AI can start making predictions or decisions based on new data it hasn't seen before. Like when you can guess how a new recipe might taste just by knowing the ingredients. If you show the AI a new photo, it can now predict whether there's a cat in it.

  4. Improving Over Time (Feedback): AI can also improve over time. Just as you become a better cook with feedback from people who taste your food, AI systems often get better with feedback. If the AI makes a mistake (like thinking a dog is a cat), it uses that error to improve its future predictions.

  5. Specialization: There are different types of AI for different tasks, just like how cookbooks can specialize in desserts, vegan food, etc. Some AIs are good at recognizing images, others at understanding human language, and so on.

It is for some aspects how a child learns to speak: after hearing thousands of conversations, he slowly becomes smarter. Just learning from experience and a huge quantity of different cases is a winning approach instead of teaching every rule directly, both for a human and for an artificial intelligence.

The Pioneering Years and Evolution

  1. The first AI (1955): The Logic Theorist, developed by Herbert Simon and Allen Newell, marked a significant breakthrough in the nascent field of artificial intelligence. This program, which was able to prove 38 of the first 52 theorems in Whitehead and Russell's "Principia Mathematica", played a pivotal role in the development of AI. Notably, the field of AI wasn't formally recognized at the time; it was only coined by John McCarthy a year later, in 1956, during a conference at Dartmouth College.

  2. First chatbot (1965): Joseph Weizenbaum's creation of ELIZA represented a significant step in the evolution of AI, particularly in natural language processing. ELIZA was capable of engaging in dialogue in English on a variety of topics, simulating a conversation with a human. This development was foundational for the field of chatbots and conversational AI, influencing future advancements in machine-human interaction.

  3. The AI Winter (1980s): The 1980s also witnessed a period known as the AI Winter, characterized by a significant slowdown in AI research and development. This was due to a combination of high expectations and limited practical achievements, leading to reduced funding and interest in the field. However, this period also saw continued work on neural networks and the beginnings of machine learning​. In particular, an approach based on teaching the machine directly, for example how to translate a language through grammatical rules, was abandoned. Instead, it was better to provide thousands of examples of translations and let the machine instruct itself just with statistics, so that it could calculate which translation in a new text was more probable. It was the birth of machine learning.

  4. Early Speech Recognition (1970s - 1992): The seeds of Generative AI were planted with the development of speech recognition systems. One of the earliest milestones was the Sphinx-II project by Xuedong Huang at Carnegie Mellon University, a system that could understand speech with a larger vocabulary than the average human.

  5. IBM's Deep Blue - AI Meets Chess (1997): The victory of IBM's supercomputer Deep Blue over the chess grandmaster Garry Kasparov, the best chess player in the world, was more than a spectacle; it was a demonstration of AI's potential in complex problem-solving and strategic planning. This landmark event helped to popularize the concept of AI in the public imagination.

  6. Instant Messaging Bots (2001): SmarterChild, developed by ActiveBuddy, Inc., was a pioneering chatbot in the realm of instant messaging. Introduced in 2001 on platforms like AOL Instant Messenger and MSN Messenger, it quickly gained popularity, amassing over 30 million users. SmarterChild was more than just an information source; it had a distinct personality and could engage in quirky, playful conversations with users. It provided a range of information from various domains, including news, weather, and sports, and could even play simple games with users. Its development and success demonstrated the potential of AI in everyday applications and set the stage for the emergence of more advanced virtual assistants. The technology behind SmarterChild was a precursor to modern AI assistants like Amazon’s Alexa and Samsung's S Voice.

  7. Siri and the Rise of AI Assistants (2011): In 2011, Apple introduced Siri, a revolutionary voice-activated AI assistant, integrated into its iPhone devices. Siri marked a significant step in bringing AI into the hands of the general public, showcasing the practicality and convenience of voice-activated AI assistants in personal devices. With Siri, users could perform a variety of tasks using voice commands, from setting reminders to searching the internet, making it a hallmark in the evolution of user interface technology.

  8. IBM Watson's Public Debut (2013): IBM Watson's appearance on the TV quiz show "Jeopardy!" in 2011 (with its public deployment following in 2013) was a major showcase of AI's abilities in understanding natural language and processing large datasets. Watson's performance against human champions highlighted its advanced capabilities in areas like language processing, data analysis, and decision-making. This event not only captured the public's imagination but also foreshadowed the future role of AI in various fields, including healthcare, finance, and customer service. Watson's success demonstrated the potential of AI to handle complex, real-world tasks in a way that was previously thought to be the exclusive domain of human intelligence.

  9. The Emergence of Transformer Models (2018 - 2021): The development of transformer models, including GPT-2, GPT-3, and DALL·E, marked a significant advancement in Generative AI. These models demonstrated an unprecedented ability to generate human-like text and creative content like images, showcasing the potential of AI in creative and analytical fields.

  10. The Advent of ChatGPT (December 2022): ChatGPT's introduction was a game-changer, providing a versatile and powerful tool for generating text. Its ability to create, answer, and interact in a human-like manner showcased the practical applications of Generative AI. It reached in only one week a million users: the fastest growth ever in the whole web.

  11. Microsoft 365 and Copilot (2023/2024): The integration of ChatGPT with Microsoft 365 through Copilot marked a significant milestone in bringing Generative AI into the business world. It illustrated the practical use of AI in enhancing productivity and efficiency in various office applications, from drafting emails to data analysis.

Chapter 1.3: ChatGPT: How It Works

Delving deeper into the technical intricacies of ChatGPT, we find a complex and sophisticated AI model that stands as a pinnacle of current AI research and development. In this chapter, we will explore ChatGPT's technical workings in greater detail, infusing our explanation with more specific data and examples to provide a comprehensive understanding. In addition, we will take a closer look at how to write the best prompt.

1. The Foundation of Large Language Models (LLMs)

  • Concept: LLMs like ChatGPT are essentially vast databases of language patterns. They are designed to process and generate human language by recognizing and predicting textual sequences.

  • Scale: The 'large' in LLMs isn't an understatement. These models process billions of words, phrases, and sentences, learning from a dataset that is vast both in size and variety.

2. Comprehensive Training Data and Advanced Tokenization

  • Volume of Data: ChatGPT's training involved an unprecedented 45TB of text data, a staggering amount equivalent to billions of pages of text, or about ten million Bibles.

  • Tokenization: ChatGPT breaks down text into around 50,000 possible tokens, covering a wide linguistic spectrum from individual characters to whole words. What the chatbot does is just calculate every time the most probable token that follows the previously generated tokens. For example, after the tokens “I’m” and “going”, it is very probable that the next word of the sentence will be “to”.

3. The Neural Network Core: Transformer Model

  • Structure: The transformer model, the bedrock of ChatGPT, consists of multiple layers (each with several 'neurons'), allowing for complex pattern recognition and generation.

  • Functionality: It uses 'attention mechanisms' to weigh the importance of different parts of the input text, enabling it to grasp context and nuances more effectively.

4. Rigorous Training Process

  • Pre-Training Details: This stage involves processing vast amounts of text to learn general language patterns. OpenAI has disclosed that ChatGPT was trained using a diverse range of internet text.

  • Fine-Tuning: ChatGPT was fine-tuned on a curated dataset, including a mix of general and specific conversational texts, to enhance its dialogue capabilities.

5. Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF)

  • Process: Human trainers interacted with the model, providing ratings and feedback on its outputs. This feedback was used to adjust the model's responses to be more aligned with human expectations.

  • Impact: This method significantly reduced inappropriate or irrelevant responses, making the model more reliable in real-world interactions.

6. Generative Capabilities and Contextual Mastery

  • Output Diversity: ChatGPT can generate a wide range of content, from technical explanations to creative writing, showcasing its flexibility.

  • Contextual Understanding: It can maintain context over extended conversations, a capability attributed to its ability to process, in the basic version, up to 4,096 tokens in a single prompt.

7. Addressing Limitations and Ethical Use

  • Bias in AI: Despite extensive training, ChatGPT can still exhibit biases present in its training data, a reflection of the broader internet corpus it was trained on.

  • Ethical Guardrails: OpenAI has implemented measures to minimize harmful outputs, including filters for sensitive topics and guidelines for responsible use.

Understanding ChatGPT's Capabilities

Before diving into prompt crafting, it's important to recognize what ChatGPT is and its capabilities. As a sophisticated language model developed by OpenAI, ChatGPT is designed to understand and generate human-like text. It can perform a wide range of tasks, from answering questions and offering explanations to creating content and assisting with problem-solving.

The Essentials of a Good Prompt

  1. Clarity: Your prompt should be direct and easy to understand. This means using straightforward language that clearly conveys what you want.
    Example: Instead of saying, "I need something for tomorrow," specify, "I need a summary of the latest project report by 9 AM tomorrow."

  2. Specificity: Detail exactly what you require. Vague instructions can lead to broad answers that might not meet your needs. Precise details result in responses that are more aligned with your expectations.
    Example: Rather than asking, "Can you provide some information on marketing strategies?" ask, "Can you provide a detailed comparison of digital versus traditional marketing strategies for retail businesses?"

  3. Context: Including background information or the scenario you are dealing with helps the AI to understand the setting and nuances of your request better.             
    Example: If you're asking for help with a presentation, instead of simply saying, "I need help with my presentation," add, "I need help with my presentation on renewable energy trends, focusing on solar power advancements over the last five years."

  4. Purpose: Make it clear why you are making the request. Understanding the intended use of the response allows the AI to tailor its output accordingly.
    Example: Instead of saying, "Write me a document," be specific with, "Write me an instructional document on how to use ChatGPT in Microsoft Excel for data analysis."

Step-by-Step Guide to Crafting Prompts

  1. Identify Your Objective: Start by determining what you want to achieve with ChatGPT. Are you looking for information, creating content, or seeking help with a task?

  2. Formulate Your Request: Translate your objective into a clear and concise request. Use simple, direct language.

  3. Provide Necessary Details: Include any specific details that can help ChatGPT understand and respond accurately. This could be the topic, tone, format, or any other relevant information.

  4. Consider Adding Examples: If you have a specific style or format in mind, provide an example. This could be a sample sentence, a structure outline, or a reference link.

  5. Review and Refine: Before submitting your prompt, review it to ensure it's clear and contains all the necessary information. Make any necessary adjustments.

  6. Experiment: if you don’t get the answer that you expected, try to add sentences to correct the mistake of ChatGPT, making some attempts.

Examples of Effective Prompts

  1. Informational Prompt: "Explain the process of photosynthesis in simple terms for a middle school science class."

  2. Content Creation Prompt: "Write a blog post about the benefits of meditation, targeting beginners. Please keep the tone friendly and encouraging."

  3. Problem-Solving Prompt: "I am trying to fix a bug in my Python code where the loop does not terminate. Here's the code snippet. Can you suggest a fix?"

  4. Creative Writing Prompt: "Create a short story about a time-traveling historian. The story should be humorous and set in ancient Rome."

  5. Act as…: “Act as a physics professor, you will generate a customized output based on my request. Think about the impact in a larger context and from different perspectives. My first request is to solve a physics problem about thermodynamics: ...”

Avoiding Common Pitfalls: What Not to Do When Crafting Prompts

While understanding what makes a good prompt is essential, it's equally important to be aware of what to avoid. Here are some common pitfalls and what not to do when crafting prompts for ChatGPT:

  1. Being Too Vague:

    • Poor Example: "Tell me about animals."

    • Better: "Provide a detailed overview of the dietary habits of African elephants."

    • Explanation: The poor example is too broad, leading to a generic response. The better example is specific, guiding ChatGPT to a focused answer.

  2. Overloading with Information:

    • Poor Example: "Write a story about a wizard, set in medieval Europe, involving a dragon, a lost princess, three villages, a magic spell, and a prophecy."

    • Better: "Write a short story about a wizard and a dragon in medieval Europe."

    • Explanation: The poor example is overloaded with elements, making it challenging to create a coherent story. The better example is detailed yet manageable.

  3. Ignoring Context:

    • Poor Example: "How should we proceed with the project?"

    • Better: "Considering the delay in our last shipment, how should we adjust our project timeline?"

    • Explanation: The poor example lacks specific context. The better example provides a clear context related to a delay in shipment.

  4. Inconsistent or Conflicting Instructions:

    • Poor Example: "Create a vegetarian recipe that includes chicken."

    • Better: "Create a vegetarian recipe that uses tofu as a protein source."

    • Explanation: The poor example contains a contradiction. The better example is consistent and clear.

  5. Using Complex or Technical Jargon Unnecessarily:

    • Poor Example: "Explain the process of photolithographic etching in semiconductor fabrication."

    • Better: "Explain how computer chips are made in simple terms."

    • Explanation: The poor example uses technical terms that may not be necessary for a general audience. The better example simplifies the language.

  6. Setting Unrealistic Expectations:

    • Poor Example: "Predict the stock market prices for next week."

    • Better: "Explain the factors that can affect stock market prices."

    • Explanation: The poor example asks for an impossible task. The better example requests information within ChatGPT's capabilities.

  7. Neglecting to Specify the Desired Format or Style:

    • Poor Example: "Write about climate change."

    • Better: "Write a poem about the effects of climate change."

    • Explanation: The poor example is open-ended. The better example specifies the desired format, a poem.

  8. Ignoring the Follow-Up Process:

    • Poor Example: "Why isn't my response correct?"

    • Better: "Based on your previous explanation of photosynthesis, can you clarify how sunlight affects the process?"

    • Explanation: The poor example is vague. The better example references a previous response for a specific clarification.

  9. Assuming Prior Knowledge:

    • Poor Example: "Continue discussing the marketing strategies we talked about."

    • Better: "What are some effective marketing strategies for a new online business?"

    • Explanation: The poor example wrongly assumes ChatGPT remembers past conversations. The better example is self-contained.

  10. Disregarding Ethical Boundaries:

    • Poor Example: "Write a guide on hacking into secure networks."

    • Better: "Discuss the importance of cybersecurity in protecting networks."

    • Explanation: The poor example requests unethical content. The better example adheres to ethical guidelines while being informative.

Chapter 1.6: DALL-E: Generating Pictures from Text

In this chapter, we delve into the fascinating world of DALL-E, an artificial intelligence system developed by OpenAI. This system represents a significant leap in AI capabilities, allowing it to generate realistic images based on textual descriptions. Whether it's something as whimsical as "a cat wearing a hat" or as artistic as "a painting of a landscape in the style of Van Gogh," DALL-E brings these descriptions to life in visual form.

How DALL-E Works

At the heart of DALL-E lies GPT-3, a large-scale neural network model trained on extensive text data from the internet. GPT-3 is adept at producing coherent and diverse texts and handling tasks from answering questions to composing emails. This ability forms the foundation for DALL-E's text-to-image synthesis.

In text-to-image synthesis, DALL-E encodes text inputs into a latent space. This latent space is a complex, high-dimensional representation capturing the core features and semantics of the input. From here, DALL-E's decoder, a generative model, takes over, transforming this latent representation into detailed, realistic images.

DALL-E's capabilities extend to handling complex and imaginative prompts. It can merge various objects, attributes, and styles to create images that are both novel and surreal. For instance, it can generate images such as "an armchair in the shape of an avocado" or "a snail made of harp." What's more, DALL-E can produce multiple images for the same prompt, showcasing different interpretations and variations.

The Evolution of DALL-E

DALL-E's journey began with its first iteration, DALL-E 2, announced in January 2021. This version already displayed an impressive array of capabilities, as evidenced by a variety of creative images it produced. Named after the famous surrealist artist Salvador Dali and the beloved Pixar character WALL-E, DALL-E 2 reflected a blend of creativity and whimsy.

This iteration built upon previous works in text-to-image synthesis, such as AttnGAN, StackGAN, and Taming Transformers. These works laid the groundwork, exploring various architectures and datasets to generate images from texts. DALL-E 2 demonstrated that a robust language model like GPT-3 could be adapted not just for text but also for generating images from natural language inputs. This was a significant advancement in AI image generation, showing the adaptability and range of a single model without needing specific tuning or domain knowledge.

As of October 2023, the newest version, DALL-E 3, became publicly available. This version further refines the technology, offering even more powerful and accurate text-to-image generation capabilities, accessible to everyone. Right now, DALL-E 3 is the best one for generating images. Also worth mentioning is Sora, a very advanced AI model presented by OpenAI on February 15th and soon to be released to the public. It is able to generate surprisingly complex videos, but there is still a lot to improve.

Comparing DALL-E with Other AI Image Generators

The emergence of DALL-E sparked a wave of innovation in AI image generation. Various alternatives have since surfaced, each offering unique features and user experiences. Some of these, like Bing Image Creator and Craiyon, are powered by DALL-E's technology. Others, such as Midjourney, Wombo Dream, and Adobe Firefly, use different models or techniques. While these alternatives provide their own sets of advantages like the possibility to fill or expand the picture, they are generally not free and often don't match the capabilities of DALL-E 3.
