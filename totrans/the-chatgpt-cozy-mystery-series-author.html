Mystery readers love returning to the same characters they have come to know as real people living in an engaging fictional world.

Agatha Christie fans, for example, still love her books, movies and TV series featuring Hercule Poirot and Miss Marple.

Just think about how much fun you could have writing a series of books with characters you have created, in a story world which could be anywhere you wish.

However, all of the wonderful elements of cozy mysteries that make this genre so attractive to readers, can also create problems if you are writing a series featuring the same main sleuth and sidekicks.

How do you keep the characters and setting fresh and interesting across a series of five or more books?

Should the characters change and grow, and when should you add in new characters into the core group that carries each story?

And what about the setting? Should you move to a different location in each book, or stay in one small local community?

In this guide, I will be exploring how you can develop a compelling cozy mystery series using characters and settings developed in the first book and then extended into a five-book series.

Limitations of AI tools such as ChatGPT

AI tools like ChatGPT have been trained on vast amounts of online data and can recognize patterns in language associated with specific fiction tropes. But they don’t understand the concept of creative author voice, or the subtle personal nuances of cozy mystery relationships and the emotional depth that is implicit in cozy mystery fiction. 

That’s why these tools can never totally replace a human author.

I prefer to think of ChatGPT as a very competent writing assistant who can suggest ideas and work through challenges at every stage of the writing process. You can then decide whether to accept these suggestions or look for better ones.

Will ChatGPT be able to write my entire cozy mystery book for me?

No. At least, not yet. It can handle fact-based nonfiction text much more easily. But as we all know, writing creative fiction is an entirely different challenge. There are also technical limitations about how many words each prompt and output can cope with. That’s why I recommend using the paid version of ChatGPT Plus rather than the free version.

That’s why it can:

Prompts

The questions and instructions you provide to ChatGPT as input are known as “Prompts”- you are prompting the system with the information it needs to search for an answer.

The secret to using ChatGPT for any form of writing, and especially creative writing, is to ask a series of questions using very clear and precise language.

I prefer to interact with ChatGPT as if I am conversing with an intelligent person with a vast amount of knowledge who is learning English as a second or third language.

They have studied hard for years to develop a working vocabulary and understand most of what you say to them.

But when you ask them to discuss concepts and be creative, you have to be:

  1. very clear about precisely what you want them to do and
  2. how you want them to do it.

A poor prompt will lead to a vague, generalized response. A clear, well-defined prompt will give you insightful, precise responses.

NOTE. The prompts used in this guide can be used with any large language model AI system, including Claude, Bard, CoPilot, Sudowrite, Novel Crafter and many others.

THE HIGH CONCEPT STORY IDEA

You may have heard of “high-concept premise” or “elevator pitch.”

This is a one-sentence description of your story idea that encapsulates the entire story in a simple way that is easy for someone to understand and immediately tells the reader why this story is unique, fresh, and intriguing.

It is the magical factor that will make your book irresistible to readers, potential agents, and publishers.

Here are a few examples of well-known high-concept novels:

  • An eleven-year-old boy discovers he's a wizard and that a notorious wizard murdered his parents. (The Harry Potter series)
  • In a dystopian society, teenagers are randomly selected from each district to fight to the death on television. (The Hunger Games series)
  • A bored and miserable meteorologist is stuck living the same day over and over. (Groundhog Day)

So, what makes a book irresistible to cozy mystery readers?

  • What would make you instantly click the buy button or put the print book in your basket in the bookstore?
  • What would get you interested and excited about a book?
  • If you pitch that book to a literary agent or editor at a publishing house, what would make them invest time in reading your book proposal?

So, how do you come up with your magical high-concept story idea?

What exactly is a Story Hook?

For me, a story hook is something that grabs a reader’s attention, immediately creates questions that demand to be answered, and makes someone want to read this book, watch the TV show, or watch the movie.

For me, the hook has to grab me by asking a question that can only be answered by reading the book.

The Four Key Ingredients of a High-Concept Story Idea

What is different about your story idea that makes it sound cool, engaging, and fresh?  Yes, readers are looking for similar elements within a specific niche but are quickly bored with copycat story ideas.

How is this different, intriguing, and new in some way?  Can you create a great high-concept story idea that you can use to sell your entire series in one sentence?

Here are four top components of high-concept, cozy mystery story ideas.

1. A Fish out of Water

This is a very common theme used by many cozy mystery authors. Essentially, it is any scenario where a stranger is dropped into a location or plot scenario where they are totally out of their comfort zone.

Characters who are totally at odds with their environment automatically struggle to adapt to this new location's culture, habits, rules, and nuances.

This creates internal and external conflict and adds exciting layers to the murder mystery investigation. Their fresh approach to solving the crime can be their greatest asset!

In Louisiana Longshot by Jana DeLeon, our sleuth, Fortune Redding, is a CIA assassin forced to go off-grid in Sinful, Louisiana, posing as a former beauty queen turned librarian.

In Coming in Hot by Deany Ray, former motorcycle gang members thirtysomething Piper and her grandmother move south into witness protection in a Florida retirement village.

In the movie Murder Mystery, a New York City cop and his hairdresser wife scramble to solve a murder on a billionaire’s luxury yacht.

In The Psychic Fair Mystery story idea, Ellie has come to visit her aunt at her craft shop and is suddenly gifted with frightening psychic powers. She is totally out of her comfort zone.

Over the course of the series, this person will move from being a stranger to finding their place in the new society, family, or plot situation.

 2. A Major External Conflict

This is an overarching problem, dilemma, or conflict that will not be solved until the end of the series.

Each book in the series will have more minor conflicts that may not be linked to the murder investigation, but the central conflict is in the background.

This hook also generates multiple subplots where this problem threatens the main characters and sidekicks.

This can be very powerful if you link it to the character arc of the sleuth. Introduce it in book one and end it in the final book in the series when the sleuth finally resolves the conflict or problem.

The key is to make this issue very clear in the book description so that the reader knows that this threat or issue is hanging over the characters from the start.

Example. In Louisiana Longshot and Coming in Hot mentioned above, getting involved in a murder investigation could mean blowing their cover, putting themselves and their new friends in mortal danger from the people they are hiding from.

Should they stay under the radar and not get involved in the murder investigation they are caught up in, or find the killer and risk attracting the criminals who are determined to silence them? That question should keep us guessing, while we are enjoying the murder investigation they cannot avoid getting caught up in.

Example. In The Antique Hunter's Guide to Murder by C L Miller, Freya Lockwood and her eccentric Aunt Carole are sucked into a thrilling mystery, as a prominent antique dealer and Freya's estranged mentor leaves her a letter full of clues just before his suspicious death.

Can Freya stay alive long enough to solve the murder? And why was he killed?

Example. In The Psychic Fair Mystery story idea, Ellie is forced to stay with her aunt until she has learned how to control and use her new psychic abilities without putting her aunt at risk. Can her new abilities be kept a secret from the rest of the town and her family? All Ellie wants to do is get back to her normal life!  How she learns and adapts is going to be tracked across the series.

3. An Aspirational or Escapist Hook

Something that plays on the hopes, aspirations and dreams of a reader and whisks them off to an escapist world outside of the normal day to day world.

How many times have you read mysteries where the sleuth inherits a run-down villa/house/bookstore by the sea from someone they barely knew?

Out of the blue, their normal humdrum life is replaced by security, hope, opportunity, and excitement, possibly even a new romance.

Cozy authors make this extra special by adding cool elements such as resident paranormal beings, animal companions and quirky kind local residents.

The reader instantly wants to know what it would be like to live there. What is the weather like? Would they be made to feel welcome? Why has this person left them this legacy?

Another option would be to have the sleuth travel to wonderful locations and help the local community to solve the murder mystery.

Steve Higgs has a very popular series featuring retired police officer Albert Smith and his dog who travel around the UK looking for regional foods – and solving murders.

Hope Callaghan takes us on luxury cruise ships with sleuth Millie Sanders, while TA Williams explores the stunning Italian countryside around Tuscany with retired police detective Dan Armstrong. Both perfect escapist backgrounds for a compelling murder mystery with a twist.

The universal theme is a universal or relatable lesson that the main character must learn by the end of the story.

This doesn't mean that your story has to be moral or boring. It simply means your story has to say something about what it is like to be a human being facing challenges in life.

Your character has to take some form of internal journey and he or she has to learn something along the way.

You have to give us something deeper to hang onto. Something that goes beyond just the chase scenes or murder investigations or missing persons or fantastical worlds. Something that goes beyond the central plot line.

All about the Sleuth

There is one fundamental aspect of the cozy murder mystery genre which explains why readers come back to writers and series they love – it is all about the characters.

Note: For the sake of convenience, I am using an example of a female sleuth throughout this book, but of course the sleuth could be any gender or creature.

I like to think of it this way.

As a reader, when I buy a cozy mystery, I want to feel that I am a tourist taking a vacation in an interesting new town/city/resort for a few days. I have booked a family-run bed and breakfast hotel, and the owners know everyone in the town. I am excited about who I will meet during my short stay and want to learn more about this town and its customs and heritage.

I want to be transported into an interesting story world where I meet warm, charming, and quirky characters I want to spend time with and get to know better. I want to listen to their stories and unique voices and dive deep into their lives.

How do I do that?

By following the journey these characters take when there is a murder in their town, and they are forced to deal with the aftermath of the crime.

One or two main characters will set out to discover who the killer is and bring them to justice before order can be restored to this small community.

In the process of investigating the murder, they reveal their true nature and backstories, making me connect with them at a much deeper level.

When I finally pack my virtual suitcase and head home, I know that I will be coming back and spending more vacations in the company of this group of interesting people who have become more like old friends.

That is why I would always recommend that you plan to write a series of books with the same main characters and setting from the start. Readers come back to this story world to reconnect with these same characters and discover what has changed in their lives.

None of that matters if the reader does not care about the main character and what happens to them.

These are ordinary people dropped into extraordinary situations by circumstances outside their control, and there should be a personal reason why the sleuth is compelled to investigate this murder.

# Does the murder take place in her community or her place of work, for example? She is driven by a sense of justice to discover who committed this terrible crime and why.

A sleuth is often a person who runs a business or has a hobby or skill that brings her into close contact with other members of her community.

No matter the genre, our role as authors is to immerse our characters in the world of the story and then reveal their thoughts and actions when they face challenges and are forced to make difficult decisions.

Readers should feel sympathy and empathy for our main characters and want to follow their journeys.

They have to care whether they will achieve their goals. And that begins with the main characters of our cozy mystery.

The Series Character Arc for Your Sleuth

Mapping the character arc of your sleuth (and cast of sidekicks), over an entire cozy mystery series takes strategic planning.  

The objective is to make the reader eager for the next installment in your sleuth’s personal life, as well as diving into the next murder mystery plot.

This engages the reader’s emotions as they vicariously enjoy the rollercoaster ups and downs of the character's interaction with the mystery they are investigating.

Character Development: Mapping the Journey

Character development is at the heart of any series, providing the emotional core and driving the narrative forward. In a cozy mystery series, the protagonist's growth is particularly crucial as readers invest in their journey from the first page of the first book to the last page of the final installment.

Where is your sleuth starting from?

Introduce your protagonist and key characters with clear, defined traits and motivations. These initial characteristics form the baseline from which their growth will be measured.

Book-by-Book Development: In each book, the protagonist should face challenges that test and reveal their character, leading to personal growth or change. These developments can be tied to the mystery plot, personal relationships, or internal conflicts, providing a clear arc within each book.

In each book, have your protagonist realize something new about themselves to add emotional depth.

How are they going to change over the series?  Overcome flaws?  Level up sleuthing skills? Find love?  Or remain a delightful curmudgeon?

Identify types of small wins for each book followed by a few steps back in others to sustain tension as desires build across the series.

The Overarching Character Arc: Over the course of the series, the protagonist undergoes a more profound and sometimes subtler transformation. This long-term growth reflects their cumulative experiences, lessons learned, and the ways in which the people and events around them have shaped them. By the series' end, they emerge as a markedly different person—more nuanced, mature, or changed in significant ways.

Incremental Growth: The series should depict incremental growth that, while subtle in individual books, results in significant change over time. This could involve the protagonist coming to terms with personal flaws, overcoming fears, or changing their outlook on life.

Cumulative Challenges: Each book’s challenges should contribute to the protagonist’s overarching growth, building on previous developments and setting the stage for future arcs.

Reflecting Change in Relationships: The protagonist’s evolving relationships with recurring characters can serve as a mirror to their growth, highlighting changes in dynamics, power, and mutual understanding. A developing love interest is a familiar subplot that can be tracked across all the series books.

Resolution and Realization: The series climax should resolve the central conflict and catalyze the protagonist’s final transformation, leading to a moment of realization or acceptance that signifies the culmination of their journey.

The protagonist’s journey from the person they are in book one to the person they become in the final book offers a gratifying experience for readers, who invest not just in the mysteries to be solved but in the characters themselves.

In commercial modern genre fiction, such as cozy mysteries, it is unusual to see main characters who do not have transformational character arcs over a series of linked books.

Incorporating character arcs in each book and across your cozy mystery series transforms your sleuth from a mere puzzle-solver to a multi-dimensional character who captures and holds the readers' attention through their actions and decisions.

Readers seem to be more invested in the sleuth’s personal journey when that character faces adversity and develops as a character.

What Is A Continuity Character In Cozy Mystery Fiction?

In cozy mystery fiction, a "continuity character" is a character who consistently appears throughout a series, providing readers with stability and familiarity. Unlike the main characters or the varying cast of suspects, victims, and sidekicks that change from book to book, continuity characters are a constant element.

They help anchor the series, often serving as a touchstone for the main characters and readers. Their presence creates a familiar environment, making readers feel like they are revisiting an old friend or a beloved place with each new book.

Characteristics of continuity characters in cozy mysteries include:

1. Consistent Presence: They appear in almost every book in the series, regardless of the central plot or setting changes.

2. Familiar Role: Their role remains essentially the same throughout the series. They may be a family member, a close friend, a colleague, or a beloved pet.

3. Relationship with the Protagonist: They often have a well-defined relationship with the main character, providing emotional support, humor, or a sounding board for ideas.

4. Limited Development: While they may experience some growth or change, continuity characters often remain relatively static regarding their personality and role. This consistency is key to their function in the series.

5. Contribution to Atmosphere: They contribute significantly to the overall atmosphere and setting of the series, reinforcing the cozy, intimate feel that is a hallmark of the genre.

6. Less Involvement in Central Mystery: Unlike the main characters or the specific sidekicks of each book, continuity characters are generally less involved in the central mystery. They might offer assistance or insights but are usually not key players in solving the case.

In essence, continuity characters in cozy mystery fiction serve as a familiar and comforting element that helps maintain a sense of continuity and community within the series, enhancing the reader's overall experience.

ChatGPT and Subplots

Subplots, essentially mini-stories that run parallel to the main plot, add depth to your novel, enrich the main storyline, and further develop your characters.

They are particularly useful if you write a continuity series of books with the same sleuth and setting.

Building Layers of Conflict into Your Story.

In every cozy murder mystery, there are five main storylines you can use to generate conflict in every scene that will keep readers hooked and turn the page to find out what happens next.

The Main Murder Plot.

The Main A Plot. This is the murder investigation that is specific to this story. Each book in a series will have one or more murders that create a unique murder mystery plot.

In a murder mystery, the overall goal is to identify the killer and bring them to justice. This is the spine and desire line of the book, driving the action forward in every scene.

We know that the murder will be solved by the end of the book, but the goal is to create a clever puzzle and make the reader wonder how the sleuth will find the killer. What skills and techniques will the sleuth use?

What is the personal motivation that compels the sleuth to investigate this murder? The reader has to believe that someone the sleuth knows and cares about is in danger, and the stakes are high. There has to be a link to the “stakes character.”

Then, there are Four Continuity Subplots.

These subplots are all character-driven, which provides layers of emotional depth to your mystery.

In some cozy mysteries, one type may dominate, but if you intend to write a series of linked books with the same sleuth and story world, all four subplots should be built into your story planning.

Subplot One. The Secondary Character Plot

This is usually a character-based subplot about the victim, a close family member, a friend, a sidekick, or a romantic relationship that creates interest, conflict, and emotional depth for the sleuth.

The quirky sidekick, for example, can spark ideas for the sleuth. It could be a pet, ghost, senior relative, etc., who is a great fit for your niche.

They are often humorous and intriguing, and they have their backstory. Each secondary character can become the main character in another book in the series.

It is possible to have a multi-protagonist series where two or more investigators work together to solve crimes, which can work very well if there is one main character for the reader to connect with. Each of the investigators has their own backstory and subplot going on.

Subplot Two. The Community Conflict Plot

Can you think of one or more characters in the local community who conflict with the sleuth for some reason in that story world that could crop up in every book in the series?

This is your continuity subplot, which will run for the entire series of books you write in this story world.

For example, this could be a family member, local police chief, or a disapproving community member. In every book that generates conflict, they will come into contact with the sleuth somehow. You can then build a situation where this antagonistic character has to go to our sleuth for help.

Subplot Three. The Character Arc of the Sleuth

When you introduced your main sleuth in the first chapter of book one, they usually came on stage with a lot of emotional baggage and unfulfilled needs and wants. These are often linked to family, loss, and absent parents or spouses.

Throughout the series of books, you can work on this internal conflict and show how the sleuth grows and “arcs” from book one to the last book in the series.

This is often linked to a romance subplot, where each book in the series continues the “will they/won’t they” get-together story thread, automatically creating emotional tension. The sleuth and the romance interest will have limiting beliefs and internal conflicts that prevent them from being together.

The aim is to have your readers looking forward to the next book in the series so they can find out what happens next in this relationship.

Subplot Four. The Setting and Storyworld

As a reader, I feel as though I am returning to a series to revisit a familiar world. The challenge with mystery series is that new characters must be introduced into your small town or location to keep things fresh while still being grounded in the same location.

Tourists, business contacts, regional fairs, or some other public event or location are all ways for the sleuth to meet new characters, dead or alive.

This is one of the reasons why many cozy mystery sleuths are set in shops, libraries, wedding venues, or work situations where the investigator can come into contact with the general public. A secondary plot linked to your setting adds extra layers to the storyline.

How to Use Murder Mystery Story Structure

How Are Stories Structured, And How Can You Build The Optimal Structure For Your Story?

In this part of the book, I will share some of the deep story structure craft I have learned as a full-time writer. More importantly, I will explain how you can use that structure to take your story from good to great.

The Power of Story Structure

The process is the same whether you use story structure to create an outline for your mystery as part of your pre-writing process or come to it after making your first draft.

You are breaking your story into a pattern that readers of this genre will recognize.

What do I mean by that? Modern readers expect to be entertained.

In a cozy mystery, the author promises the reader that the sleuth will solve a crime by the end of the book.

In the process, you will take your readers on an emotional roller coaster of a journey with the sleuth – and the stages of that journey are very specific to cozy mystery fiction.

So, What Are These Story Structure Patterns?

My goal is to give you a solid framework for any cozy mystery you can adapt, use, take which parts make sense, and then apply it to your outline or draft.

Imagine that you are building a house.

You start at the bottom with the foundations, the groundwork, the floor layout, and the underpinnings of the structure. Then, the building work begins for an office block, residential home, or retail store – whatever your plan dictates.

Story structure is the equivalent foundations that underpin all of your work.

On top of those foundations, you can build any fresh and new story you like, of any length and genre. The foundations of the story stay the same.

The story you build on those foundations is your creative genius - but you need that solid structural framework to support the weight of your story.

Does that make sense? I hope so.

Screenwriting Techniques and Fiction

The good news is that the same story structure techniques screenwriters use can be applied to fiction. And especially commercial genre fiction.

The best model for commercial genre fiction is the Four-Act story structure.

Why? Because the goal of a screenwriter is precisely the same as that of a novelist—to create a compelling emotional journey for the audience/readers, and there can be no better journey than the up-and-down rollercoaster ride of crime fiction.

Murder Mystery Story Structure

For me, the mystery story structure is made up of two layers.

#1. A classical Four Act story plan that provides the backbone to all fiction, irrespective of genre.

#2. A top layer of compulsory scenes and plot events that are essential to every cozy murder mystery.

Like any other fiction genre, cozy mysteries have obligatory scenes and a recognizable story structure. Readers expect to see a traditional pattern of events, clues and reveals, setbacks and dead ends, leading to a climax final confrontation between the sleuth and the real killer.
These chapters are added to the Four-Act framework like muscle and sinew on a skeleton, building up a murder mystery story with power, authenticity, and originality.

That’s why every cozy murder mystery may have the same basic underlying structure, but the outcome is unique and different – because the content of your obligatory scenes is your own.

Classical Four-Act Story Structure

In a properly structured movie or novel, the story consists of Four Acts, defined by nine key plot points. This means that your first draft of the novel needs to have a number of key dramatic twists, sudden obstacles, or reveals, called “turning points.” When the story shifts in some remarkable way or something is revealed, action must be taken, and the stakes increase. Here is a conflict plan for these twists.

Scenes, Sequences, and Four Acts

In classical story structure, scenes are collected into Sequences of scenes with a start, middle, and end for each Sequence.

  • Every scene ends with a turning point that hooks the next scene.

  • Every Sequence ends with a more significant turning point that hooks onto the next Scene Sequence.

  • Sequences of scenes are then collected together to form Acts.

  • Every Act ends with a significant turning point that hooks onto the first scene of the following Sequence of the next Act.

In this way, the story moves on and builds, and instead of a sagging middle, the emotional and story tension builds at a controlled pace that will have the readers turning the page to find out what happens next.

In this way, the story moves on and builds, and each scene, Sequence of scenes, and Act pivots into the next unit of action, and there are no sagging middles.

What’s more, the twist at the end of every scene, scene Sequence, and then Act ensures that the tension and conflict are always building, keeping the story moving and the reader totally hooked on your story. You are totally in control over how you create a page-turning read!

Sequences and Chapters

A Sequence is a collection of scenes divided into chapters. Each sequence builds into an Act in the 4-act Structure. For most genre fiction novels, there could be two or three scenes in a Sequence, and each chapter is a Sequence. 

A chapter in genre fiction can contain one scene or several scenes, depending on their length and the target word count of the complete book. Clearly, the number of scenes in each chapter will depend on the length of your work.

You may not need 24 chapters, or you might need to add more steps, but it is somewhere to start.

For very fast-paced mystery novels, each chapter may only contain one long scene. It is entirely up to you!

I see this plot outline as signposts that take the reader from the start to the end of the murder mystery journey.

Important. My personal story structure map is not a formula or join-the-dots writing but rather a story pattern that I think cozy mystery readers (like me) will recognize and enjoy.

This works for me, but it may not fit your project or writing process well. Go with whatever works for you!

One thing is certain. The plot is built up by the actions that the characters take under pressure.

#Character is revealed by their actions, reactions, and extraordinary decisions in response to challenges introduced by the murder and subsequent investigation.

#In turn, the plot is driven by those reactions and decisions in a series of Scenes and Sequels to those scenes where the decision taken at the end of one scene triggers the response to that action in the opening of the next scene.

Start by building a solid sense of who your main characters are. Then, add a dilemma, challenge, or conflict. Their responses, actions, and decisions will automatically generate your plot. Then, you can start shaping those reactions into the best form possible for this story.

WORKING WITH ChatGPT AND AI

What is ChatGPT?

Artificial intelligence is a broad field of computer science focused on creating systems capable of performing tasks that typically require human intelligence.

A key component of AI is Natural Language Processing (NLP). NLP allows machines to understand, interpret, and generate human language in a way that is meaningful and contextually appropriate.

How ChatGPT Works: Training, Language Model, and Abilities

Large language models like ChatGPT are trained using vast amounts of data. By processing millions of sentences, phrases, and words, the AI model learns a language's structure, grammar rules, common phrases, and facts.

ChatGPT is based on a type of machine learning model known as a transformer neural network, specifically, the Generative Pre-trained Transformer model.

Training ChatGPT involves two stages: pre-training and fine-tuning. The model is exposed to a vast dataset containing parts of the internet during pre-training.

The model doesn't know specifics about which documents were in its training set or have access to any specific documents or sources. Instead, it learns to predict the next word in a sentence based on the words it has seen so far. Then, it is fine-tuned through reinforcement learning from human feedback, focusing it on more specific tasks, like maintaining a coherent conversation or writing a piece of fiction.

The first GPT model, GPT-1, was a proof-of-concept model developed by OpenAI in 2018. GPT-3, unveiled in June 2020, took things a step further with a massive leap in the size of the model and its capabilities. Each iteration improved the language model's ability to generate coherent, contextually relevant sentences and respond effectively to various prompts.

It doesn't understand the text it generates but predicts what comes next based on its training.

ChatGPT: Purpose and Abilities

The primary purpose of ChatGPT is to generate human-like text based on the prompts it's given. ChatGPT can understand and continue a text based on the context provided by the prompt, making it useful for various purposes, from drafting emails to tutoring in various subjects, and writing fiction.

Understanding ChatGPT: Tokens, Memory Limitations, and Evolution from 3.5 to 4

Within a single conversation, ChatGPT's understanding is bound by a window of 'memory' or context length defined by 'tokens.'

A token is a chunk of text that can be as short as one character or as long as one word in English (e.g., 'a,' 'apple'). In other languages, a token might not correspond to a whole word.

The maximum number of tokens that ChatGPT can handle simultaneously – the 'context window' – limits the conversation length.  If a conversation exceeds this limit, the AI loses sight of the earlier tokens, affecting its ability to maintain context and coherence.

ChatGPT-4 has a larger model size, which corresponds to improved fluency, creativity, and comprehension.

This means that ChatGPT-4 can generate higher-quality outputs, particularly when dealing with complex tasks or long conversations.

As we look to the future, continual advancements in AI tools such as ChatGPT will improve their ability to support creative writing projects while still maintaining your unique author voice and writing style.

How to get the most out of ChatGPT

Leveraging the power of ChatGPT for your writing projects can be transformative, but here are a few key guidelines to get the most out of the tool.

An Introduction to Writing with AI

In AI-assisted writing, the author and the AI tool work together to generate an output that meets the author’s needs for that part of the creative writing process.

For example, ChatGPT can generate a character profile for your cozy mystery detective, but it's the human author who provides the criteria, writes the prompts, and then critically evaluates and refines the character profile the tool generates. It's a collaborative process where the AI and the author work with their unique strengths.

Using ChatGPT allows you to focus on those areas of the writing process that are your strengths, while the AI tool works on the other aspects.

This is a very different approach and will need a mindset shift.

As authors, we are accustomed to the solitary nature of writing, including staring at a blank page, crafting text from scratch, and then endlessly revising and editing the manuscript until we're happy with it.

With AI, the solitary nature of writing shifts to a more collaborative one when the "co-writer" is an artificial intelligence. In any form of collaboration, the way we communicate with our colleagues will determine the quality of the response we receive.

1. Understand How Prompts Work

Prompt engineering is the skillful crafting of instructions that guide the AI's outputs. Prompts are crucial in guiding ChatGPT. The more specific and clear your prompts are, the more relevant the output will be.

ChatGPT's interaction model is based on a prompt-and-response system. You, the user, provide a prompt, like the starting sentence of a story or a question, and the model generates a response based on what it has learned during its training. You can then ask for more information.

The way you structure your prompts can significantly influence the type of response you get.

Worked Example of Prompt Structure

What to do: Give me ten story ideas for

The Subject: a fantasy historical cozy mystery story featuring a brilliant amateur detective and an elusive thief who is an elf.

The Context: The story is set in Victorian London.

The Style: Each story idea should be engaging, have a murder to investigate and be attractive to readers of historical fantasy mystery fiction.

If you're plotting a cozy mystery novel, you might begin with a detailed prompt that outlines the main characters, the setting, and the general direction of the plot.

New Ideas: At the initial brainstorming stage, prompts can be questions or open-ended scenarios.

For example, an author might use the prompt, "What could be some unique complications in a love story between a Victorian private detective and an elf living in the London underworld?"

Drafting: Once the basic story framework is in place, prompts can be more directive, outlining a scenario or event in the story. A prompt like, "Describe the first time the detective meets the elf that he has been following through the dark London streets into the forbidden zones," gives the AI clear guidance. Including character names, descriptions, and emotional cues can help produce more nuanced responses.

Revising: In the revision stage, prompts can be used to rework existing content. If a specific dialogue feels stilted, authors can prompt the AI with, "Rewrite the following dialogue in a more casual, playful tone..." Similarly, for improving narrative descriptions, prompts like "Describe the same event from a different character's perspective..." can provide fresh insights.

Polishing: Finally, during the polishing stage, authors can use prompts to refine the language, ensuring the text is engaging and grammatically correct. A prompt could be, "Proofread the following paragraph for grammar and style," or "Rewrite this passage using more vivid, sensory language..."

2. Try Role-Playing for Dialogue

A role-playing approach can be effective in creating dialogue. Define the role of the AI, for instance, as a character in your novel, and engage in a conversation as if you were another character.

Let’s say that you are creating a dialogue between your Victorian amateur detective and a police officer who is investigating a murder at a grand ball. You could assign roles like this: "You are a brilliant Victorian aristocratic amateur sleuth trying to calm down a demanding police officer sent to investigate a murder and the theft of a very special moonstone.”

You can also ask the AI to interview one or more characters as though they were living people, asking about their lives and personality.

3. Review and Iterate

ChatGPT’s responses are not always perfect. If the output isn't what you expected, you may need to rephrase your prompt or request a regeneration. Experimenting with different prompts and learning from these trials can lead to better results.

For instance, if a prompt "Describe a small coastal town" generates a response that's too generic, you might adjust it to "Describe a small coastal town on an evening in summer when the tourists have left the harbor for the residents to enjoy. Provide sensory details of the scene from the viewpoint of a barista who enjoys walking along the harbor in the evening with her rescue dog and watching the fishing boats."

By incorporating these strategies and approaches into your use of ChatGPT, you'll be better equipped to create engaging and compelling content. The more you use and experiment with this technology, the more effective you'll become.

ChatGPT in Creative Writing: Strengths and Limitations

The strengths of ChatGPT in creative writing lie in its ability to generate diverse and creative narratives, produce large amounts of text quickly, and provide unique perspectives that inspire new ideas. It can help overcome writer's block, increase productivity, and be a valuable tool for brainstorming and drafting.

However, these strengths come with limitations.

ChatGPT's text generation is based on patterns it learned during training, which means it can occasionally produce inappropriate or nonsensical outputs. 

It can sometimes be overly verbose and may not always maintain perfect consistency, especially in longer narratives. Furthermore, while it can follow the instructions in a prompt, it doesn't understand the story like a human would.

As we navigate this brave new world of AI-assisted writing, remember that tools like ChatGPT are just that. Tools.

This is a tool to help you brainstorm ideas, spark new ones, and then shape YOUR version of those ideas, tropes, themes, characters, and settings into a working outline of your cozy mystery faster than you thought possible.

Challenges using ChatGPT

In this chapter, I want to discuss some ethical and legal aspects of using ChatGPT, or any other form of AI, to support your creative writing.

OWNERSHIP AND Copyright challenges

You have just seen, and I hope you tested out for yourself, how ChatGPT can write the text of a working draft of your cozy mystery novel using the prompts you provided.

The challenge is this: You may have provided unique prompts based on your ideas and the ideas generated by ChatGPT, but the AI has written the text for that scene or chapter.

Who owns that piece of writing?

As you can imagine, this is a complex and rapidly evolving area of intellectual property law. You should understand the current situation but be aware that these issues are being examined globally.

Please note that I am not a lawyer, and this section should not be considered legal advice for your country and situation, but I have researched this topic for my work.

My understanding at this time is that the copyright laws in place are designed to protect any work that human authors have created.

Due to their creative involvement, the author who created the prompts and then revised, edited, and shaped the output generated by ChatGPT holds the copyright for the final text.

In other words, they took the AI's ideas and used them as inspiration to create a final original work that is substantially different from the response ChatGPT generated.

AI did not write the text. The author used AI as a writing assistant.

This means that:

#1. The prompts you input into ChatGPT to generate prose must be your specific and original ideas, even if they were inspired by ideas suggested by ChatGPT, and don’t infringe on the intellectual property of other authors.

I have seen videos on YouTube that suggest that you prompt ChatGPT to rewrite the text of your scenes in the style of the bestselling authors in your niche, so your book is as good as the competition.

Please do not do that. Plagiarism is real. Although some writers do this as a writing exercise to improve their skills and learn their craft, I still would not do it.

Intellectual Property lawyers will make a lot of money in AI in the coming years because authors' copyrighted works have been used to train large language models.

ChatGPT has been trained using an enormous range of online material and may have “learned” the style of popular books in your niche as part of learning how language patterns are constructed. It does not hold or recall specific sources or documents. It generates responses based on predicting what should come next in a piece of text based on the patterns and structures it has learned during training.

The chance of an exact sentence or turn of phrase being precisely the same as the text in an existing work of fiction is very remote, but the last thing you want is to be accused of plagiarism of another author’s work.

#2. Do not use copyrighted characters, settings, or plot elements.

I recommend that you don’t use copyrighted characters, trade names, settings, or plot elements in any of your prompts. It is easy to ask ChatGPT to create a plot outline for a seven-book YA Fantasy series set in a fantasy school for witchcraft and wizardry, featuring a young orphan hero like the one created by J.K. Rowling.

Please do not do that. You have plenty of ideas of your own.

If you are concerned about plagiarism, use a plagiarism detection tool before publishing. A quick internet search will show you what is available in both free and paid options.

The more specific and unique your prompts, the less likely the output will be similar to published books.

Review what has been generated, then change the prompts to include your ideas for characters, settings, and hooks, for example, and then regenerate responses until you get a unique result.

#3. Do not simply copy and paste the prose generated by ChatGPT and use this directly in your manuscript. AI Detection is rapidly evolving.

There may be “writers” who would rather not take the time to study story craft and are more interested in creating a 50,000-word cozy mystery novel in a day without any creative or human input whatsoever. Yes, I have shown you how you can do that. But that is their choice. 

Please don’t do that.

Not only will the quality be questionable, but many publishers are checking manuscripts with AI detection systems and may reject books that they consider are not the result of a substantive amount of human creativity.

If you are concerned, a range of free and paid AI detection software is available, and the list is growing rapidly. I would encourage you to research and evaluate the free options.

#4. Value your creative talent.

Currently, ChatGPT generates creative writing ideas faster than I can type, and ChatGPT-Plus can manage entire chapters of text.

Use the tool the way it was intended to be used.

To rapidly triage a range of ideas for plot threads and characters, for example, and generate a draft outline of your cozy mystery manuscript faster than you thought possible.

Then, you can put on your “cozy mystery writer hat” and deepen the character arc, building layers of conflict into the plot, and having fun with dialogue and luscious settings.

Creative writing and storytelling are very human activities.

No AI system, no matter how sophisticated and trained, will understand the complexity and subtle nuances of the authentic human emotional conflict implicit in a romance or mystery novel.

Just like any scrappy first draft, you should expect to take the ideas that emerge and rework them, rewrite and reshape them, and then revise the text to add your personal style, creative flair, and original voice.

This is when your unique talent can shine!
