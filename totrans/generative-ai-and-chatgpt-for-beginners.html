Generative Artificial Intelligence (or GenAI for short) is a special form of artificial intelligence that’s capable of generating various types of content, such as text, images, videos, and code.

Artificial Intelligence has long been a hot technology topic, and it has been applied in various sectors and industries for the last few decades.

For instance, Netflix uses AI to recommend shows to users based on their viewing history. Similarly, Tesla and Google have leveraged AI in the development of self-driving technology.

But Generative AI is the first kind of AI that’s available for everyone. This technology came to the attention of the general public on November 30 of 2022, when OpenAI released ChatGPT and quickly gained 1 million users in just 5 days:

The answers provided by Perplexity contain citations to the original website used as the source of those answers, allowing users to explore or verify the given information.

A Quick History of Generative AI

AI is developing at an unprecedented rate, and to appreciate how we got to where we are now, it’s important to know how it all began. AI has a rich history that dates back to the 1940s, with notable progress occurring in almost every decade.

This section will look over some of the significant events that strongly influenced the growth of AI. Some of the technology terms below may be unfamiliar to you, but don’t worry. They will be explained further in the next chapter.

The history of Generative AI can be traced back to 1943, when Warren McCulloch and Walter Pitts developed the first neural network model.

A neural network is a computer system modeled after the human brain. This system is capable of processing large amounts of data through interconnected nodes (also called neurons) which work together to recognize patterns and make decisions based on input data.

Neural networks excel in tasks such as image and speech recognition, natural language processing, and even playing complex games.

Then in 1959, the computer scientist Arthur Samuel popularized the term "machine learning" and developed the first machine learning algorithm that can learn how to play checkers, a board game similar to chess.

Then in 1960s, MIT Professor Joseph Weizenbaum created the first chatbot called ELIZA.

ELIZA is an experimental program created to simulate conversations with a psychotherapist. It would respond to prompts using natural language that sounds empathic.

The groundwork for computer vision was also done during this time, when researchers began to develop the human face recognition and identification program.

In 1970s, the artificial intelligence project ground to a halt because investors are disappointed with the lack of progress in the field.

Nevertheless, the chatbot programs were picked up by individual companies because they can serve specific use cases, such as answering common questions and relaying complex situations to the support agent in charge.

While chatbots are unable to carry on intelligent conversations, they still save time and reduce expenses, and business leaders love that.

The development of machine learning algorithms continued in individual companies interested in finding new ways to cut costs and increase productivity.

As the technology advanced, neural networks gave rise to deep learning. In 1980, computer scientist Kunihiko Fukushima developed the first deep learning system called Neocognitron, which utilizes multiple layers of neurons to achieve even greater levels of accuracy and performance.

Deep learning paved the way for significant breakthroughs in AI, transforming industries and leading to the resurgence of interest and investment in artificial intelligence in the decades that followed.

Fast forward to 1990s, governments and companies began to look at artificial intelligence again and were surprised to see the technology has improved significantly while out of sight.

In 2009, AI researcher Fei-Fei Li published ImageNet, a large image database used to train computers to recognize objects in images. The dataset enabled rapid advances in computer vision in the 2010s.

Next, the Generative Adversarial Network (GAN) was developed by Ian Goodfellow in 2014. The architecture pits two neural networks against each other to generate more accurate and realistic results.

Then in 2017, researchers at Google introduced a new neural network model called transformer. This new model can learn context by tracking relationships in sequential data (like words in a sentence).

For example, when you ask, "What color is the sky?" The transformer model is able to identify and associate the words "sky", "color", and "blue" as relevant based on its training data.

The model uses this knowledge to form a response: "The sky is blue."

The transformer model was then implemented by OpenAI, the company behind ChatGPT, to create the GPT large language models (LLMs).

GPT itself stands for Generative Pre-trained Transformer, and the name comes from the transformer model.

In 2022, OpenAI decided to release ChatGPT, which was powered by GPT-3, and the rest is history.

As you can see, Generative AI has come a long way, and progress was very slow at first. However, recent advancements have accelerated its development and adoption.

With the release of models like GPT-3 and beyond, AI has achieved excellent capabilities in natural language understanding and generation.

Chapter 2: Understanding Key Technologies Behind Generative AI

To really understand how Generative AI works, we need to take a step back and learn the technologies that power the application.

We’re going to start with what artificial intelligence is and how machine learning works. Then, we’ll delve into the intricacies of neural networks and deep learning.

From there, we’ll explore transformers, a breakthrough architecture that has revolutionized the field. Finally, we’ll discuss large language models, which are the advanced systems that bring Generative AI to life.

Don’t be afraid of all these fancy terms because I will introduce them in the most friendly way possible. Let’s jump in.

Artificial Intelligence

Simply put, artificial intelligence (or AI) is a field that focuses on creating systems capable of performing tasks that normally require human intelligence.

These tasks include reasoning, learning, problem-solving, perception, language understanding, and decision-making. For example, you can create AI systems that detect suspicious transactions from financial data, or predict property prices based on available price data.

AI systems can be designed to perform specific tasks, or they can aim to replicate human intelligence more broadly.

The fields of study that make AI viable today are called machine learning and deep learning, which are crucial components in developing intelligent systems capable of adapting and improving over time:

As you can see above, Generative AI is a very specific implementation of AI. It is only a small portion of a greater whole.

Machine Learning

As the name implies, machine learning is a subdivision of AI study that focuses on the development of algorithms that enable computers to learn and make predictions or decisions based on available data.

How do machines learn? In the same way humans can learn to differentiate things, which is through communication, pattern recognition, and interaction with their environment.

For example, to teach a baby about orange, we would show the baby an image of the fruit and communicate its name verbally:

You can test if the baby is able to recognize an orange by putting it in a basket of fruits. If the baby can identify the fruit correctly, then the baby understands what an orange is:

Similarly, we can teach a machine to recognize an object by showing it again and again. We show what an orange looks like so the machine can identify the colors, the properties, and everything else that makes an orange, orange:

Keep in mind that a machine learns far more slower when compared to a baby. It needs lots and lots of data for training until it can be intelligent enough. The ChatGPT we have today was trained on millions of text documents, almost the size of the internet itself.

Machine learning techniques are categorized into three modes of learning:

  1. Supervised learning - The model is trained on labeled data (show and describe images)

  2. Unsupervised learning - The model identifies patterns in unlabeled data (just show images)

  3. Reinforcement learning - The model learns by interacting with its environment and receiving feedback (trial and error)

Unlike traditional programming, where explicit instructions are provided, machine learning systems improve their performance by identifying patterns and relationships within large datasets.

This process involves training models on historical data, allowing them to generalize and apply their knowledge to new, unseen data.

Neural Networks

A Neural network is a type of machine learning model inspired by the human brain’s network of neurons. They consist of layers of interconnected nodes, or neurons, which work together to process input data and produce output.

A typical neural network has an input layer, one or more hidden layers, and an output layer:

The input layer receives the raw data, the hidden layers transform and process this data through a series of weighted connections, and the output layer produces the final result.

Neural networks can learn through trial and error by comparing the output to the expected result.

The model is fundamental to many advanced AI applications, including image and speech recognition, language translation, and document summarization. Their ability to learn complex patterns and representations from data makes them indispensable in creating powerful AI systems.

Deep Learning

Deep learning is a subdivision of machine learning that emerged in an attempt to make machine learning more accurate and efficient.

The main difference between deep learning and machine learning is the structure of the neural network models used by the computer.

In deep learning, the neural networks typically have three or more (up to a thousand) hidden layers:

These advanced neural networks are designed to mimic the human brain’s structure and function, enabling computers to learn and make decisions in a way that is more similar to human thought processes.

Deep learning models excel at handling large and complex datasets, making them particularly effective for tasks such as image and speech recognition.

By using multiple layers of interconnected nodes, or neurons, deep learning models can automatically discover intricate patterns and representations in the data, enabling them to achieve high levels of accuracy in various use cases.

Transformer Model

The transformer model is a type of neural network that learns context and meaning by tracking relationships in a sequence of data, like the influence of a word in a sentence.

This ability is enabled because of the self-attention mechanism introduced in transformer models. When the model pays attention to one word, it looks to the surrounding words to get the context of that word.

We’ve seen an example before that when asked with the question "What color is the sky?", the transformer model is able to identify and associate the words "sky", "color", and "blue" as relevant based on its training data.

The following image shows a simplified transformer model process:

After receiving input from the user, the model transforms this input into vector embeddings, which are numerical representations of the input data. These vector embeddings capture the meaning and relationships of the words in a way that can be processed by computer models.

The neural network model then searches through its training data for embeddings that are similar to the input. Based on these similarities, the model generates a response, which is then decoded into natural language that we can understand.

There are additional technical details in the transformer model, but they are not essential to understand unless you wish to explore the topic further.

Large Language Model (LLM)

Large Language Model is the engine that drives the Generative AI systems we have today. The model was built upon the transformer model and trained on vast amounts of text data.

You can think of a Large Language Model like a digital tree that grows when fed by data. The branches of the tree will extend with each new piece of information, creating a large and complex network of knowledge.

When you send an instruction, the tree will search through its branches of knowledge for the matching information required to carry out that instruction.

Because of the transformer architecture, LLM excels at understanding and generating human-like text. It can predict the next word in a sentence based on the context formed by the previous words.

This ability to analyze context enables them to perform a wide range of language-related tasks, from composing essays and writing code to engaging in conversation and answering questions.

Large language models are trained on a diverse and extensive corpus of text data to learn general language patterns, and then fine-tuned to enhance their performance in particular domains. This two-step process allows these models to adapt to various applications with remarkable accuracy and coherence.

As of this writing, LLM is the core technology behind Generative AI tools such as ChatGPT, ChatPDF, Claude, and many others.

It is expected that other fields requiring a deep understanding of human language will harness this technology in the future.

Summary

In this chapter, we just went through the most important concepts that make up the foundation of Generative AI.

While new models might be released tomorrow, the fundamentals of Generative AI will remain the same unless we have another research breakthrough.

By understanding the fundamental technologies behind Generative AI, you can dispel common misconceptions around AI, such as AI can fully understand and comprehend context in the same way humans do, or it becoming sentient.

Generative AI is a very specific implementation of artificial intelligence that leverages the power of large language model, which uses numbers, statistics and similarities to create an accurate response. The chance of it being sentient is virtually nonexistent.

Limitations of ChatGPT

While ChatGPT offers many advantages, you need to be aware of its limitations so that you can use the tool effectively and responsibly.

Here are three main limitations of ChatGPT:

Might Generate Incorrect Information

One significant limitation of ChatGPT is its potential to generate incorrect or misleading information.

Although the model is trained on a vast amount of data, it doesn’t have real-time access to verify facts and can sometimes produce responses that are inaccurate or outdated.

This can be particularly problematic in situations where precise and reliable information is crucial. Users should always cross-check critical information from authoritative sources to ensure its accuracy.

Might Generate Harmful or Biased Answers

ChatGPT training data includes a wide range of human text, which can contain biases and potentially harmful content. Although safeguards and filters are in place, the AI may still produce responses that reflect these biases or are otherwise inappropriate. Despite efforts to minimize such occurrences, ChatGPT might occasionally generate harmful or biased answers.

It’s essential for users to approach the AI’s responses critically and to be aware of the potential for such issues. OpenAI continues to work on improving these aspects to make AI safer and unbiased.

Also note that ChatGPT is not a replacement for professional services, such as therapists, coaches, or legal advisors, so please do not attempt to use the tool to supplant these services.

Always consult a qualified professional for advice tailored to your specific circumstances.

Knowledge Cutoff

ChatGPT training data only includes information available up to a certain date, so the AI system has no awareness of new events, developments, or trends after the date.

When ChatGPT was released, its cutoff date was September 2021. This cutoff date was continually updated as the development of ChatGPT continues.

Chapter 4: Prompt Engineering For Non-Technical People

In the context of Generative AI, a prompt is simply a question, instruction, or input that you provide to an AI system so that you can receive a particular response back.

This response can be whatever the AI system is trained to produce. For ChatGPT, it’s likely a text of information, an article, or a piece of code. For another AI system trained on images, it might generate new images or edit existing ones.

When you test ChatGPT in the previous chapter, you’ve already learned how to give a prompt to an AI system.

Prompt engineering is a new field of study for writing and optimizing prompts so that humans can get an accurate and relevant response from Generative AI.

I found that there are 4 strategies that can help me get the desired results. They are:

  1. Writing clear instructions

  2. Providing reference

  3. Breaking a big task into smaller tasks

  4. Giving specific role

Let’s explore these 4 options next.

Write Clear Instructions

ChatGPT and other Generative AI technologies are not able to read your mind or know who you are.

This means you need to provide explicit and detailed instructions to ensure the AI understands your request accurately. Here are some tips to help you write clear instructions for Generative AI:

Include Specific Details

Generic prompts result in generic answers. If you want to get a highly relevant answer, you need to provide any important details in the prompt.

For example, if your prompt is "Tell me about dogs," then ChatGPT will answer with generic information about dogs. If you only want information about a specific breed of dogs, so you could say "Tell me about the different breeds of Poodles and their characteristics."

Use Simple Language

The simpler the language you use, the easier it will be for ChatGPT to understand and process your request.

For instance, "Explain how photosynthesis works" is more straightforward than "Provide an in-depth analysis of the photosynthetic process."

Iterate and Refine

Because AI remembers past conversations, you can iterate and refine your instructions to help AI understand the request. Prompt engineering is a mix of art and science, and sometimes you might need several attempts before you get the response you want.

Providing feedback or additional clarification can help ChatGPT generate a more accurate response on subsequent attempts.

Specify the Output Length and Format

When you see the output is longer than you expected, you can ask the AI for a shorter output. For example:

Summarize the text delimited by triple quotes below in about 300 words.

"""insert text here"""

If you want bullet points instead of paragraphs, then say so in the prompt as well:

Summarize the text delimited by triple quotes below as 3 bullet points.

"""insert text here"""

Now the summary will be formatted as bullet points instead of paragraphs.

Provide Reference

If you want ChatGPT to answer from a reference text, you can say so in the prompt.

For example, send the prompt below after you upload a PDF file:

Use the provided document to answer the following question.
If the answer cannot be found in the articles, write "I could not find an answer."

Question: <insert question here>

This way, ChatGPT will not try to come up with an answer derived from its training data. It will only refer to your PDF document for the answer.

If you want ChatGPT to also cite the source, you can further improve the prompt as follows:

Use the provided document to answer the following question.
If the answer cannot be found in the articles, write "I could not find an answer."
If an answer to the question is provided, it must be annotated with a citation.
Use the following format for the citation:
({"citation": <passages from the document>}).

Question: <insert question here>

Now ChatGPT will tell you which passage it refers to for the answer.

Break a Big Task Into Smaller Tasks

One effective prompt engineering strategy involves breaking down a complex task into smaller, more manageable prompts to enhance the clarity and precision of the responses.

This approach allows the AI to focus on one aspect of the problem at a time, leading to more detailed and accurate outputs.

This strategy is best suited when you need the AI to perform complex reasoning, multi-step calculations, or provide detailed explanations.

To show you an example, the following prompt can be considered a big task:

Describe 5 challenges in implementing AI effectively and suggest potential solutions to these challenges

While the AI can handle the task just fine, you can improve the quality of the response by splitting the prompt in two.

First, you can ask for the challenges:

Describe 5 challenges in implementing AI effectively

This helps the AI to concentrate on identifying and elaborating on the challenges without the added complexity of immediately proposing solutions.

Once the challenges are clearly outlined, you can then follow up with a related prompt:

Now suggest potential solutions to these challenges

By taking the previously established context into account, the AI can now shift its focus to problem-solving.

This sequential prompting not only enhances the quality of the generated content but also mirrors a logical, step-by-step approach that humans often use when tackling complex issues.

By building context incrementally, the AI is better equipped to deliver in-depth and thoughtful responses.

This method can also reduce the risk of incomplete or superficial answers, as the AI is not overwhelmed by trying to address multiple aspects of a complex idea simultaneously.

Giving Specific Role

Another way to improve Generative AI results is by telling it to respond from the perspective of a specific role.

Role-playing prompts instruct the AI system to adopt a specific role, similar to an actor in a movie or play. By assigning a role, users can guide the AI’s responses to be more aligned with the desired tone, expertise, and context.

This technique can enhance the quality and relevancy of the output, making it more useful and effective for the intended audience.

For example, suppose you are creating an AI support agent for a technology company. You can instruct the AI to play the role of a support agent as follows:

You are a helpful and empathetic support agent for a technology company. Your mission is to provide a supportive and understanding response to the customer's questions.

If you don't know the answer, gently ask if they would like to be referred to a human assistant.

To effectively use role-playing prompts, it’s important to clearly define the role and its characteristics. In the example above, a restriction is added to the AI system, instructing it to direct the customer to a real human agent when it is unable to assist.

Specific details about the role’s expertise, style of communication, and the context in which it operates can help the AI generate more precise responses.

Instead of simply asking the AI to respond as a scientist, specifying a particular field of science, such as a marine biologist or an astrophysicist, can lead to more targeted and accurate answers.

Additionally, role-playing prompts can be layered to create more nuanced interactions. Combining multiple roles within a single prompt can help simulate complex scenarios.

For example, an AI can be asked to respond as a financial advisor who also has a background in psychology, offering financial advice that takes into account emotional and psychological factors:

You are a financial advisor with a background in psychology. You offer advice that addresses both the financial aspects and the psychological impact of market volatility on the client.
You communicate in a professional, empathetic manner, prioritizing the client's well-being ahead of maximizing returns.

However, keep in mind that the AI’s responses should not be taken as professional advice. No matter how good the result gets, AI is just giving you what it figured out from its training data.

As always, use the AI’s responses at your own discretion and risk.

In this chapter, we have explored what prompt engineering is and how you can improve the AI response by giving it a better prompt.

Generic prompts + AI = Generic results

Specific prompts + AI = Specific results

The four strategies explained here will help you to be more specific when giving instructions to AI.

Refine your prompt if the result does not meet your expectations. Eventually, you will intuitively understand how to achieve the desired responses. Just have a little patience when working with an AI system.

Chapter 8: Ethical Considerations and Responsible Use of AI

The rapid development and deployment of AI present many opportunities for innovation and efficiency.

Bias and Fairness in AI

Bias and fairness in AI are critical issues that have garnered significant attention in recent years.

This issue is particularly concerning in areas such as hiring, finance, and healthcare, where biased AI decisions can significantly impact people’s lives.

Artificial Intelligence systems often reflect the biases present in the data they are trained on, which can lead to unfair and discriminatory outcomes.

Bias in AI can originate from several sources, such as:

​▪​

Human input that may contain subjective biases

​▪​

AI algorithmic design that accidentally favors particular groups

​▪​

Historical data that reflects existing prejudices

To give an example, suppose you are training an AI system for hiring, and you give a dataset that consists of:

​▪​

100 male resumes

​▪​

50 female resumes

This dataset is unbalanced because it has more male resumes compared to female resumes, which can cause the AI system being trained to favor male candidates over female candidates.

The AI system may learn to associate certain characteristics, such as male names or male-dominated job titles, with a higher likelihood of success or better qualifications. The bias might also exclude disabled people or certain minority groups.

As a result, The AI system may recommend more male candidates for interviews, leading to a lack of diversity and potential loss of good candidates in the hiring process.

To resolve this issue, you need to make sure that the training data used to train the AI system is well balanced. If you have 100 ideal male resumes, then make sure you also have an equal number of ideal female resumes.

Another example of AI bias might occur in the financial sector, where biased credit scoring algorithms can deny loans to deserving individuals based on race or gender.

How to Mitigate AI Bias

Here are several strategies that can help address the bias in an AI system:

​▪​

Make sure that you have good training data.

​▪​

Assign trusted individuals to look after the system.

​▪​

Develop a fairness-aware algorithm

​▪​

Continuous monitoring

The first step is to ensure that you have diverse training data that are representative of the population or use cases the AI system will encounter.

After the dataset is ready, it is important to assign dependable people to supervise and guarantee that the AI system isn’t making biased judgments.

Additionally, fairness-aware algorithms can be designed to minimize bias and promote equitable outcomes. Continuous monitoring and auditing of AI systems are also essential to identify and rectify biases that may emerge over time.

Data Privacy and Security

AI systems often rely on vast amounts of training data, which raises serious security and privacy concerns.

Back to the previous example, training an AI system to help in the hiring process requires real resume data.

AI systems scan resumes using algorithms to find keywords, experiences, and talents that match the job description. You need to feed the AI system with a large collection of resumes, training it to recognize relevant information and make reliable assessments.

The resumes contain sensitive personal information such as names, contact details, addresses, educational backgrounds, and work histories. Unauthorized access to this information can lead to identity theft, fraud, and other malicious activities.

You need to ensure that the information remains confidential and is accessed only by authorized personnel.

How to Improve Data Privacy and Security

To ensure that data privacy and security are improved, it is essential to:

​▪​

Obtain consent from people

​▪​

Comply with data protection regulations such as GDPR and CCPA

​▪​

Anonymize data when possible

​▪​

Regularly audit data usage

When collecting data for AI training, you need to make sure that you get explicit consent from people before collecting, storing, or using their data. You also need to explain how their information will be used.

You also need to comply with data protection regulations such as the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) in the European Union and the California Consumer Privacy Act (CCPA) in the United States.

When possible, you need to protect and anonymize personally identifiable information (PII) to prevent unauthorized access or misuse. For example, OpenAI removes any PII from the data they want to use to improve ChatGPT.

Finally, you need to regularly audit data usage and storage practices to identify potential vulnerabilities, ensure ongoing compliance with privacy regulations, and make necessary adjustments to maintain robust security measures.

Malicious Actors

As Generative AI continues to expand, the potential for its misuse becomes a growing concern for everyone.

To protect human society, addressing the risks associated with bad AI users is very important. Here are some ways AI can be used for malicious intent:

​▪​

Phishing scams

​▪​

Impersonation

​▪​

Doxxing

Phishing is a form of social engineering with the intention of deceiving people into revealing sensitive information. Leveraging the power of Generative AI, criminals can organize highly targeted phishing messages that are tailored to specific recipients.

With proper grammar and authoritative sounding campaigns, a phishing attack that uses Generative AI boosts the probability of successful deception and data theft

Additionally, malicious actors can also use AI to clone voice and create videos to impersonate friends and family members.

In February 4 2024, an employee lost $25 million to fraudsters[5] because of a deepfake video impersonating the Chief Financial Officer. The worker attended a video call meeting with the CFO and several staff members, but it turns out everyone there was not real except him.

Another way AI can be used maliciously is through doxxing, which is the act of disclosing personally identifiable information, such as the phone number or address online (in social media or forums)

Large Language Models that power AI tools like ChatGPT are trained on vast amounts of internet data, which makes them able to interpret the location of an image. This means bad actors can accurately predict where you live based on a single photo you shared on social media.

How to Detect and Prevent Malicious AI Use

Detecting and preventing malicious AI use requires a multifaceted approach. The first and most important step is to increase public awareness and educate individuals about the risks and how to protect themselves.

Everyone should be educated on the best practices for protecting their personal information, such as adding the Multi-factor authentication process to safeguard their accounts.

AI-based tools can be developed to identify and counteract malicious activities. For example, Sentinel AI is a technology company that uses machine learning algorithms to detect and combat fake information[6].

Governments and international organizations also need to establish strict regulations to provide guidelines and enforce accountability for AI use.

Summary

In the following years, the use of Generative AI will only expand to more aspects of our lives, from healthcare and finance to marketing and human resources management.

As with any powerful technology, keeping Generative AI under control is necessary to create a safe future for everyone.

To ensure that AI aligns with human values and social norms, we can begin by developing a good strategy for using AI ethically and responsibly.

Chapter 9: Future Trends - AI Development and Job Market Impact

It is expected that Generative AI will continue to improve and be integrated into various aspects of our lives. The technology definitely will change industries and reshape the job market.

Will AI take your job? What new opportunities will emerge? This chapter will explore these critical questions and give some realistic predictions.

Will AI Take Your Job?

A general concern regarding AI is its potential to replace human jobs for efficiency reasons. As AI becomes more advanced, people fear that it could make a significant portion of the workforce obsolete, particularly in industries reliant on repetitive tasks.

This shift could lead to widespread unemployment and economic instability, further widening the social gap between the rich and the poor.

While it is true that AI can automate repetitive and mundane tasks, the reality is that professionals with deep-level expertise stand to gain the most from Generative AI.

This is because Generative AI relieves workers from handling essential basic tasks, giving them more time to concentrate on strategic decision-making and critical responsibilities.

For example, in healthcare, AI can assist doctors by quickly analyzing medical images, helping doctors to detect the disease faster and leaving more time to focus on diagnosis and patient care.

In finance, AI tools can handle data-heavy tasks such as fraud detection and risk assessment, but analysts are still needed to verify and make decisions based on the data given by AI.

After exploring the previous chapters, you may come to understand that Generative AI like ChatGPT is merely a tool. It represents the most extensive repository of knowledge ever created by humans.

However, without knowing how to use it effectively, you might perceive it as just a slightly more intelligent chatbot.

When using ChatGPT, a good prompt produces a good result, and your skill level determines how effective you are with the tool.

This also means that someone who has never worked as a software developer, marketer, or doctor cannot suddenly use ChatGPT to perform the duties of these professions.

Rather than replacing jobs, AI simply augments human capabilities, making professionals more efficient and effective.

New Opportunities Will Emerge

Supporting the point that AI does not necessarily eliminate jobs, it is essential to recognize that while some jobs may become obsolete, new opportunities will emerge.

The digital revolution of the 2000s is a prime example, where the rise of the internet led to the decline of traditional roles in printed newspaper and encyclopedia businesses, but created new professions such as web designers, software developers, and digital marketers.

In the same manner, the propagation of AI tools is set to generate a new wave of job opportunities. New roles like AI ethics and safety manager, data scientist, and machine learning engineer will see more demand in the future.

Moreover, roles focused on integrating AI into various sectors, managing AI systems, and ensuring their ethical use will become increasingly vital.

Industries will also see the creation of entirely new job categories. For instance, the entertainment industry is exploring AI for content generation, leading to roles in supervising AI-created content and developing interactive experiences.

In education, AI tutors and personalized learning plan designers are emerging as AI tailors education to individual student needs.

More Wealth For Everyone

The purpose of tools like ChatGPT and other AI technologies is to drive innovation, enhance productivity, and create new business opportunities across various industries. This means there will be more wealth created for everyone.

Lane Wagner, a software developer and founder of Boot.dev elaborates[7] on the following scenario:

Suppose a business has several projects that will significantly add value once completed.
One particular project is estimated to generate $100,000 in revenue, but the cost to complete this project is also $100,000.

In this scenario, the project would generate a $0 return on investment (ROI), so it will be abandoned.

Now, imagine that ChatGPT boosts worker productivity by 200%, reducing the time required to complete the project.

After recalculating, the cost of the project decreases to $50,000. With this reduced cost, the project becomes a lucrative opportunity and is executed because of its positive ROI.

Additionally, the reduction in cost means more personnel can be assigned to handle a greater number of projects in an effort to gain more revenue.

Small businesses can leverage AI to compete with larger enterprises by utilizing tools that were previously unaffordable or inaccessible. This leveling of the playing field encourages entrepreneurship and can lead to a more rich and competitive market landscape.

The impact of AI-driven productivity gains and new business ventures are expected to contribute to overall economic growth, potentially leading to more wealth and improved standards of living for everyone.

Summary

While the rapid advancement of Generative AI presents a danger, it also has significant opportunities.

AI will cause some jobs to vanish, but data from reputable sources so far shows that the ones who benefit the most from Generative AI are professionals with advanced skill sets.

New jobs that didn’t exist in the past would also emerge as the change in society requires new roles, particularly those relevant to an AI-integrated world.

We can’t predict the future with certainty, but it’s far more productive to learn how Generative AI works, integrate it into our routines, and discover ways to create wealth with this technology rather than worrying about whether it will bring widespread unemployment and economic instability.
