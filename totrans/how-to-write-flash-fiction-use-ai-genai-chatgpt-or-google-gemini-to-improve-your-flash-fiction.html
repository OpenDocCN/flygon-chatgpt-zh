500 words on Finding the Spark

If you are reading this book, you probably have already written at least one pieces of flash fiction. One question you will often be asked is: Where do you get your ideas?

Okay well, here are my secrets:

The Inspiration Walk:

I highly recommend walking outdoors. Whether accompanied by a pet or solo, aim for a 5-loop through your local area; if possible, include a park or green space.

For me, a 5-kilometer walk takes me about 40 minutes and unfolds in three phases. The first 10 minutes might be cluttered with daily life — work, family, etc. As I proceed, I let my mind wander to my book and focus on my characters, either reviewing existing copy or planning something new.

I know an author should always carry a notebook, but I tend to use my phone to type myself reminders or to dictate scenes and dialogues as they are fresh in my mind. Honestly, it’s not in the least bit weird to pause in your walk and spend a few moments speaking to your phone.

Bonus: if you set a good pace, you can burn 125 to 200 calories during a 5-kilometer walk.

Photographic Inspiration:

I also use my phone to take photos, and I’m a big fan of cutting memorable photos and headlines out of newspapers.

Make it a routine to capture anything that catches your eye, be it unusual street art or striking patterns.

A single photo might just inspire the title or the first line of your next story.

Creative Consumption:

Dive deep into other’s creative works. I love sci-fi and fantasy on TV and at the movies, and I make no apologies for watching everything. Having said that, if I don’t like it or I think the plot is not working, I don’t necessarily watch everything to the end.

I am also inspired by ballet dancers, gymnasts and skaters. The beauty and self-discipline these artists exhibit always force me back to the blank page or the computer screen and inspire me to practice, improve and excel just as they do.

Live performance of any kind has its own energy that can act like lightening to a writer, so head down to your nearest pub or club and sip a beer while your local band performs. It will be great for your writing.

Bonus: you have something other than writing to talk to your friends about.

Phone Apps I recommend:

Notes or Notebook

Generally, a free app, this allows you to quickly type down a few lines. You can then either email to yourself and cut and paste into a new or existing fiction.

Rev

This a dictation app where you pay for a transcript to be emailed you. There are two price plans AI generated text, and human transcription. Texts up to 5,000 words will be returned to you by email with an hour of being dictated.

One top tip is to dictate short sequences up to 5-10 minutes long, and the texts come back within 15 minutes, longer texts can take several hours to be processed.

250 words on The Doing

Making Time to Write:

  • Treat writing as a non-negotiable part of your day. Tell everyone you know.

  • Find your time, block it out on your calendar, and/or set an alarm on your phone.

  • Claim your space—designate a specific chair and table as ‘your writing space ‘, and don’t use it for anything else.

  • Start writing every day. Even if it’s just for 15 minutes. Think of it as a daily ritual, crucial to your creative soul.

Believing Your Writing Is Important:

  • Never underestimate the value of your voice.

  • Your writing is a unique lens through which the world can be viewed.

  • Each word you write adds to the tapestry of human expression.

  • Remember, every great writer was once where you are now.

  • Ultimately, the only person who has to enjoy your work is you.

Choose your weapons:

Whether you’re a fan of the traditional pen and paper or more tech-inclined, the medium is secondary to the message.

I work on a laptop all day, so when I want to write in the evening, I give myself permission to write longhand in a notebook and use a transcription service to dictate and capture my handwritten notes into computer-based text.

Sometimes I write directly onto my laptop, but it does help if I switch off the internet. The MS Word ‘Focus’ option or other ‘do not interrupt’ functions are available on most software. Investigate how you can avoid idling on YouTube and Google News when you know you should be writing. And just do it.

250 words on Other Options:

Writing Jellies:

These can be organised with a few writing buddies or something your local writer’s critique group might consider. It involves hiring a small hall for the day, preferably one with a small kitchen. Setting up the hall with tables and chairs and inviting everyone to ‘come and spend the day writing’. There are three rules to making your jelly a success:

  • Be strict about the chatter. If you want to catch up, go and shut yourself in the kitchen.

  • Organize a pot-luck lunch; suggest everyone bring a plate to share; you will find you have an amazing buffet with food to spare.

  • Bring many extra multi-plugs and extension cables, so everyone has the chance to power their laptop.

Cafes or Libraries

Sometimes what your writing needs is a new location. If there’s a large Starbucks where they won’t notice if you linger all day in the corner, or if your local library has workstations or tables. These are perfect options for leaving your normal writing chair and finding a new energy or source of inspiration.

Writing Retreats:

Google for centres across the US and UK. It can be expensive but very indulgent and fun to do. Make it your main holiday and enjoy.

Keep writing, keep believing, and let your stories soar.

Over and out.

500 words on Editing Flash Fiction

Okay, let’s go over the basics. Here is a checklist of things to consider when editing your flash fiction.

Do you start with a strong opening?

The opening sentence is one of the most critical parts of your flash fiction story. It needs to grab the reader’s attention and make them want to keep reading. A strong opening can set the tone for the entire story, and it can help you stand out from the competition.

Try to avoid using clichéd openings like “It was a dark and stormy night” or “Once upon a time.”

Instead, try to come up with something unique and unexpected that will pique the reader’s curiosity.

For example, “The blood was the first thing she noticed.”

Does every word count?

In flash fiction, every word counts. You have a limited word count, so every word needs to serve a purpose. Use strong verbs, vivid descriptions, and sensory details to make your story come alive.

Avoid using unnecessary adjectives and adverbs.

Instead, show the reader what’s happening through action and dialogue. For example, instead of saying, “She was very sad,” show the reader how she feels through her actions and dialogue.

I always find that in my first draft, my flash fiction might be 700–1000 words long, and I personally love to set the challenge of honing an overlong text down to a 500-word count.

If you have written something that is 700 words long, sharpen your red pencil; that is pretty much the perfect first draft for a 500-word flash fiction.

Create Compelling Characters

Even with a limited word count, it’s important to create compelling characters that the reader can relate to and care about. You don’t need to give them a detailed backstory, but you should give them unique personalities and motivations.

Try to avoid using clichéd character types like the brooding bad boy or the perfect girl next door. Instead, try to create characters that are more complex and nuanced. For example, a brooding bad boy who volunteers at a soup kitchen on the weekends or a perfect girl next door who secretly struggles with anxiety.

Is your Dialogue quotable?

Dialogue or conversation, can be a very good way to convey a lot of information and move a story forward. There are quite a few pits falls you need to be aware of:

  • Beware of realistic dialogue; most people speak with lots of hums, hahs, and repetitions accompanied by looks and hand waves? This is almost impossible to recreate on the page.

  • Beware of common sayings and cliché hackneyed phrases, and if you are wondering what I mean, anything that has been a punchline in a movie or a lyric in a well-known song is, to me, very suspect. I recommend you cut it.

  • Dialogue is the best tool you have for revealing character. For example:

Child: I know I’m going to win

Mum: Really, who told you that?

The child is hopeful, her head full of stars; the mum is critical—too critical or maybe she has a more realistic assessment of her chances.

Beware of too much dialogue

In flash fiction, you cannot have overlong conversations. Occasionally, writers write 500 words of just dialogue. This rarely works. It might read well, and it might give some good twists and turns, but this will never win against flash fiction with narrative backdrops, charismatic characters, and punchy dialogue.

500 words on Polishing your Flash Fiction

Edit, Edit, Edit

Editing is crucial in flash fiction. Beyond the limited word count, the fiction needs to sing.

Here are some ideas for editing your document:

  • Read your story out loud to see how it sounds; this will tell you a lot about the story arc, overlong descriptions, or unrealistic dialogue.

  • Use software to check for typos and grammatical errors.

A few notes on feedback

Though a word of warning, here are a list of people who will never read your writing:

  • Your husband, wife or life partner

  • Your kids—I mean, you’re kidding, right? Children are always disdainful of their parents’ pursuits.

  • The nice author and editor you met at a writer’s conference last summer. Believe me, they get bombarded with people asking them to read their work, and they are also busy enough with their own work not to have time for yours.

Here is a list of people who can probably NOT help:

  • Your best friend

  • The nice lady from your church or office who never says ‘no’

  • Your mother may read your work, but I can guarantee her responses will not be helpful.

So where should you go for feedback?

Here are my top tips.

  • Take your work to a workshop where you are encouraged to bring writing and to get feedback.

  • Join a writer’s group locally or set one up. The local library can normally help.

  • On Fiver.com, you will find lots of experienced editors and writers who will give you feedback for $5 or less. I would recommend asking for 3 or 4, and while $20 is a lot of money, you will have three to four nice reports, and unlike one person’s opinion, you can look at several.

Research the Competition

Before you submit your flash fiction story to a competition, research the competition. Read previous winners to get a sense of what the judges are looking for. Pay attention to the style, tone, and themes of the winning entries, analyse the stories, and determine what makes them work.

Each year, the Farnham Flash Fiction Competition publishes its longlisted winners in a booklet. This is a very good place to see the standard and variety of entries that make their way to a competition.

Submit multiple entries.

If the rules allow it, submit multiple entries. This increases your chances of winning as well as giving you an opportunity to showcase different writing styles or concepts.

Don’t give up.

Winning a flash fiction competition can be demanding, but don’t give up. Keep writing and submitting, and don’t be discouraged. Every competition is an opportunity to improve and hone your craft.

Good luck!

A brief history of ChatGPT

ChatGPT is an AI chatbot utilizing natural language processing for human-like dialogue creation, capable of generating text, images, or videos in response to user prompts.

  • It simulates intelligence and self-awareness by drawing from a vast library of content, including books, articles, and reference texts, allowing it to respond accurately to a wide range of topics.

  • The analogy of pre-agreed answers highlights ChatGPT’s ability to deduce correct responses without actual understanding.

Since its public release, ChatGPT has evolved into a versatile tool for writing assistance, offering help from idea generation to draft composition and improvement, thereby aiding in overcoming writer’s block and boosting creativity.

June 2020: Launch of GPT-3, primarily for researchers and developers, showcasing a significant advancement in language model capabilities and hinting at the potential for AI in creative and conversational applications.

November 30, 2022: OpenAI releases ChatGPT to the public, fine-tuned for interactive and conversational use, marking its debut as an AI capable of generating human-like responses in chat format.

Specialist versions are now Available

At the start of 2023, Open AI released a number of specialist versions of ChatGPT, which were available as subscriptions. This included.

ChatGPT for Creative Writers

This version is trained to understand nuances in storytelling, plot development, and character creation, making it an invaluable tool for authors and content creators seeking to add depth and intricacy to their narratives.

ChatGPT for Business and Marketing

This is aimed at enhancing communication, marketing, and branding efforts. These iterations helped professionals craft pitches, write marketing copy, and develop strategic content that aligns with business goals.

Understanding Open Source

Because the technology behind ChatGPT is open source, OpenAI AI declared itself committed to sharing its findings, many different companies have now created different versions of ChatGPT. It is worth remembering that at source, they are all the same system but with different overlays or more specific functionality.

Looking to the future, the potential for further specialized iterations of ChatGPT is vast, but this book aims to help you understand the basic/initial tool and in doing so, you will also have a better understanding of the others.

400 words: And the Future?

Picture this: your own AI writing coach, one who knows your work intimately. It spots those pesky adverbs you overuse, or the moments when a character suddenly sounds out of place. But it’s more than an editor – this AI brainstorms ideas with you, tossing out plot twists like confetti as you struggle with writer’s block. Will it replace the writer? Never that. It will make the act of writing feel less lonely.

AI, for all its cleverness, isn’t perfect. It can struggle with the messy bits, the emotional nuances that make a story truly sing. But where it shines? Spotting typos and overused phrases before you do. Imagine an AI that knows the delicate line between inspiration and offensive tropes. A watchful eye that flags outdated language and points you towards better options. Not to censor, but to make your work resonate with even more readers.

Think of AIs that become the masters of different worlds. One writes thrillers that keep you guessing until the last page, another spins historical romances with impeccable research. You’d have a team of expert assistants, each a click away.

Sure, there are kinks to iron out. But what if AI could take care of the tedious tasks, like grammar checks and research legwork? What if it could brainstorm with you, offering fresh ideas when you’re stuck? The future starts now, and I, for one, am excited to see where this collaboration between writer and machine takes us.

Acknowledging AI strengths and weaknesses:

The Good Stuff:

  • Imagine never seeing another typo. That’s the realm of the AI – spotting those tiny errors, saving you from blushing later.

  • Need a fresh spin on a sentence? An AI can offer endless variations, some silly, some surprisingly good. No more staring at a blank page.

  • Facts are at your fingertips. An AI assistant can dig up research in a tenth of the time you’d spend on it.

The Not-So-Good Stuff:

  • That spark of an original idea, the human messiness that makes a good story? That’s still on us. AI can mimic, not innovate.

  • The whispered joke, the unspoken heartache… these are hard for an AI to understand. Expect it to miss the nuances of your writing.

  • Should a machine get credit for a story? It’s a question for the future, but we need to keep asking it, as AI becomes more powerful.

Remember: AI is just a tool, like Word or Grammar checker - it helps you write, yes faster and better, but you still have to do the work.

250 words: AI software that is useful to writers.

Let’s be honest, the world of AI writing tools is a bit of a wild jungle at the moment. New ones pop up like mushrooms after the rain, promising to solve all your writerly woes. But before you get overwhelmed, here’s a simple starting point:

The Heavy Hitters:

These are the big names most writers have at least heard of:

ChatGPT (OpenAI): A powerful conversational AI. Think of it as your brainstorming buddy, good for generating ideas, plot twists, and even whole passages of text (with caveats, of course!).

Gemini (Google): Designed specifically for creative writers. Gemini offers help with plot development, character arcs, and even analyzing your writing style to provide personalized feedback.

Accessing the Tools

Most of these have free versions that you can access directly through their websites. Just search for them, create an account, and you’re ready to start experimenting.

Important Note: Free versions usually have limits. You might only be able to generate a certain amount of text or use some features a limited number of times.

Paid Upgrades… Are they worth it?

This depends on how much you’ll use the AI tools. If you’re just dipping your toes in, stick with it for now.

If you find yourself relying on AI frequently, paid versions offer faster responses, more features, and generally higher-quality output.

Checklist: Before You Hit “Generate”

What’s Your Goal?

Define what you need help with—ideas, editing, research? This helps you pick the right tool. Remember, It’s a Tool, Not a Magic Wand. AI output needs editing and polishing, always.

Experiment!

Play around, feed the AI odd prompts, and see what it spits out. This is how you learn what works and what doesn’t.

Privacy Matters:

Read the terms and conditions of each tool. Know what data is being collected and how it’s used.

Be inspired, not intimidated.

Even the most sophisticated AI can’t replace the human spark in a story. That’s still on us!

One last tip:

The AI writing world changes fast. Blogs and online communities focused on these tools are great for keeping up to date and getting new ideas.

500 words: My first foray into creating AI content.

OK, this is where the shouting starts. I was quite surprised when my daughter told me she would never use AI either for content or for imagery. And you may have read many articles that predicted the end of all creative writing as machines finally replaced humans.

I have always been curious about technology, and I tried ChatGPT as one of the first million users. I was astonished at how good it was at some tasks that previously would have taken hours to do.

One of the first things I asked ChatGPT to do was write a 500-word blog that reviewed the 1976 classic film Star Wars.

Write a 500-word review praising George Lucas’s 1976 film Star Wars, aimed at a YA audience of 14–18 year-olds.

Here is the AI-generated film review.

In the world of cinematic legends, George Lucas’s 1976 masterpiece, Star Wars, stands as a colossal beacon of imagination, adventure, and innovation. For young adults aged 14–18, this film isn’t just a movie; it’s a portal to a universe brimming with endless possibilities, where the line between the real and the extraordinary blurs into an adventure of a lifetime.

*Star Wars*, or more precisely, *Star Wars: Episode IV: A New Hope*, introduces us to a galaxy torn apart by conflict, where the oppressive Galactic Empire reigns with an iron fist and the Rebel Alliance fights valiantly for freedom.   1. It shows you stylistic differences between different writers that are sometimes difficult to put a finger on.

  3. It can give you some very unusual ideas and point you new directions.

  1. I am not suggesting you use this to write the next Tom Clancy bestseller.

  3. You stop thinking and rely on the machine.

500 words on AI Prompts for writing.

Talking to a machine about writing might feel strange at first. Words like ‘prompt’ and ‘query’ have a clinical edge, far from the messy daydreams that usually fuel a story. But think of an AI writing tool as… well, a tool. A hammer doesn’t build the house on its own, right? It’s all about having the right tool for the job and knowing how to use it.

The trick with AI prompts is to make them a little bit surprising. Too vague, and you’ll get generic results. Too specific, and it’ll choke the machine’s ability to offer those unexpected sparks of inspiration. It’s a balancing act – a playful one.

Top 10 AI Prompts for Creative Writers

“Rewrite this scene, but set it underwater.” Take an existing piece of writing and force an absurd shift. The results may be unusable, but they’ll jar your thinking loose from its usual patterns.

“A love story between a robot and a potted plant.” Challenge the machine to find the human in the non-human. Quirky pairings can lead to unexpectedly rich themes.

“Write a 500-word story where the final sentence is the same as the first.” Adding constraints can spark creativity. This one forces you to think about structure from the very beginning.

“A dialogue between a time traveler and someone who doesn’t believe in time travel.” Built-in conflict! Perfect for working on sharp dialogue and testing your characters’ core beliefs.

“Describe a smell so vividly that I can almost taste it.” This grounds the AI in the sensory world, away from abstract ideas. Great for practicing evocative writing.

“Give me three alternate titles for my story, each changing the genre entirely.” Does your murder mystery have a rom-com title hiding within? This forces you to look at your work with fresh eyes.

“Write a haiku from the perspective of my cat/dog/houseplant.” Shifting viewpoint is a writer’s superpower. An AI can help you practice this skill in a playful, low-stakes way.

“Create a villain who is secretly motivated by profound kindness.” Contradiction makes for compelling characters. See what the AI throws back at you, it might be surprisingly insightful.

“What would happen if [mundane object] became magical?” A paperclip, a lost sock… infusing the everyday with wonder is a classic storytelling trick. An AI might offer surprising twists.

“I’m stuck. Just give me the first line of a story, any story.” Sometimes, all you need is a kickstart. Let the AI give you that initial push and see where it goes.

Remember: The best AI prompt is the one that makes YOU want to write. Get ready to be surprised!

ChatGPT can dramatically speed up the writing process. The software can generate first drafts and suggest revisions, AI tools can also assist in proof-reading, copy editing and reviewing your texts.

250 words on how to recognise AI generated fiction.

As we have already seen, recognizing AI-generated fiction, including flash fiction, involves looking for certain telltale signs that differentiate it from human stories. While AI can create compelling narratives, there are subtle clues in the text that hint at its non-human origin.

Watch out for:

Lack of Emotional Depth

AI-generated fiction often lacks the emotional depth within human experience that comes naturally to human writers. Characters may lack complexity or undergo developments that feel forced or unnatural. Emotional scenes might not hit the mark due to the AI’s inability to fully grasp and convey the subtleties of human emotions.

Inconsistent Character Voice

In flash fiction, where every word counts, character voice is paramount. AI might struggle to maintain a consistent and unique voice for each character, especially over the span of a short story. Human authors, conversely, imbue their characters with distinct voices reflective of their backgrounds, personalities, and experiences, something AI-generated texts might fail to capture convincingly.

Odd or Out-of-Place Details

AI models can sometimes introduce details that, while factually accurate, feel oddly placed or irrelevant to the story’s flow. These missteps in narrative coherence can disrupt the immersive quality of fiction, revealing the non-human hand behind the work.

Surface-Level Interactions and Dialogues

The interactions and dialogues in AI-generated fiction tend to be more surface-level, lacking the subtlety and depth that come from genuine human interaction. Dialogue may feel stilted or overly formal, missing the idiosyncrasies and imperfections that characterize real human speech.

As a writer, you probably also read more than most. Train yourself to recognise a human vs. an AI voice. Learn to appreciate the human touch of original writing. Keep your wits about you and be critical of what you read, and you can be sure the value of original human creativity will continue undiminished.

Puttenham survived the great zombie apocalypse. Thanks to the bells.
