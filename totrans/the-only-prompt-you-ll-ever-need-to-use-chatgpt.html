Chapter 1
The Most Common Mistake: Asking As If You’re Talking to a Human

This is a mistake almost everyone makes in the beginning: treating ChatGPT as if there’s just another person on the other side of the screen. I can already picture the scene: you open a new chat, type in a vague, loose question, and expect the AI to guess what you really want. Something like: "give me a business idea" or "write a text about productivity" . Then, when the answer comes, you frown and think, “well, that was a load of 💩 .” You’re left with the impression that the AI is shallow, generic, or even useless. But the issue isn’t the tool — it’s how the request was made.

In fact, this is the main reason I hear when someone says things like, “This GPT thing isn’t impressive,” or “I tried it, and it didn’t work — I didn’t get anything useful,” or even “I don’t get what the hype is all about; I asked something and got a totally empty, pointless answer.” That frustration, in almost all cases, comes from a poorly structured prompt — unclear, without direction. And that’s completely understandable — no one ever taught us how to talk to an AI.

Asking something from ChatGPT without context, direction, or structure is like calling up an architect and saying, “Build something for me.” They might try, but you’ve left out everything: the style of the house, number of bedrooms, the size of the lot, the budget, the intended use. The result? The project turns out wrong — or at the very least, completely different from what you wanted. It's the same with AI.

Of course, it’s absolutely possible — and even expected — that you’ll correct ChatGPT as the conversation goes on. You can adjust the course, give feedback, clarify what you meant, or even change your entire line of thought. That’s part of the natural dynamic of using the tool. But there’s an inconvenient truth here: when someone starts a chat vaguely, it’s usually because they don’t realize that they are the one who needs to take control of the conversation. The mistake isn’t just technical — it’s psychological. The person doesn’t realize that in this kind of interaction, they’re not just a passive participant, but the one in the driver’s seat. We, the users of AI, are the active agents. If we stay passive, hoping the AI will magically guess our intentions, the results will always be mediocre or frustrating. The clarity of the request is the responsibility of the one writing it — not the machine’s . This is a new kind of digital literacy: learning to express yourself with precision in order to get what you want.

And it’s interesting to realize that this behavior — this difficulty in being clear, direct, and objective — doesn’t come out of nowhere. We can trace its roots back to our earliest years in school, during the literacy stage. We were taught — or rather, trained — to act passively. The teacher was the ultimate authority figure, and the student, most of the time, was expected to just "stay quiet," listen, and repeat. Questioning wasn’t encouraged — sometimes it was even punished (I still have uncomfortable memories of moments when I tried to question what was being taught in class and was quickly shut down, as if simply asking were a challenge to the teacher’s authority). The result is an entire generation of people who struggle to express clearly what they want, what they think, and what they need. They don’t know how (or that they can) ask good questions. And when they encounter an AI — which depends precisely on that kind of direction — they end up frustrated. Not because the tool is limited, but because our communication has been limited from an early age. And now, using this technology, that reflex becomes even more visible.

Artificial intelligence needs clues. The more context it has, the more accurate, useful, and creative the response will be. That doesn’t mean writing an essay every time — it means thinking clearly before you ask. What do you want? For whom? With what tone? In what format? What outcome are you hoping for with this response? These are the questions that shape a good prompt. And this is exactly where most people get lost .

Before asking anything from ChatGPT, you need to have at least a basic idea of what you’d like to receive. That might sound simple, but it’s one of the biggest obstacles for those just starting out. I can already hear you saying: “But how am I supposed to be clear about what I want if I don’t even know what I want yet?” And that’s a totally valid question. The truth is, we often only figure out what we want during the conversation, as we explore. But even in that discovery process, you still need to start with some kind of intention — even if it’s vague, even if it’s just a rough starting point. “I want to understand a topic better,” “I want to test an idea,” “I need help organizing a text.” That’s already something.

You don’t need to have all the answers (if you did, you probably wouldn’t be using an AI in the first place ) — but you do need a purposeful question. ChatGPT can help you explore, but it needs to know which direction to take. Without that, it just spins in circles. Don’t worry: in the next chapter, we’ll dive into how you can gain clarity through the conversation itself. For now, the most important thing to understand is that your part starts before you type anything — it starts with an intention, a direction, however small or vague.

In the chapters ahead, you’ll see how tiny adjustments make all the difference. A vague request produces a generic answer. A specific, well-structured request results in something that feels tailor-made. That’s what we’re aiming for. And the good news? You’re about to learn exactly how to do it.

Chapter 2
What a Prompt Really Is (and Why It's More Than Just a Question)

Let’s start with a simple statement: a prompt is not just a question. It’s much more than that. A real prompt is a combination of clear, well-directed instructions that tell the AI exactly what it needs to do, how to do it, for whom, with what tone, and in what format. It’s like explaining in detail what you need to someone who’s going to help you create something. The more information you provide — like the target audience, desired style, final goal — the better the result.

Imagine you hired a designer to create the cover of your book. You wouldn’t just say, “Make me a cover.” You’d tell them the book’s genre, target audience, the kind of feeling you want to convey (mystery? lightness? professionalism?), preferred colors, font style, required elements. All of that helps the designer deliver something that truly meets your expectations. Likewise, you don’t just tell an architect, “Draw me a project.” You sit down, explain what you need, talk about the kind of building, the lot size, how many rooms, the style you like, your budget, whether it’s for living or renting, whether you want something modern or traditional. You give context. You provide direction. Only then can the architect create a plan aligned with your needs and expectations. With ChatGPT, the logic is the same: the more elements you provide, the better the final result .

If you simply tell GPT, “Write a text about marketing,” the result will likely be generic and not very useful. But if you say: “Write a blog post of up to 500 words, in a conversational tone, aimed at beginner entrepreneurs who want to understand the basics of digital marketing, using simple language and including practical examples,” the outcome is completely different. The result will be much closer to what you actually need — because the AI was given a clear map to follow.

This chapter is the beginning of a shift. Here, you’ll start to realize that when it comes to AI, clarity is power. And the prompt is your most important tool. In the next chapters, we’re going to build the only prompt you’ll ever need to start more effective conversations — and show you how to tweak it for almost any situation. BUT before we do that, we need to go through a stage I call Preparation.

Preparation

Before you learn how to structure a good prompt, there’s one fundamental truth you need to understand: AI won’t do the work for you. What it will do is help you do better at what you’re already trying to do — or what you already know how to do and want to improve. And this all starts with an essential (and maybe even uncomfortable) question: Do you know exactly what you want ChatGPT to give you?

A lot of people freeze up here. But Ismar, if I already knew what I wanted, why would I need help from AI? The answer is simple: ChatGPT is not the main character in this story — you are. It’s not the author, the scriptwriter, or the mastermind. YOU are the brain. ChatGPT is just an insight machine. A source of ideas. It helps you think, organize, create, revise, simulate, and propose paths — but only after you point the way.

Have you ever seen the movie The Intern ?

The Intern is a comedy in which Robert De Niro plays a 70-year-old widower who decides to return to the job market as a senior intern at a fashion startup led by a young entrepreneur. Over time, he earns everyone’s trust through his calm demeanor, attention to detail, and life experience. He shows that it’s possible to combine knowledge and a willingness to help with new ideas and technologies. His supportive attitude and attentiveness make him indispensable — exactly the kind of role AI can play in your daily routine, if you know how to guide it.

AI is like the character played by Robert De Niro. A dedicated, attentive partner, full of skills and ready to contribute — but it waits for you to tell it where to go. You are still in control. That mindset is what separates those who achieve incredible results with AI from those who complain it “doesn’t work.”

And here’s an important distinction that many people still haven’t understood: the difference between content generated by AI and content assisted by AI. Automatically generated content, without soul, without filter, without purpose… that’s the kind of stuff clogging social media with repetitive, empty junk no one reads to the end. Think weird videos showing bizarre transformations — like jars of mayonnaise swallowing cars or birds playing violins.

That’s what we’re calling digital garbage. Empty entertainment that adds nothing to people’s lives. It only exists to generate comments, likes, and shares — interactions that feed the algorithm and bring in a few cents for the creator. Meanwhile, those who consume this kind of content are just reinforcing a cycle that rewards shallow sensationalism over real, applicable knowledge.

But there’s another kind of content — AI-assisted content — where the machine helps, but the human is the one thinking and directing. AI-assisted content is where artificial intelligence works as a strategic partner: it helps structure ideas, suggests improvements, expands your perspective, and saves time on operational tasks — but it doesn’t replace human reasoning.

That’s the kind of content helping people pass exams, land job promotions, and increase their business revenue. Often, you don’t even realize AI was involved — because it was used intelligently. And you know what these people have in common? They’ve learned how to use AI well.

They’re the ones Sam Altman, CEO of OpenAI, was talking about when he said: “AI won’t take your job, but someone who knows how to use it will.”

Alright, but what about preparation? How does it actually work ? Basically, the idea is to use the AI itself to figure out what you want to ask it. Sounds like a paradox? It’s not. It’s actually much simpler than it seems.

Imagine you’re sitting in front of ChatGPT, not knowing exactly where to begin, what to ask for, or how to ask it. Instead of typing something vague or random, you can use the AI as an exploration tool. You might say: “I have a rough idea about [your topic], but I’m not sure how to turn it into a clear request.” Or: “I want to improve my productivity, but I don’t know exactly where to start. What questions can you ask me to help clarify what I need?”

At that moment, the AI becomes an intelligent mirror. It asks you questions, proposes directions, organizes your thoughts, and helps you see more clearly what was previously tangled up in your head. This process is incredibly valuable — and few people use ChatGPT (or any AI) in this way.

It’s like having a personal consultant who helps you organize your thoughts before anything else. This preparation stage is often what determines whether you’ll have a powerful conversation with AI or just another shallow exchange. Using AI to gain clarity about what you want to ask is already, in itself, an exercise in critical and strategic thinking. And it’s also one of the biggest proofs that, in the end, you’re the one in control.

So if you just opened a blank chat and you’re feeling that familiar “I don’t know what to ask” or “I don’t know how to explain it” kind of block, here’s a simple generic prompt that can save you:

   ChatGPT, I’d like your help to [understand / explore / organize / clarify / structure / review / discover possibilities around] [topic or problem]. I’m still a bit lost on how to approach this, so please ask me some questions to help clarify my thinking, better understand my goal, and turn this into a clearer, more structured request. Please guide me through this conversation.

This kind of prompt works as a starting point for nearly any situation. It activates AI’s “consultant mode” and transforms the exchange into a collaborative process of discovery. And the best part: as you interact, you’ll realize how much easier it is to shape, guide, and refine what you want than you initially thought.

Here are some practical examples of how to use AI to help formulate your own request (use these when you’re unsure how to begin, what to ask, or how to ask it ):

   I’m trying to create an online course, but I’m not sure which topic would be most relevant for my audience. Can you ask me a few questions to help me clarify that choice?

   I have a vague idea for an eBook on personal finance, but I don’t know where to begin. Can you help me outline the first steps and identify my ideal target audience?

   I work at a law firm and want to use AI to optimize my workflow, but I’m not exactly sure which tasks I could delegate or how to organize that process. Can you ask questions to help me understand the firm’s needs and build a practical AI usage plan?

   I want to start creating content for Instagram, but I’m not sure what style fits me best. Can you show me a few different formats and help me figure out which one I’d feel more comfortable with?

   I’m studying for a public exam, but I’m not sure which field to pursue. Help me with questions that can guide me to choose the path that fits my profile best.

   I want to use AI to help me write more attractive ads for my real estate agency, but I’m not sure how to describe the type of property or audience I want to reach. Can you ask questions to help me clarify those points and draft a more effective ad template?

These examples show how AI can act as a thinking partner, pulling the thread until you reach what you truly want to build or solve.

And now that you understand what makes a good prompt, the role of AI as an assistant, and the power of preparation — it’s time to put everything into practice. Next, you’ll learn the full structure of the only prompt you’ll ever need to turn a vague idea into a productive conversation with AI. Get ready — this is where the gold begins.

 

 

 

Chapter3
The Only Prompt You’ll Ever Need to Use ChatGPT

We’ve reached the core of this book. If you’ve followed along so far, you’re now ready to discover the practical structure that can transform any conversation with ChatGPT. This is the foundation, the heart, the compass. It’s the only prompt you really need to master — and one that can be adapted to nearly any task.

The structure is simple, but extremely powerful:

 

You are [role]. Your goal is [task]. Do this for [audience], using a [tone of voice], while respecting [limitations]. Respond in [format ].

 

This structure works because it gives the AI everything it needs to do a good job: role, objective, audience, tone, constraints, and desired format. Think of it as a template — a starting point you can customize depending on what you need.

Think of this prompt structure like a pizza dough recipe: the base is always the same, but the toppings — or rather, the content — can vary in nearly infinite ways. You can go classic, experiment with bold combinations, or create something totally unexpected. Your imagination is the only limit. And the more you practice, the better you’ll get at mixing the right ingredients to reach the ideal flavor — or in this case, the perfect response from AI.

 

You are [role] – This is the first element of the prompt and it plays a fundamental role: it gives the AI an identity. By assigning a role — like “experienced copywriter,” “financial consultant,” or “university professor” — you’re telling the AI how it should think and behave in its response. Without this, answers tend to be generic and shallow, because the AI doesn’t know which point of view or specialty it’s supposed to represent.

Here are some example roles the AI could take on depending on your context:

·        Copywriter

·        Math teacher

·        Business consultant

·        Consumer law attorney

·        Graphic designer

·        Personal trainer

·        Clinical psychologist

·        Digital marketing specialist

·        Proofreader

·        Investigative journalist

·        Career coach

·        Senior Python developer

·        Screenwriter

·        Professional translator

·        Productivity mentor

 

The role you choose influences the tone, vocabulary, and approach the AI will adopt. The more specific you are here, the more tailored the response will be.

 

Your goal is [task] – This part of the prompt clearly states what you want the AI to deliver. It might be a text, a suggestion, a plan, a list of ideas, and so on. Defining the goal helps the AI understand the main action it should take. Without a well-defined goal, the response may get lost or fall short of your practical needs. Here are some example tasks you can plug into your prompt:

·        Write an article

·        Summarize a text

·        Create a study schedule

·        Generate content ideas

·        Review a paragraph

·        Create a posting calendar

·        Structure a business proposal

·        Simulate a job interview

·        Design a lesson plan

·        Build a comparison table

·        Develop a business plan

·        Identify weaknesses in a strategy

·        Create a travel checklist

·        Review contract clauses

·        Write an academic paper introduction

·        Generate survey questions

Or even help organize the steps of a personal or professional project.

 

Do this for [audience] – This element guides the language, tone, and even the examples the AI will use. Speaking to college students is very different from speaking to investors, and the AI needs to know who it’s addressing. When you omit the audience, the AI tends to take the most neutral — and often shallow — path. For example, you might ask the AI to speak to:

·        High school students trying to choose a career

·        Entrepreneurs starting a digital business

·        Healthcare professionals seeking to improve communication with patients

·        Executives who need clear and concise presentations

·        People who are completely new to the topic

·        Experienced investors looking for strategic insights

Each audience demands a different approach. The AI can even adopt the perspective of a mentor, a colleague, a technical expert, a patient teacher, or even a fictional character, depending on the situation. The more specific you are here, the more personalized and useful the response will be.

 

Use a [tone of voice] – Tone is the soul of communication. It can be inspiring, formal, provocative, fun, technical, friendly... This detail makes content feel more authentic and suitable for the context. If you don’t specify a tone, the AI typically replies in a neutral way — which can make the text feel bland or impersonal. Plus, you can even train the AI to sound exactly like you. That means, with the right commands and some time, you can get ChatGPT to speak with your tone of voice, your preferences, your unique writing style. That goes far beyond just using a generic tone — it’s true personalization. I’ve included a bonus chapter at the end of this book to show you exactly how to do that in practice.

Here are some tone of voice variations you can try when creating your prompt:

·        Formal : ideal for corporate settings, academic texts, or institutional communication.

·        Informal : works well for social media, personal emails, and casual messaging.

·        Inspiring : great for motivating, engaging, and prompting action — especially for personal development or leadership content.

·        Provocative : used to grab attention, challenge ideas, or encourage deep reflection.

·        Playful : light, humorous, and creative — perfect for entertaining and keeping the audience engaged.

·        Technical : direct, precise, and packed with specific terms — ideal for fields like engineering, law, or medicine.

·        Empathetic : sensitive, caring, and human — useful for mental health, customer support, or emotionally-driven topics.

·        Authoritative : assertive and confident — good for texts that require an expert stance or firm decision-making.

·        Persuasive : ideal for convincing, influencing, and driving action — widely used in sales, marketing, awareness campaigns, or persuasive writing.

Testar diferentes tons pode mudar completamente o impacto da mensagem. Um mesmo conteúdo, com tons diferentes, gera reações diferentes — e é aí que você começa a dominar a linguagem assistida por IA.

Testing different tones can completely change the impact of your message. The same content, presented in different tones, can generate entirely different reactions — and that’s when you begin to master AI-assisted communication.

 

Respecting [limitations] – Every creative task needs boundaries. That might be a word count, an estimated duration, or even a structural rule. These limits help the AI stay focused and avoid “rambling” in its responses. Think of limitations like train tracks: they keep the AI on course. Ignoring this part can result in overly long or off-topic answers. Here are some example limitations you can use:

·        Respond in no more than 300 words

·        Use short sentences and simple language

·        Avoid technical jargon

·        Limit to 3 paragraphs

·        The response should fit on a single presentation slide

·        Do not use English terms

·        Include no more than 5 items in the list

·        Focus only on solutions applicable in Brazil

·        Do not exceed 2 minutes of reading time

These limitations help the AI deliver something that fits your real-world context. It’s like giving the answer a clear frame, so it comes out ready to use — without wasting time on unnecessary edits.

 

Respond in [format] – Finally, the format is the frame of the delivery. You can ask for a list, a paragraph-style text, a script, a table, bullet points, and more. This saves you time because it already shapes the answer in the right way for your use. If you skip this part, you’ll likely have to edit it a lot afterward. Here are some possible formats you can request:

·        Plain text (like an article, paragraph, or continuous explanation)

·        Numbered list (for steps, tips, strategies)

·        Bulleted list (great for summaries or highlights)

·        Table (for comparisons, schedules, or organizing information)

·        Script (used for videos, podcasts, or presentations)

·        Outline format (for loose ideas, brainstorming, or initial organization)

·        Email format (with greeting, body, and signature)

·        Slide (short and impactful text, ideal for presentations)

·        Sales script (with simulated questions and answers)

·        Dialogue or conversation simulation (for training support or interview scenarios)

·        Opinion article (journalistic style, with arguments, thesis, and counterpoints)

·        News report (with lead, body, and closing structure)

·        Chronicle (a light, reflective text about everyday life)

·        Critical review (analysis of films, books, or events)

·        Poem (free verse, rhymed, or following a specific structure)

·        Short story (fictional narrative with beginning, middle, and end)

·        Personal letter (informal format, with greeting, body, and closing)

·        Speech (with persuasive tone, directed at a specific audience)

·        Theatrical script (with character dialogue, stage directions, and scene breakdowns)

·        Academic essay (with introduction, theoretical framework, analysis, and conclusion)

·        Checklist (items to mark for tasks or steps to follow)

·        Personal journal (with an intimate, reflective, and subjective tone)

·        Recipe (with ingredients, preparation method, and extra tips)

·        Action plan (organized steps with deadlines and responsibilities)

·        Legal argument (structure for petitions, legal opinions, or defenses)

·        Podcast script (with intro, main topics, transitions, and closing)

·        Step-by-step manual (practical guidance for task execution)

·        Descriptive flowchart (with sequentially narrated steps)

As you can see, the number of possible formats is huge — and what’s most interesting is that these are just a few examples. If I really tried to list them all, this chapter would turn into a book of its own. Choosing a good format speeds up the process and delivers a response that’s much closer to your real-world needs.

These six elements, when combined, form a solid foundation for any kind of request. They work like ingredients in a recipe: each one has a specific function, and when used together, the result is much more flavorful and on point.

Even though this structure includes six main elements, you don’t have to stick to them rigidly. Think of these blocks as a starting point — not fixed rules. Depending on the situation, you can adapt, skip, or even add new elements that make sense for your context. For example, you might include a section for visual references, specify trustworthy sources to consider, or request that the AI follow a particular logical structure, like problem-solution. What matters most is understanding that this structure was designed to give you freedom with direction — not to limit your creativity. Use it as a tool that evolves alongside your process.

Let’s see it in action.

 

Here are some practical examples to visualize how the prompt structure can be used:

   Create a business proposal: You are a business consultant with experience in B2B sales. Your goal is to write a business proposal for a potential client. Do this for mid-level technology company executives, using a professional, clear, and persuasive tone, with a one-page limit. Respond in a text-based proposal format divided into introduction, solution, differentiators, and call to action.

   Summarize an academic article: You are an academic reviewer. Your goal is to summarize a scientific article. Do this for undergraduate students, using a didactic and objective tone, with a 300-word limit. Respond in an informative summary format using accessible language.

   Plan a study routine: You are an educational coach. Your goal is to create a weekly study routine. Do this for someone who works full-time and has 2 hours available each night, using a motivational and realistic tone. Respond in a table format with days of the week, time, and activity.

   Create content for an awareness campaign: You are a social copywriter with experience in public campaigns. Your goal is to write a message for a mental health awareness campaign. Do this for high school teenagers, using an empathetic and direct tone, with a 150-word limit. Respond in a social media post format.

   Write a prospecting email: You are an experienced copywriter. Your goal is to write a prospecting email. Do this for small business owners in the food industry, using a friendly and professional tone, with a 200-word limit. Respond in ready-to-send email format.

   Create an Instagram post: You are a digital content creator. Your goal is to create a post for Instagram. Do this for young people just starting their entrepreneurial journey, using an inspiring tone and simple, informal language. Respond with a ready-to-publish caption.

   Plan a YouTube video: You are an experienced scriptwriter for educational videos. Your goal is to outline a script for a 5-minute video. Do this for law students, using a didactic and relaxed tone, following the structure: introduction, development with examples, and call to action at the end. Respond in a bullet-point script format.

   Get practical advice: You are a productivity consultant. Your goal is to help me better organize my mornings. Do this for someone who works from home, using a direct and encouraging tone, with a limit of 5 simple tips. Respond in a numbered list format .

   Generate questions for a podcast interview: You are a content producer specialized in interviews. Your goal is to generate creative questions for an interview with a non-fiction book author. Do this for an audience interested in personal development, using a curious and informal tone, with a limit of 10 questions. Respond in a numbered list format.

   Write a product description for an online store: You are an e-commerce copywriter focused on persuasion. Your goal is to write a product description for a self-care kit. Do this for women aged 25 to 40, using a light and welcoming tone, avoiding technical terms. Respond in a short paragraph format.

   Build a promotional plan for a local event: You are an event marketing specialist. Your goal is to create a promotional plan for a cultural fair happening in a middle-class neighborhood. Do this for local residents , using an upbeat and inviting tone, with a limit of 3 main promotion channels. Respond in a bullet-point plan format.

   Write a motivational message for a sales team: You are a team leader with strong communication skills. Your goal is to write a motivational message for your sales team facing challenging targets this month. Do this for experienced salespeople, using an inspiring and firm tone, with a 200-word limit. Respond in internal message format.

   Translate technical content into layman's terms: You are a technical communicator. Your goal is to translate a scientific explanation about vaccines for the general public . Do this for parents with young children, using an educational and accessible tone, avoiding scientific jargon. Respond in a continuous text format with up to 250 words.

As you can see, the prompt structure gives you control and clarity. It’s like placing a custom order instead of tossing out random words and hoping the AI guesses what you want. And the more you use this model, the more natural it becomes.

In the next chapters, we’ll explore ways to adapt and refine this structure — making you increasingly fluent in the art of AI communication.

 

Chapter 4
The Art of Refinement: How to Talk to AI and Improve Its Answers

If the prompt is the seed, then the conversation is the fertile soil where the idea grows and takes shape. Knowing how to write a good prompt is essential, but what turns an ordinary response into a brilliant one is your ability to keep the conversation going with the AI. Refining means engaging, adjusting, polishing.

Many people make the mistake of treating AI like a machine that gives instant, final answers. They ask a question, get a response, and if they don’t like it, they close the chat. But the real power lies in continuing. In saying things like: “That’s not quite what I meant,” “Can you simplify this?”, “Try again with more examples,” “Explain it like I’m 10 years old,” “Use more technical language,” “Now rewrite it with more emotion.”

These kinds of follow-up commands are like editing tools. They not only steer the AI in the right direction — they also teach it how to think better next time.

Let’s simulate a conversation to illustrate this idea:

 

 

 

 

 

Initial Request: Write me a text about habits.

Response: A generic text, briefly discussing healthy habits and the importance of discipline.

Refinement 1: Now rewrite this text for a young audience, with a relaxed tone and real-life examples.

Response: The text uses a lighter tone, includes examples like cell phones and social media, and adds a touch of humor.

Refinement 2: Trim it down to 200 words and end with a reflective question.

Adjusted Response: A more direct text, with accessible language, ending with: “What simple habit could you start today to improve your routine?”

Refinement 3: Now add a catchy title and a short subtitle that sums up the main idea.

Final Result :

Title: How About Changing a Habit Without the Drama?

Subtitle: Small actions that fit into your routine — and truly make a difference.

Text: A straightforward piece, well-positioned for the audience, ending with a prompt to interact — perfect for an Instagram post, for example.

Let’s look at another example:

Initial request: Write a paragraph about the benefits of reading.

AI’s response: Reading is an activity that brings countless benefits, such as increasing vocabulary, improving concentration, and stimulating creativity.

Refinement 1: Now rewrite this paragraph for children between 8 and 10 years old, using simple and playful language.

Response: Reading is like entering a magical world! When you read books, you learn new words, get smarter, and use your imagination like you're playing.

Refinement 2: Add a metaphor comparing reading to an adventure and keep the fun tone.

Response: Reading is like hopping on a rocket ship and traveling through worlds full of adventures! Each page brings new words, cool ideas, and amazing stories that make your imagination soar.

Refinement 3: Now turn this text into an introduction for a children's book that encourages reading, with a title and a question at the end.

Final result :

Title: Blast Off into Reading!

Text: Reading is like stepping into a spaceship filled with incredible stories. You can meet dragons, solve mysteries, or visit distant planets — all without leaving your seat! Each word is a step on this journey. Ready to start yours?

This example shows how adjusting the audience, tone, and intention of the content can completely transform the response. Refining isn’t about fixing a mistake — it’s about shaping, coloring, and giving purpose to a raw idea until it looks exactly how you imagined it (or even better).

⚠ ️ These examples were presented in a simplified way just to illustrate the process. In practice, your conversations with the AI — and your refinements — may (and likely will) involve much more than three or four interactions. In fact, the best responses and most refined results usually come after several exchanges, adjustments, and redirections. It’s common for a truly useful conversation to span dozens of interactions. That’s not a flaw — it’s a sign that you’re genuinely thinking alongside the AI, guiding the dialogue with clarity, intention, and depth.

 

Notice how interacting with AI can go beyond simply "improving" a response. You’re co-creating, leading with intention. It’s an iterative process — each refinement takes you closer to a version that’s more precise, more useful, and more aligned with what you want to communicate.

This is the art of refinement. It’s not about settling for the first response. It’s about guiding the AI toward the ideal result. And that requires an active role.

Here are some suggestions for commands and questions you can use to refine your conversations with AI. Think of them as shortcuts to help shape the response more accurately:

   Simplify this explanation

   Add real-life, relatable examples

   Use a more empathetic tone

   Rewrite using more technical language

   Turn this into a bullet-point list with short, clear topics

   Trim it down to a maximum of X words

   Include a summary at the end

   Swap the examples to better fit audience X

   Rewrite with more emotion and impact

   Add a clear call to action at the end

   Structure it using problem, cause, and solution

   Explain it as if I were 12 years old

   Translate it into casual, everyday language

   Include data or statistics to strengthen your point

   Rewrite to be clearer for someone unfamiliar with the topic

   Turn this into a script for a 3-minute video

   Adapt this text into a slide presentation format

   Create a professional email version of this content

   Use more emotionally engaging language

   Split this into 3 parts for a social media series

   Add rhetorical questions throughout to encourage reflection

   Create a condensed version to use as a social media caption

Using these refinements is like giving feedback to a teammate: the more specific and objective you are, the better the outcome will be in the next response.

Once again, it’s important to emphasize that these suggestions are just starting points. Each one can (and should) be adapted to your needs.

Chapter 5
How to Use AI to Think Better (Not Just Generate Answers)

In the previous chapters, we introduced "THE ONLY PROMPT" you need to start using ChatGPT. This model is ideal for initiating any conversation with clarity and direction, serving as a solid foundation for most interactions.

However, when using AI as a thinking partner , it can be helpful to experiment with more open-ended, reflective, or exploratory variations — ones that go beyond traditional formats and invite the AI to act as a thought-provoker, idea organizer, or even a mirror for your own thinking. Especially here, in the context of ideation and reflection, it’s worth loosening the structure a bit and making room for more fluid, curious, and creative dialogue.

Back in Chapter 2, we explored how ChatGPT can serve as a practical assistant to help organize thoughts and shape early-stage ideas. That chapter focused on preparation: using AI to clarify your goal, generate possibilities, and turn fuzzy thoughts into something more concrete. Now, we’re going to move beyond preparation. In this chapter, we’ll use AI as a true ally in reasoning, exploring ideas, and asking the right questions . In other words, if before AI helped you stay organized, now it helps you think better, challenge assumptions, and create clearer, more effective paths toward whatever you’re trying to build.

The most common path to developing any new skill — and yes, using AI intelligently is absolutely a skill — is to start from what we already know. We begin in familiar territory, build confidence, and little by little, start exploring more advanced and refined ways to apply that knowledge. In this chapter, that’s exactly what we’re going to do: we’re going to move beyond using AI as a simple answer machine, and take things up a level. We’re going to use AI to think with us, help us organize our ideas more clearly, and most importantly, help us uncover the right questions to guide our thinking.

A lot of people still view ChatGPT and other AI tools as little more than text generators. You type a question, copy the answer, and that’s it. But that’s just the surface. If you want to, AI can be so much more : it can become a tool to think better . And thinking better means seeing with more clarity, organizing ideas more logically, and exploring possibilities that weren’t even on your radar before.

Throughout this book, you’ve learned how to write clearer and more targeted prompts. Now, let’s take the next step: using AI as an extension of your thinking. As a thought partner. As a mirror. The real breakthrough happens when you stop using AI only to get answers — and start using it to discover better questions.

So now, I’m going to show you 4 practical ways to use ChatGPT as a tool to think more clearly, organize your ideas better, ask smarter questions, and ultimately, get responses that are far more useful and aligned with what you truly need.

.

1. Thinking Together: How to Use AI to Explore Ideas, Questions, and Directions

Let’s imagine you have a rough idea for a project, but you’re unsure where to begin. A good prompt might look like this:

   I have an idea for a project about financial education for teens, but it’s still all over the place. Help me organize this idea and explore possibilities. You can ask me questions to help clarify my thinking.

Here are some other ways you can ask AI to think through ideas with you:

   I’m considering writing a book about reading habits, but I’m not sure what angle to take. Help me explore different approaches and target audiences. Ask me questions that might point to the best direction.

   I want to restructure my business, but I’m not sure whether to keep my current focus or diversify. Help me explore pros, cons, and possible paths based on different scenarios.

   I’ve been thinking about launching a YouTube channel about artificial intelligence, but I don’t know where to start. Can you help me brainstorm initial ideas, video formats, and ways to position myself?

   I’m thinking of offering a new consulting service for legal professionals, but I need help structuring the offer. Help me outline the key points I should include.

With this type of prompt, the AI doesn’t just respond — it participates in the process. It asks questions. Suggests directions. Identifies gaps. The conversation becomes a kind of thought consultancy. This is where AI begins to act as a true clarity tool.

From there, you start the process of refinement and iteration . And it’s important to understand that iteration is not the same as interaction . Interaction is the exchange itself — a question and an answer.

Iteration, on the other hand, is the ongoing cycle of testing, adjusting, and improving. In other words, you don’t just talk to the AI once — you come back, revise, experiment with variations, compare outcomes, and gradually land on versions that are more refined and aligned with your needs. This iteration cycle is what turns a good idea into a polished one — ready to be implemented.

2. Counterpoint and Provocation: What If You’re Wrong?

Another incredibly powerful use is asking the AI to challenge your idea (this one’s my personal favorite). Something like:

   Here’s my idea: [describe it]. I want you to find weaknesses, counterarguments, or scenarios where this might fail.

Or even:

   Act as an expert who completely disagrees with my point of view. Show me opposing arguments in a respectful way.

   I believe remote work is the ideal model. Take the opposite stance and present strong, well-reasoned arguments against it.

   I’m planning to open a coffee shop aimed at young, tech-savvy customers. What risks or blind spots might I be missing?

   I want to launch an online course that’s 100% asynchronous. Challenge this plan by highlighting potential drawbacks and suggesting alternatives.

   I’m confident that my Instagram messaging is aligned with my audience. Question that certainty and point out what I might not be seeing.

   I’m working on a project that blends AI with traditional education. List potential criticisms and cultural barriers that could arise.

   I run an online store that sells natural products. Even though I get a good number of site visits, sales aren’t happening. List the main objections customers might have when buying in this niche — and suggest ways to overcome each one.

This kind of interaction opens up space for different perspectives, which sharpens your critical thinking. And best of all: you don’t feel judged or exposed like you might during a heated debate with another person.

3. AI as a Cognitive Mirror

The term may sound sophisticated, but the idea is simple: use AI as a mirror to see your own thoughts more clearly. When you explain something to ChatGPT, you’re actually forcing your own reasoning to take shape. When you ask it to summarize, rephrase, or improve your explanation, you’re not just polishing text — you’re refining your thinking. Your thinking. For example:

   I'm going to explain my idea the best way I can. Then, I want you to organize it into clear steps and point out anything that’s confusing. My idea is [describe your idea as clearly as you can].

   I want to write an article about leadership, but I’m not sure what approach to take. Can you help me find a unique angle or one that connects better with my audience?

   I'm planning a career change, but I’m still unsure about which direction to follow. Can you help identify possibilities based on my background and preferences?

   I’m thinking about launching a subscription service for book summaries, but I’m concerned about the competition. Can you highlight potential risks and suggest differentiators that could help me stand out in the market?

   I’m developing a course for beginners in personal finance. Can you help me understand the main challenges this audience faces and suggest a logical and engaging way to organize the topics?

   I’ve jotted down several video ideas for YouTube, but they feel disconnected. Can you help organize them by theme and suggest a cohesive editorial direction?

This kind of prompt turns AI into a mental facilitator — someone who helps you think more clearly, not because they have the perfect answers, but because they reflect back what’s already in your mind in a clearer, more structured way.

4. From a Vague Idea to an Action Plan

Now imagine you’re thinking something like: "I want to create a course about productivity, but I still don’t know the audience, the format, or even where to begin."

With the right prompt, that vague thought turns into a concrete plan: "Help me turn this vague idea into a step-by-step action plan. Guide me with questions and suggest options based on my answers."

Then the AI can :

·        Suggest target audiences based on your area of expertise

·        Propose viable formats (workshop, eBook, mentorship program)

·        Help define the schedule, modules, title, and promotional strategies

Another example:

   I have an idea for a talk about creativity at work, but I’m not sure how to make it connect with more technical professionals. Help me find an approach that makes the topic accessible and relevant to this audience.

   I want to create a digital product to help small business owners organize their finances, but I’m torn between using spreadsheets, video lessons, or a practical guide. Can you help me compare these options based on ease of use, perceived value, and sales potential?

In the end, you’ll walk away with a structured outline. Something that was fuzzy in your mind now becomes clear, actionable, and realistic.

AI doesn’t replace your thinking. It expands it.

That’s the key to making your use of AI truly strategic. When you get used to using artificial intelligence to organize ideas, test assumptions, challenge your thinking, and structure plans, you develop something far beyond a technical skill: you begin to think more deeply. It’s as if every conversation with AI becomes a mental workout, where you're encouraged to be more clear, more coherent, and more creative.

Over time, you become quicker at spotting vague ideas, more skilled at turning uncertainty into productive questions, and more confident when making decisions. AI doesn’t think for you — but it forces you to think better. And this continuous process, this guided and iterative practice, sharpens your mental clarity.

The more you train this kind of assisted reasoning, the more you develop the ability to think strategically even without AI. Artificial intelligence becomes both a mirror and a springboard. It helps you see with greater clarity — and leap further.

The more you use AI to think, the more you sharpen your own clarity. And clarity is the first step toward any smart decision.

Don’t just use AI as a generator of answers. Use it as a mirror that reflects your ideas back to you — more polished than when they went in.

Conclusion
 You don’t need to memorize a thousand formulas. You just need to understand how to give clear instructions

If you’ve made it this far, congratulations — not just for finishing the book, but for stepping into a new phase of your relationship with artificial intelligence. You now understand what a real prompt is, recognize the most common mistakes, know the essential structure for any request, and have learned how to refine a response until it’s exactly right.

And most importantly: you’ve realized that success with AI isn’t about memorizing commands, copying ready-made formulas, or following rigid rules. The secret lies in your intention and the clarity with which you lead the conversation. The more objective, specific, and creative you are, the better the response will be. AI doesn’t read minds — it responds to what you communicate.

You now have a map. A model you can apply, adapt, refine, and evolve. You don’t need to depend on endless tutorials or confusing videos anymore. You have autonomy.

Of course, practice will make all the difference. At first, you might miss the tone, overcomplicate your request, or forget to include an important detail. That’s okay. That’s how learning works. The more you use it, the easier it gets. The more you converse with AI, the more natural the process becomes. It learns from you — and you learn more about yourself while interacting with it.

Remember: AI is just a tool. What makes it powerful is your clarity in guiding it. You are the one in charge. The technology only follows the direction you provide.

So go ahead. Test it. Practice. Use the only prompt you need. And see what’s possible when good questions meet well-placed words.

🎁 Bonus Chapter
How to Train ChatGPT to Write Like You

As promised, here’s your bonus chapter 😉

Yes, you can train ChatGPT to write in a way that sounds like you. In fact, with a little practice and some well-crafted instructions, it can start adopting your writing style, tone of voice, and even your favorite quirks. Below are the steps that will help you make that happen.

1. Collect Your Own Samples

The first step is to build a mini-library of your personal writing style. Gather texts you've written before: emails, social media posts, book chapters, articles, presentations — even casual messages you’d send in a WhatsApp group. The more variety, the better. Choose examples you feel truly reflect your voice and the way you express yourself.

2. Ask for a Style Analysis

Paste these texts into ChatGPT and ask for an analysis. You can say something like: "Analyze the following texts and tell me the key characteristics of my writing style." It will likely identify patterns such as your preferred vocabulary, sentence rhythm, structural choices, use of metaphors, overall tone, and other subtle details you might not even notice yourself.

.

3. Create a Personal Style Guide

Based on what ChatGPT identifies (and what you also recognize), write a brief summary of your main writing style characteristics. Example: "I use short sentences , I like to give practical examples , I write in a friendly and conversational tone , I avoid overly formal words, and I like to end with a strong final sentence ."

4. Use That Guide in Your Prompts

For each new request, paste that guide at the beginning of your prompt. Something like: "Write this content using the following style : [your guide ]." This alone will significantly influence the tone and structure of the response.

5. Reinforce With Feedback

Tweak the responses that come back and tell ChatGPT what worked and what needs adjusting. For example: "I liked the tone , but it sounds too formal. Can you make it more relaxed ?" or "Add an example like I usually do in my writing." The AI learns from this type of ongoing conversation.

 

 

 

⚠ ️ ChatGPT has a memory feature (when enabled) that allows it to learn from your interactions over time. This means that as you engage in conversations, give feedback, and guide its responses, it gradually adapts to your style, preferences, and needs. This memory can be updated, adjusted, or even erased at any time — and it's one of the most powerful ways to make AI evolve alongside you. It's like training a personal assistant: the more you interact, the more in tune it becomes with your way of thinking and communicating.

 

 

6. Use Custom Instructions

In ChatGPT, you can set permanent custom instructions that tell it how you want it to respond every time. This is where you can paste your personal style guide and ask it to always write in that style — unless you specify otherwise. Here’s how to set it up:

1.    Go to the settings menu (click your name or profile icon in the upper right corner of ChatGPT).

2.    Click on "Custom Instructions" or “Customize ChatGPT.”

3.    In the field that says “What would you like ChatGPT to know about you to provide better responses?”, paste your personal style guide.

4.    In the field “How would you like ChatGPT to respond?”, write something like: “I want you to always write based on my style, keeping the tone [casual, direct, technical, creative, etc.], as described in the guide above.”

5.    Save the changes .

Accept That You’ll Still Need to Review

Even with all this, the text may not sound 100% like you — and that’s okay. Think of ChatGPT as a very eager intern: it does most of the work, but you still give it the final polish. We’ve learned how to do that throughout this book 😉
